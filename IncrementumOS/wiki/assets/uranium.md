# Uranium

Part of the **metals** theme — highest conviction tier. Currently no position (zeroed as of early 2026).

---

## Thesis

Uranium sits inside Eduardo's hard assets framework — the same macro worldview that drove gold, silver, and copper to large allocations. The underlying logic: since 2024, persistent fiscal stress in the US (debt/GDP ~120%, deficit above 6% of GDP, 30-year Treasuries near 5%), institutional credibility erosion at the Fed, and an increasingly multipolar geopolitical order have made hard assets the appropriate hedge against a weaker dollar and structurally higher inflation. Within that framework, uranium occupies a distinct tier: it is not just a monetary hedge but a fuel-cycle scarcity bet, sitting at the intersection of energy security and geopolitical concentration. [Incrementum-internal, 2025-10-12; 2025-12-15]

The structural deficit is Eduardo's primary claim: 175Mlbs of annual demand against 150Mlbs of primary production. [Incrementum-internal, 2025-10-12; 2025-12-15] That gap does not register clearly in spot price because ~85% of uranium is transacted via long-term contracts — the spot market is a thin sliver of actual trade, masking the underlying supply-demand imbalance. [Incrementum-internal, 2025-12-15; EIA 2023 data] Utilities currently have adequate near-term coverage from existing stockpiles, but their long-term contracts roll off in the 2026-27 window, triggering a wave of renegotiation that must reprice supply at levels consistent with the structural deficit — not current spot. [Incrementum-internal, 2025-10-12]

Layered on top: 40% of global uranium enrichment capacity remains in Russia. [Incrementum-internal, 2025-10-12; 2025-12-15] This is not a cyclical tail risk — it is a structural geopolitical overhang embedded in the supply chain of every Western utility that operates Russian-designed reactors or sources Russian enrichment services.

Uranium is not an energy-transition bet. It is a fuel-cycle scarcity bet: demand is inelastic (fuel cost is ~5% of nuclear power cost, no substitution possible), supply is geopolitically concentrated and structurally underfunded, and the reactor buildout wave is multi-jurisdictional and backed by US industrial policy, European energy security mandates, and China's domestic energy mix targets. [Pequeno-Mota, 2025-10-28; WILTW, 2025-07-24]

**Current posture:** Zeroed as of early 2026. Eduardo exited before Iran war escalation — demonstrating that macro/geopolitical tail risk overrules structural thesis integrity in the short run. The structural thesis is intact. Re-entry is pending; the question is when signal-to-noise justifies rebuilding the position.

## Drivers

### Driver 1: Structural supply deficit (Eduardo's frame)

Eduardo's claim: 175Mlbs annual demand vs. 150Mlbs primary production. [Incrementum-internal, 2025-10-12; 2025-12-15] The gap is structural, not cyclical, because:

- Uranium mine development takes 10-15 years from discovery to first production — a binding physical constraint on any supply response regardless of price. [Pequeno-Mota, 2025-10-28]
- No major new supply relief before 2028 at the earliest. NexGen's Arrow deposit is in final permitting (production not before 2030, delays could push to 2031-32). Denison's Wheeler River needs final permits; H1/2028 is the earliest start. [WILTW, 2025-07-24]
- Spot price does not reflect the deficit: ~85% of uranium traded via long-term contracts means spot is a thin market. [Incrementum-internal, 2025-12-15; EIA 2023 data] Supporting: Pequeno-Mota's incentive-price threshold of >$100/lb for new supply has not yet been sustainably reached, meaning the commodity market has not solved the problem. [Pequeno-Mota, 2025-10-28]
- Cameco is restricting output at McArthur River/Key Lake and Cigar Lake despite available capacity, prioritizing long-term contracts over spot. [WILTW, 2025-07-24]
- Inventory drawdown at utilities: utilities have bought below replacement rate for a decade, depleting buffers. Uncovered requirements accelerate toward the 2026-27 contract rollover window. [WILTW, 2025-07-24]

**Number reconciliation — 175 vs. 190-200Mlbs:** Eduardo's notes cite ~175Mlbs/yr current demand [Incrementum-internal, 2025-10-12; 2025-12-15]. WILTW's 190-200Mlbs/yr figure represents the *replacement rate* for the existing 440-reactor fleet — i.e., the ongoing demand needed to keep current reactors fueled at full utilization without drawing down inventories. [WILTW, 2025-07-24] The two numbers are consistent, not conflicting: Eduardo's 175Mlbs is current realized consumption, which runs below full-utilization replacement rate because secondary supply (existing inventory drawdown, downblending of enrichment tails) bridges the gap. The structural deficit operates on both frames — Eduardo's gap (175 demand vs. 150 produced = 25Mlbs annual shortfall met by secondary supply) and WILTW's gap (190-200 replacement rate vs. 150-160 produced = 30-50Mlbs shortfall) both describe the same physical reality. The operative mechanism: as secondary supply depletes, the realized consumption figure must converge toward the replacement rate, widening the shortfall that primary production must fill. The thesis is robust to either framing.

**Testable near-term hypothesis:** Pequeno-Mota's 2025-10-28 view — "uranium fuel buyer paralysis likely to end in 2025" — is the single near-term-falsifiable claim on this page. Year-end 2025 contracting actuals (TradeTech, UxC) confirm or reject it. Status as of 2026-04-30: data not in source materials — this is the single most important factual gap. [WEB-SCREEN]

Supporting [Pequeno-Mota, 2025-10-28]: WNA Reference Scenario — nuclear capacity 686 GWe by 2040 (current: 372 GWe), consuming ~390Mlbs U3O8, implying ~120% demand growth to 2040. Upper scenario: 966 GWe / 530Mlbs. This is an external projection; Eduardo's structural deficit framing stands independently of WNA scenario realization.

### Driver 2: Reactor buildout (Eduardo's frame)

Eduardo's claim: 440 operational reactors in 50 countries, 60 more under construction. [Incrementum-internal, 2025-10-12; 2025-12-15] This is the installed-base demand anchor — existing reactors require continuous fuel supply regardless of new build pace.

Supporting [WILTW, 2025-07-24]:
- **China buildout:** CNEA targets 200GW installed capacity by 2040 — 10Mlbs/yr of incremental demand. China targeting 150 new reactors by 2035.
- **US industrial policy:** Four Executive Orders signed May 2025 target 400GW by 2050, NRC permit review from 30-42 months to 18 months [exact figures from Pequeno-Mota full PDF; Pequeno-Mota, 2025-10-28], ten large reactors under construction by 2030. Note: EOs are administrative directives, not legislation — subject to regulatory execution, court challenges, and political continuity. Big Beautiful Bill's IRA nuclear credit preservation is enacted law — more durable.
- **Europe reversal:** Germany reversed opposition to nuclear in EU legislation. Czech Republic: $18B deal with South Korea for two new plants. UK committed £14.2B to nuclear buildout. Belgium dropped phase-out. France: €6B to extend 20 reactor lifetimes.
- **SMRs:** Record six major approvals or construction starts globally in 2025. 100-200 SMRs by 2040 could drive 20,000-40,000 tonnes/yr incremental demand. [WILTW, 2025-07-24]

### Driver 3: Geopolitical concentration (Eduardo's frame)

Eduardo's claim: 40% of global uranium enrichment capacity still in Russia. [Incrementum-internal, 2025-10-12; 2025-12-15] This creates a structural vulnerability in Western utility supply chains that is independent of the price cycle.

Supporting [WILTW, 2025-07-24]:
- US ban on Russian uranium (2024) already enacted. EU Parliament considering ban covering 30.1% of EU enriched uranium sourced from Russia in 2023 (historically up to 38%). Combined US-EU ban would create immediate, severe pressure on supply chains.
- Niger fully nationalized Somair in June 2025 — live illustration of political risk in supply concentration. [WILTW, 2025-07-24] Note: this is a 2025 event, not an anticipated risk — it has already occurred.
- Kazakhstan (Kazatomprom) remains the world's largest producer; any disruption to Russian-Kazakhstani supply chains would hit simultaneously across enrichment and mining.

### Driver 4: Tech/AI/data center demand growth

Secondary layer — relevant but not Eduardo's primary frame. US nuclear-power announcements tied to data centers and AI: 16 over the past two years, equivalent to ~30% of current domestic atomic power. Amazon/Talen partnership for up to 1.9GW from Susquehanna plant. [WILTW, 2025-07-24]

Distinction: AI demand here is uranium-via-electricity-load, not uranium-correlated-with-chip-cycle. Thesis breaks on this driver if data center load is met by gas/renewables instead of nuclear, even if AI capex grows. This driver amplifies but does not anchor the structural thesis.

## Risks / Invalidation

**Primary risk — Eduardo's worldview (AI deflationary boom):** If AI-driven productivity gains are large enough to absorb labor market disruption without triggering a consumer credit crisis, the US could grow its way out of the fiscal spiral. That outcome weakens the case for hard assets broadly — gold, silver, copper, and uranium all lose their macro tailwind. This is the downside risk Eduardo named explicitly in his macro notes. [Incrementum-internal, 2025-10-12; 2025-12-15] Note: for uranium specifically, the AI-deflationary impact is partially dampened relative to gold and silver, because the fuel-cycle scarcity case is structurally independent of the dollar/fiscal trajectory — reactor fuel requirements do not disappear in a disinflationary scenario. The link is not zero (reactor buildout financing depends on capital costs and project economics), but uranium's dual nature — monetary hedge component AND fuel-cycle scarcity component — means the AI-deflation risk hits the first component harder than the second. As written, the primary risk is accurate for the metals theme; its bite on uranium is real but asymmetric.

**Eduardo's execution pattern risk:** The Iran exit demonstrates that macro/geopolitical risk events overrule the structural thesis in the short run. Eduardo zeroed before the escalation — structural deficit still existed, thesis was intact, but he correctly identified that the tail risk profile had deteriorated. Re-entry depends on tail risk assessment, not just structural metrics. The lesson: "cash is an active weapon" (risk-first §2) means the exit was correct independent of whether the Iran event ultimately damaged the structural thesis.

**Secondary risks:**

- **EO follow-through failure:** Trump's May 2025 EOs are administrative, not legislative. Permitting acceleration and 400GW targets require regulatory execution and political continuity. Risk: EOs generate headline visibility without physical capacity additions.
- **Financing and construction cost inflation:** Reactor builds are capital-intensive. Rising rates and construction cost inflation could slow projects without changing uranium demand trajectory — but they delay fuel-buyer commitment.
- **Kazatomprom/Russia disruption as double-edged risk:** Supply disruption could spike spot acutely, but could also raise political risk premium that compresses equity multiples even as spot rises. This is the only driver-and-risk on this page where the same factor appears on both sides. The asymmetry: structural supply concentration is a slow-moving thesis tailwind; acute disruption is a fast-moving equity-multiple compressor. Eduardo's 2024-25 exit was triggered by the second mechanism overwhelming the first. Re-entry must explicitly assess where in this cycle we are.
- **Sustained low contracting:** If utilities continue below-replacement-rate buying deeper into 2026, it signals demand expectations are lower than the thesis implies. Paralysis becoming structural — not temporary — is the invalidating signal.
- **Chinese demand revision:** China's 150-reactor / 200GW targets are policy-stated, not contracted. Any revision to China's nuclear buildout timeline would reduce the demand growth most responsible for the 120% by 2040 framing.
- **Secondary supply overhang:** Enrichment tails re-enrichment or HEU downblending could provide incremental supply if prices rise enough. Historically a cyclical dampener.
- **Data center power source mix:** If AI/hyperscaler power demand is met by gas turbines and renewables instead of nuclear PPAs, the AI/data-center driver disappears entirely. This is a partial thesis-weakener, not a full invalidator (Drivers 1-3 stand independently), but it removes the most-cited 2024-25 narrative tailwind.

**Invalidation threshold:** Invalidation requires sustained contracting at fractional-replacement-rate levels through 2027 AND visible NRC permitting stagnation. Specific thresholds to be set by Eduardo against current data.

## Implementation

**Universe:** Uranium is an equities and ETF story. Uranium futures (CME UxC U3O8) exist but are illiquid; physical uranium is held via closed-end funds (Sprott Physical Uranium Trust SPUT, Yellow Cake YCA); equities are the primary liquid expression.

**Spectrum from lower to higher volatility / leverage to spot price:**
- ETFs: URA (Global X Uranium ETF), URNM (Sprott Uranium Miners ETF) — diversified exposure, URNM more concentrated in miners. Less single-stock risk; lower potential upside vs. spot price.
- Senior producers (Cameco, Paladin PDN): Cameco has established production with long-term contracts providing revenue visibility. Paladin (PDN) restarted Langer Heinrich 2024, ramp ongoing — closer correlation to long-term contract price than spot. Lower vol, lower convexity.
- Development-stage with clear path to production (NexGen NXG — Arrow deposit in final permitting): higher optionality, driven by permitting milestones and spot price. More binary near-term risk.
- Juniors / explorers (Bannerman BMN, Boss Energy BOE): highest leverage to price cycle, most execution risk. Appropriate for small-asymmetric sizing only. Silex (SLX) is laser enrichment IP / GLE JV — different cash-flow profile from miners. Not a junior in the conventional sense; closer to a tech-licensing / strategic-supply play. Treat separately. [Pequeno-Mota overweight, reaffirmed 2025-10-28: PDN, BMN, NXG, SLX, BOE]

**Sizing implications (risk-first framework):**
