Key point: In our view, the discussion around “how many white collar jobs will 
AI disrupt?” misses the forest for the trees: Into already-accelerating western 
sovereign debt strains, even a small amount of white collar job disruption 
will likely be enough to trigger a major financial crisis.  We have no margin 
for error, let alone a deflationary shock as large as AI may soon become. 

 
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
In June 2004, Fed Chair Alan Greenspan raised interest rates for the first time in four years.  US home prices did not peak 
until 12 months later, around June 2005.   

From then, it took at least another 6-9 months before noticeable strains began being seen in subprime mortgage markets, 
in late 2006, but even then, nobody thought it was more than a localized problem, a headwind to the broader economy 
which was still strong.  Recall that Fed Chair Ben Bernanke famously said in May 2007 that “subprime mortgage woes 
won’t seriously hurt the US economy”. 

Then, in July 2007, two Bear Stearns hedge funds that had invested heavily in subprime mortgage bonds were declared 
to be “essentially worthless” virtually overnight; when that happened, all of Wall Street stopped and essentially said, “Wait, 
what?!”   

The virtual overnight collapse of those Bear Stearns mortgage funds marked the acceleration of a crisis that per above, 
had been already building for over three years.  From there, as we all know, events accelerated meaningfully, even as 
policymakers and US banking elites would continue to repeatedly publicly assure investors that everything was fine for the 
next 9-12 months.   

Here’s why we are revisiting this history: We believe that the first week of February 2026 was the “July 2007 Bear 
Stearns hedge fund ‘Wait, what?!’ moment” for AI disruption of white collar jobs.  Now, as then, the tremors 
started three years earlier: 

In the May 3, 2023 edition of FFTT titled “AI + Robotics may be about to “Blockbuster Video” the status quo, debt-
backed global financial system”, we asked… 

What is going to happen when a bigger deflationary wage shock hits a broader swath of America and the world, in a far more 
compressed time scale than what happened 2001-08?  What happens to the value of consumer loans?  Mortgage-Backed 
Securities?  Commercial real estate?  Bank capital levels?  The banking system?  Global derivatives where “notional becomes net” 
if just one big counterparty goes bankrupt?  Global sovereign debt?     

…before concluding:  

It is mindboggling, too much to consider all the potential implications.  However, there are a few things that are crystal clear to us 
about this dynamic that we want to leave with clients:   

1.  The current debt-backed financial system is fundamentally incompatible with what appears likely about to happen in 

technology, labor, wages, and tax receipts. 

2.  While we could see a brief moment of deflationary pressures that drive a bid for long-dated bonds, such a moment would 
likely be fleeting, as deflationary pressures into current global debt and fiscal positions will quickly drive a global debt and 
sovereign debt default cycle.  Unless there is a global moratorium placed on “autonomous labor” (not a policy 
recommendation, nor something we think is likely), then either global Central Banks would soon have to fully reserve most 
if not all global debt outstanding, or stand aside as a massive global debt deflation unfolds chaotically (i.e., a Depression).  

3.  Given a) the leverage already in the system and the already-precarious fiscal position of the global reserve currency 
issuer USA, and b) the pace at which “autonomous labor” appears to be progressing, the market developments 
“autonomous labor” is likely to cause are likely to unfold far faster than virtually any market participant currently believes.  

In plain English: The expansion of “autonomous labor” will likely briefly bid but then violently break global debt markets, 
and by extension, virtually all other markets, remarkably fast.  Global Central Banks will highly likely have to “fully 
reserve” global bond markets in coming years (i.e., buy up the majority of global bond markets with printed money.)

There are potentially massive social and political implications as well.  Suddenly, shrinking demographics may no longer be a risk 
but a political asset, as countries with lots of young people find those unemployed young people driving political instability instead 
of economic growth.  Unlike young people, old people tend to not participate in social unrest.   

If/as “autonomous labor” continues moving at exponential rates that leave the entire financial system in the same position as 
Blockbuster Video’s management team as digital download speeds became fast enough to allow NFLX to instantly deliver movies 
to every household in the US, virtually everything conventional wisdom knows to be true in macro may be wrong.  This could easily 
be the biggest “black swan” we have seen in our careers.   

Critically, the deflation shock “autonomous labor” represents ultimately results in a massive increase in human living standards 
(imagine a fleet of $5/hour robots doing everything you need to do).  The problem is NOT the deflation driven by tech; the problem 
is the deflation driven by tech taking place so rapidly in a debt-backed global financial system that needs inflation to avoid 
collapsing.   

In cases of either a massive and accelerating deflationary shock into an over-levered system (default risk) or global Central Banks 
printing money to try to prevent said deflationary shock from causing nominal sovereign debt defaults, inflation-hedged, 
counterparty risk free collateral should outperform every other asset on the board.  In our view physical gold and BTC are the 
two best and cleanest expressions of that trade.   

We revisited the topic again two years ago, in the May 28, 2024 edition of FFTT titled “Mr. X: “AI is fundamentally 
incompatible with the sovereign debt-based global monetary system””, we wrote the following… 

FFTT: So if AI, automation, and humanoid robotics impact the world even directionally as much as many investors think it 
will, SPX/TLT, NDX/TLT, Gold/TLT, & BTC/TLT charts should continue to re-rate much…higher? 

Mr. X: Exactly.  It’s quite the paradox, isn’t it?  A highly deflationary, productivity-driving impulse is actually NEGATIVE for long-
term USTs and other western sovereign bonds because US debt and deficits are so high that the Fed and/or Treasury cannot allow 
the free market to set rates on the higher US government deficits these productivity enhancers will drive, because if they did, the 
system would collapse, so they will likely do the only thing they can, which is apply more and more USD liquidity at ever-shorter 
intervals to keep the system functioning. 

…before concluding as follows:   

In our view, it is underappreciated by strategists and asset allocators that the sovereign debt-based global monetary 
system is fundamentally incompatible with the AI and other productivity-enhancing (wage deflationary) technologies 
many of those same strategists and asset allocators are clearly extremely excited about given the valuations being 
placed on investments tied to AI, etc.. 

If AI, humanoid robotics, and other significant productivity enhancers drive even directionally the productivity (wage 
deflation) some believe, the global monetary system will likely have to continue migrating even faster towards one with 
an equity-based neutral reserve asset like gold or BTC, suggesting continued significant price upside to both gold and 
BTC. 

This can happen via “big bang” (policy move), or organically (increasingly frequent USD liquidity injections to cap UST 
volatility caused by increasing US deficits as wage and job growth stagnate) – either way works for us.    

There is no change to our positioning.  We continue to be bullish on gold, BTC, US industrials, US electrical infrastructure, 
energy production assets, copper, and bearish on long-term USTs and other long-term western sovereign debt relative to 
those assets.  We continue to believe US nominal GDP growth and inflation will generally surprise to the upside.

SO HOW DO WE MAKE MONEY WITH THIS? 

It increasingly appears to us that early February 2026 for AI disruption of white collar work was akin to July 
2007 for subprime losses: Both moments represented markets beginning to fear/acknowledge the disruption 
was going to be much bigger than they had thought up to that point. 

We can only recall feeling this way two other times in our 30-year career: In 4q07 when it became apparent to 
us that the global financial system might be in real trouble, and for a few weeks in early March 2020 (before 
policymakers responded with mega-QE.) 

Everyone knows AI and robotics will likely be good for productivity and therefore corporate margins…and up until the 
first week of February, that was all anyone seemed to care about.  But all of a sudden, markets appear to be thinking 
about the potential downsides of “too fast” AI and robotics productivity gains that we have been warning about for 
three years: 

•  What are the risks to consumer credit and how quickly would losses metastasize through private credit and 

the broader financial system? 

•  What are the competitive risks to US multinationals abroad and at home if China implements and executes on 
AI and robotics better and faster than US multinationals (as China appears to be doing in many sectors)? 

•  What happens to US fiscal deficits if unemployment rises just a bit, given that employment is ~50% of US 

receipts and US deficits are already at record high levels v. GDP for peacetime? 

•  What happens to global sovereign debt markets which are already straining (led by Japan), before any 
instability to global employment, fiscal deficits, and/or profit margins caused by AI and robotics?   

Given the above unanswered questions that are now beginning to be asked by markets, we are astonished at the 
overall complacency in markets we are still seeing – whether we look at equity volatility (VIX), credit spreads, MOVE 
UST Volatility Index, or even XLP (consumer staples) divided by XLY (consumer discretionary) – investors are not 
worried, but we think the fear seen in software on the chart on page 11 will soon spread to other sectors. 

Given the above, we have >50% of our liquid net worth in gold and cash/T-Bills.  We are waiting for price action that 
tells us either that the “AI disruption is totally bunk” (even as a growing number of signs seem to be suggesting that AI 
disruption is NOT bunk)……or for signs that investors are as unnerved as we are (i.e., much lower asset prices for 
virtually everything but gold and USD.)  In the short run, this deflationary shock would likely also be good for 10y USTs 
(yields down)…but only for a bit…10y UST yields would likely bottom and begin rising after a couple of weeks in a 
“whoosh” down scenario, as we saw post-Liberation Day last year, and numerous other instances in recent years. 

Our base case mental model for the disruption AI will likely bring to white collar work in the next few years remains the 
China into the WTO levels of disruption for US manufacturing workers…except faster and from a starting point of a 
global sovereign debt crisis already underway.  This is why we are so overweight gold and cash/T-Bills; we think 
a “whoosh” down is coming, and we would look to deploy cash into it.  In our view, BTC’s weakness is an early 
warning of the dynamic above, and we think BTC is likely to go lower before we buy back what we sold in November.   

We continue to believe that the ultimate response to AI deflation by US and global policymakers will have to be a 
significant injection of USD liquidity…but our base case is that global policymakers likely cannot do so until they have 
a crisis, as policymakers are almost always reactionary to crises…rarely if ever are they proactive.
