China’s Economic Policies Are Causing Damage to Others, IMF Warns - Bloomberg  

The International Monetary Fund faulted China’s economic policies for causing waste at home and damage abroad 
and called for a reorientation by Beijing to embrace a model based on domestic consumer spending. 

Tree Ring: This week the IMF published in an “Article IV consultation” that China’s economic policies are “causing 
damage to trading partners” economies and encouraged China to “transition to a consumption-led growth model”: 

“Transitioning to a consumption-led growth model should be the overarching priority,” the IMF’s executive directors said in a 
statement Wednesday released alongside the Washington-based lender’s annual review of China’s economy, known as an 
Article IV consultation.  In that review, IMF staff highlighted China’s large current-account surplus, which has featured “adverse 
spillovers to trading partners.”  

The IMF said that some of China’s “excess current account surplus” despite the significant rise in CNY v. USD in 2025 
was due to a boost from “the inflation-adjusted weakening of the CNY”, which is the apparently the IMF-policy-speak way 
of saying “China experienced a big gain in productivity and therefore global competitiveness even though the

Some of that excess stems from exports getting a boost from “real depreciation of the RMB,” the fund said, referring to the 
inflation-adjusted weakening of the renminbi, also known as the yuan. 

The IMF suggested that a weaker yuan, measured in trade-weighted, inflation adjusted terms, had given Chinese goods an 
advantage overseas, while imports languished amid subdued domestic demand. Staff estimated the yuan was around 16% 
undervalued, with its range stretching from 12.1% to 20.7%.

China’s representative on the IMF’s executive board, Zhengxin Zhang, took issue with the criticism, saying in a separate

front-loading caused by Washington’s trade policy.

South Korea offers tax breaks on overseas stock sales to stem currency’s slide  

South Korea has introduced tax breaks for individuals who sell overseas stocks and reinvest in the domestic market 
as it tries to stem a craze for US equities that has sharply weakened the local currency. The finance ministry on

reinvested in South Korean stocks.

“The move is aimed at stabilizing the foreign exchange market and activating the country’s capital markets by 
encouraging the return of individuals investing in overseas stocks,” said Choi Ji-young, a finance ministry official. 
“Individuals made up less than 10 per cent of Korean investors’ overseas investment before 2020, but they now make 
up over 30 per cent.”
