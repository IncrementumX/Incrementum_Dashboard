The design behind Nixon’s August 15, 1971, dollar strategy did not emerge until October 1973, more than two years later, and

Certain influential voices in the Anglo-American financial establishment had devised a strategy to create again a strong dollar,

In May 1973, with the dramatic fall of the dollar still vivid, a group of 84 of the world's top financial and political insiders met at 
Saltsjobaden, Sweden, the secluded island resort of the Swedish Wallenberg banking family. This gathering of Prince

So why do we feel the gold/oil ratio will continue to move higher over time? 

Our view is that as gold and oil are the last two assets the USD was backed by as reserve currency, the gold/oil ratio 
(GoR) is the barometer of the health of the Petrodollar system.   

A lower GoR means a healthier Petrodollar (low gold = lower competition for USTs + higher oil = more Petrodollar 
surpluses to go into USTs); a higher GoR is a weaker Petrodollar (high gold = higher competition for USTs + relatively low 
oil = lower Petrodollar flows into USTs.) 

Given this, one can infer/conclude that the unallocated paper gold market centered in London was allowed to expand to 
unimaginable levels (50x?  100x?  500x?) in order to manage the price of gold and therefore the GoR and therefore 
defend the Petrodollar system/keep the Petrodollar system healthy, in order to support UST demand and US deficits.   

Had there never been an unallocated paper gold market, the GoR would likely already have gone to far higher 
levels than where it trades today, as every year, the net surpluses of an oil market that historically was 10-20x 
bigger than annual global physical gold production would have been bidding for physical gold.  That alone 
suggests a GoR at least 10-20x higher than the historical range of 15-25 bbls/oz, or 150-500 bbls/oz.   

Even if we are 100% wrong about the above, we can argue the opposite direction too and still arrive at the 
conclusion that GoR has to go a lot higher.   

Let’s pretend the GoR falls massively v. oil via oil rising sharply – every single sovereign bond in the world will quickly be 
in crisis and at risk of default if oil rises sharply, as inflation surges, the economy shrinks, and interest rates spike.  The oil 
inflation would likely drive rates to levels that drive interest expense > receipts for most western countries (more 
on this in a moment).  This was BIS Chair Jelle Zijlstra’s point in 1980:  

…the 2nd Oil Crisis could be worked through, slowly, but the international financial system could not survive a 3rd Oil Crisis – 
the inflation would make it impossible to recycle the petrodollars to the oil importing countries with any hope of repayment 
[FFTT: in real (oil) terms], trade would crumble, and the system would be brought to its knees. 

-BIS Chair Jelle Zijlstra, 1980 

Gold would be the last man standing in that case, so in our view, all roads lead to gold and a higher GoR 
ultimately.  Either: 

1.  Policymakers devalue oil v. gold (after making sure the “right” people have the “right” amount of gold, 
which might explain much of what has been happening with Central Bank gold purchases over the past 
10-15 years), or;  

2.  They let oil rip, collapsing sovereign debt markets, which collapses banking systems backed by that 

sovereign debt, which also collapses currencies backed by that sovereign debt (including the USD, EUR, 
JPY, etc.), and the world floods to gold as the last man standing, leaving the GoR far higher as the global 
economy is in tatters while gold is the only asset left that does not require a Central Bank printing press 
to make it money good (as all sovereign debt would at that point.) 

Option #1 is far more attractive for everyone involved; we hope that is the way things go, but either way, gold 
wins relative to virtually all other assets (thereby recapitalizing global sovereign and Central Bank balance 
sheets.)
