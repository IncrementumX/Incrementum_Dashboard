The Angell Paradox, named after Norman Angell, suggests that in highly interdependent economies, actions like war or 
sanctions often harm the aggressor or sanctioning nation more than the target, because disrupting trade and credit backfires, 
making everyone poorer by undermining commercial trust and legal certainty. It's essentially self-inflicted economic damage, 
where seizing assets or halting trade destroys the foundations of global commerce, hurting the sanctioning state's own 
industries and financial systems more than anticipated.

South Korea offers tax breaks on overseas stock sales to stem currency’s slide  

South Korea has introduced tax breaks for individuals who sell overseas stocks and reinvest in the domestic market 
as it tries to stem a craze for US equities that has sharply weakened the local currency. The finance ministry on 
Wednesday said proceeds from sales of foreign equities would be exempt from capital gains tax if the money was 
reinvested in South Korean stocks.  

The measure comes a day after South Korea’s won hit an eight-month low of Won1,484 against the dollar. Authorities 
are concerned the exchange rate could breach the Won1,500 level last seen during the 2008 financial crisis and the 
Asian currency meltdown of 1997.  

Concern over the weaker won has intensified in recent months as it could raise import prices and depress domestic 
consumption, aggravating the country’s economic slowdown. Authorities have blamed the currency’s weakness on 
retail investors’ increasing US equity holdings, which reached a record $160bn at the end of November, a fourfold 
rise from five years ago, according to Korea Securities Depository data.  

“The move is aimed at stabilizing the foreign exchange market and activating the country’s capital markets by 
encouraging the return of individuals investing in overseas stocks,” said Choi Ji-young, a finance ministry official. 
“Individuals made up less than 10 per cent of Korean investors’ overseas investment before 2020, but they now make 
up over 30 per cent.”
