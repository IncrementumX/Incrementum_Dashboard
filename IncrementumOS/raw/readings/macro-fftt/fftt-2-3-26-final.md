and global debt spiral 

The Fed should re-examine its great mistakes that led to the great inflation. It should 
abandon the dogma that inflation is caused when the economy grows too much and 
workers get paid too much. Inflation is caused when government spends too much and 
prints too much. Money on Wall Street is too easy, and credit on Main Street is too tight. 
The Fed’s bloated balance sheet, designed to support the biggest firms in a bygone crisis 
era, can be reduced significantly. That largesse can be redeployed in the form of lower 
interest rates to support households and small and medium-size businesses. 

-Kevin Warsh, WSJ op-ed, 11/16/25

We do NOT think Kevin Warsh is likely to be hawkish at the Fed.  With that said, many investors and analysts appear 
to believe that, so we thought it would be useful to revisit exactly why Warsh cannot be hawkish: Warsh’s operating 
environment prohibits him being hawkish, unless his goal is touching off a US and global debt death spiral. 

In the following pages, we will show why it is a double-entry bookkeeping certainty that if Kevin Warsh attempts to 
be hawkish or does anything to meaningfully tighten financial conditions or otherwise strengthens the USD “too 
much”, he is assured of tanking virtually every asset except for the USD and maybe gold, until he either relents 
on his own or is forced to by the ensuing US and global debt death spiral triggered by such hawkishness.

With us having spent the past eight pages conclusively showing that Warsh CANNOT be hawkish even if he wanted to, let 
us now highlight the plentiful evidence that he has no intention of being hawkish…indeed, Warsh’s own WSJ op-ed

The Federal Reserve’s broken leadership: Kevin Warsh – 11/16/25 
The Federal Reserve’s Broken Leadership - WSJ  

Second, inflation is a choice, and the Fed’s track record under Chairman Jerome Powell is one of unwise choices. 
The Fed should re-examine its great mistakes that led to the great inflation. It should abandon the dogma that 
inflation is caused when the economy grows too much and workers get paid too much. Inflation is caused when 
government spends too much and prints too much. Money on Wall Street is too easy, and credit on Main Street is too 
tight. The Fed’s bloated balance sheet, designed to support the biggest firms in a bygone crisis era, can be reduced 
significantly. That largesse can be redeployed in the form of lower interest rates to support households and small and 
medium-size businesses.   

Third, the Fed should take responsibility for its regulatory failures, including a deposit run on banks in late 2022 and 
early 2023. The Fed’s rules and regulations have systematically disadvantaged small and medium-size banks, which 
has slowed the flow of credit to the real economy. Fed leadership should support rather than undermine the good 
early work of the new vice chairman for supervision, Michelle Bowman, who is crafting a new regulatory framework. 

Fourth, the Fed under Janet Yellen and Mr. Powell has spent more than a decade negotiating bank regulatory and 
supervisory standards with its global counterparts in Basel, Switzerland. Fed leaders have tried to bind U.S. banks to 
a complicated, vaunted set of rules in the name of global regulatory convergence. In my view, the Basel endgame 
isn’t America’s endgame. A new, reformed American regulatory regime should make the U.S. the best place for the 
world’s banks to do business. That would spur new lending here at home. 

What Warsh leaves unsaid above: If the bond market revolts on higher growth, then policymakers will in our view

SO HOW DO WE MAKE MONEY WITH THIS? 

In our view, statements like “Warsh is going to be hawkish” and “the Fed is going to have its independence restored 
by Warsh” are the 2026 equivalent of 2025’s “Elon and DOGE are going to find $2tn in spending cuts”, and “the US 
has all the leverage v. China”, and 2022’s “Powell is going to be another Volcker.”  Sixth grade math told us DOGE, 
and China leverage, and Powell statements were all nonsense, and that sixth grade math won out in each 
case.   

In the same vein, sixth grade math tells us that “Warsh is going to be hawkish” and “the Fed is going to have 
its independence restored” are equally nonsensical.  So too are the investors saying Warsh, Bessent, and 
Druckenmiller can run a new version of the “Soros Imperial Dollar Cycle” (Loose fiscal + tighter monetary 
policy = higher USD, lower inflation, lower yields, higher equities.)   

Sixth grade math also tells us the “Soros Imperial Dollar Cycle” playbook, if attempted, will lead to USD up, 
everything else down (except gold), until either Warsh et al relent, or until the US and world go into a debt 
death spiral, likely led by Japan and the JGB market (which would amount to the US putting a bullet in what is 
supposedly a key ally in the great power competition v. China.) 

Our base case is that Warsh will be as dovish as Trump needs; the USD will resume its orderly descent 
(particularly v. CNY and EUR), gold will resume its ascent, along with industrial equities… 

...but we are concerned as it seems there are a number of investors and policymakers that seem to think 
Warsh being hawkish would be a smart idea.  It is not.  It is a foolish idea that would lead to disaster for the 
US and world, quickly…but that does not mean Warsh will not try…some people do not believe the stove is 
hot until they burn their own fingers to prove it. 

We remain significantly overweight gold, gold miners, and cash/T-Bills to start 2026, and are watching closely for 
further clues on the actual path forward (i.e., Warsh dovish, or Warsh is hawkish, crashes the US and global economy 
into a ditch, and then relents to dovishness.)
