“Asia refiners scour the world for oil as Strait of Hormuz flows halted”  

Asia refiners scour the world for oil as Strait of Hormuz flows halted – 3/17/26 
Iran War: Asia Refiners Scour the World for Oil as Strait of Hormuz Flows Halted - Bloomberg  

Asia’s oil refiners are ramping up crude purchases from outside the Middle East, the latest sign of a scramble to 
secure supplies as shipping through the vital Strait of Hormuz remains all-but halted. 

After initially balking at sky-high crude prices, Asian fuelmakers have returned to the market to secure cargoes from 
the US to Africa and the North Sea, with scant signs of a resolution to the war. They are now willing to pay elevated 
premiums to secure supply, traders familiar with the matter said. 

The upheaval in the Middle East has triggered fuel shortages in several parts of the world and even the US, the top 
oil producer, has seen gasoline and diesel prices surge. Asia — which depends heavily on crude from producers in 
the Persian Gulf — faces the greatest exposure to the disruption. 

Refiners in China, South Korea, India and Thailand have snapped up millions of barrels of crude recently, especially 
for prompt deliveries, the traders said.  Market behavior is also shifting. Trading activity for Middle East crude has 
slowed markedly, as refiners prioritize securing supply over participating in the spot market.

China's fuel export ban to further tighten Asia supply | Reuters  

China's ban on exports of diesel, gasoline and jet fuel is poised to exacerbate fuel shortages and further boost prices

against Iran. 

Even before the ban, Asian refiners were scrambling to secure alternative crude shipments while several refineries in

China close to tapping commercial oil stockpiles, FGE says – 3/18/26 
China Close to Tapping Commercial Oil Stockpiles, FGE Says - Bloomberg 

China, the world’s biggest crude importer, is close to tapping its vast commercial oil reserves as the Middle East war 
shows no signs of ending, according to FGE NexantECA. 

A drawdown in commercial and operational stockpiles amounting to as much as 1 million barrels a day may happen 
over the next four to six weeks, according to the industry consultant’s base case scenario. Processors — particularly 
in southern China — may be allowed to draw on commercial stockpiles to limit the extent of run cuts or prevent 
shutdowns, it said. 

It’s a lever that China can afford to pull. After more than a year of aggressive stockpiling, Beijing has built up an 
estimated 1.4 billion barrels of reserves that could be tapped if the Strait of Hormuz remains effectively shut.
