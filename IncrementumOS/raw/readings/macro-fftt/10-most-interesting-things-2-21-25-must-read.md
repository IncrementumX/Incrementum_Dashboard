“US interest expense being greater than US defense spending ‘sends a signal of weakness’ to the ‘axis of ill will’” 

Tree Ring: Niall Ferguson highlighted his “Ferguson’s Law” again this week (“any great power that spends more in interest 
payments on its debt than on defense will not be great for long”), noting that the US now falls into this category as of 
2024, and that this “sends a signal of weakness” to Beijing, Moscow, Tehran etc. 

Over the past year since Ferguson first publicly highlighted his namesake “law”, we have repeatedly highlighted it as an 
important historical observation, so we will not rehash it again.  Our interest in highlighting Ferguson’s comments on 
it this week are as follows: It is now a matter of US national security to significantly devalue the real value of $36T 
in US Federal debt. 

Translation? We hear all the time from people ranging from anonymous commenters on X to legendary investor 
Warren Buffett to “Never bet against America.”  We agree…but what Ferguson’s Law is in essence saying is 
“Never betting against America” means USTs must lose on a real basis… “yuge”…. “bigly.” 

The US now being in violation of Ferguson’s Law, and our adversaries knowing about it, means betting on LT 
USTs maintaining their value in real terms is betting against America.  The portion of the “Never bet against 
America” crowd that owns LT USTs is in denial about this realpolitik reality. 

Paradoxically, gold, BTC, T-Bills, and gold miners remain our four biggest positions by far, precisely because we 
are betting ON America…which has over the past 80 years twice massively devalued the USD v. gold (1933 and 
1971-80)…and the acuity of the US fiscal and debt position suggest the US is just about due for another such 
devaluation.  Let’s watch.

Bessent interview: Treasury Secretary says terming out US debt “A Long Way Off” – 2/20/25 
Bessent Interview: Treasury Secretary Says Terming Out US Debt ‘A Long Way Off’ - Bloomberg 

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent said that any move to boost the share of longer-term Treasuries in government 
debt issuance is some ways off, given current hurdles that include elevated inflation and the Federal Reserve’s 
quantitative tightening program. 

“That’s a long way off, and we’re going to see what the market wants,” Bessent said in an interview with Bloomberg 
Television’s Bloomberg Surveillance on Thursday, when asked about “terming out” sales of US Treasuries sales. “It’s 
going to be path dependent.” 

Before he came into office, Bessent had repeatedly criticized then-Treasury Secretary Janet Yellen for boosting the 
share of bills, which mature in up to a year, in US debt — something he argued held down longer-term yields and was 
done to boost the economy before the election. Yet Bessent retained the Yellen team’s plan for debt issuance earlier 
this month. 

Asked about his past comments, Bessent said Thursday that “the previous administration shortened some of the 
duration, and we haven’t shortened it further.” 

“The Fed said that they may stop their balance sheet runoff,” Bessent said. It would be “easier for me to extend 
duration when I’m not competing with another big seller.” He also said that he met with Fed Chair Jerome Powell on 
Wednesday, and would continue his practice of not commenting publicly on current monetary policy. 

Tree Ring: We had just gotten done typing that “betting on LT USTs maintaining their value in real terms is betting against 
America” in the prior Tree Ring point when US Treasury Secretary Bessent gave the Bloomberg interview above in which 
he confirmed our point, and the point we made in these pages two weeks ago (below): Bessent did not and will not 
term out US debt because he cannot do so without risking touching off a US debt spiral as he pushes the 10y 
UST yield >5%. 

Bessent’s failure to follow his own advice now that he is in the Treasury seat suggests Bessent got “read-in” on the 
actual state of affairs at the long end of the UST market and so Bessent did the only thing he could do for now – he 
kept Yellen’s UST issuance strategy.   

Allow us to translate into “macro speak” what Bessent said above: “I am targeting the level of LT UST issuance 
based on the level of the 10y UST yield, which recent history has shown cannot go much above 4.8% without 
causing market and economic problems.  In other words, I am continuing Yellen’s soft form of Yield Curve 
Control (YCC.)  It would help me greatly in maintaining this soft form of YCC if Powell would stop selling USTs, 
and I talked to him earlier this week, but I will neither confirm nor deny that I talked to him about soft form YCC.” 

A continuation of the soft form of YCC that Yellen had been practicing, combined with cooperation by the Fed, 
would be negative for USD, positive for gold and BTC, positive for LT UST yields (remember, lower USD within 
reason = lower 10y UST yields.)
