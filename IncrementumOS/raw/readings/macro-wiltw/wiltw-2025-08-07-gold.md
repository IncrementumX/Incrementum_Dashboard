Global gold demand continues to grow 
faster than mine supply, which has 
been flat for seven years despite a near 
tripling of prices. Stagflationary U.S. 
economic trends, falling confidence in 
data reporting and uncertainty regarding 
the Fed’s independence underscore the 
intrinsic value of the yellow metal. Recent 
temporary price weakness is presenting 
buying opportunities in mining shares.

Investment  demand  has  been  driving  the  gold  market  so  far  this  year.  Global 
gold demand rose 3% y/y in 2Q-2025, to 1,249 tonnes, but investment demand 
grew 78% y/y in the second quarter. This was driven by an 11% y/y increase in 
bar and coin demand and the turnaround from 7 tonnes of ETF outflows a year 
ago to a 171 tonne inflow to global ETFs during Q2. 

And during the first half of this year, ETF demand was more than three times 
the  level  of  2024’s  second  half,  as  shown  in  the  left-hand  chart  below.  (Last 
year’s first half showed an outflow). China has been a particularly-strong driver of 
investment flows, as the right-hand chart shows.

It  is  also  worth  noting  that,  according  to  a  recent  World  Gold  Council  (WGC)

held in gold to rise over the next year, while 43% plan to increase their own 
gold  holdings.  While  nobody  expects  gold  holdings  to  decline,  73%  expect 
lower reserve shares in USD.

But  the  bigger,  more  intractable  issue  hanging  overhead  is  the  loss  of  trust  in

He elaborated, as follows:

There is the case of Greece, where the government faked deficit numbers 
for years, contributing to a debilitating debt crisis that required multiple 
rounds  of  bailouts.  The  country  then  criminally  prosecuted  the  head  of 
statistical  agency  when  he  insisted  on  reporting  the  true  figures,  further 
eroding the country’s international standing…

Perhaps  most  famously,  there  is  the  case  of  Argentina,  which  in  the  2000s 
and  2010s  systematically  understated  inflation  figures  to  such  a  degree  that

a  debt  crisis  that  ultimately  led  to  it  defaulting  on  its  international 
obligations.

The more that trust in Washington, the USD and USTs is undermined, the greater 
the  attractiveness  of  gold,  which  does  not  carry  political  or  counterparty  risk.

likelihood  that  Fed  policy  will  loosen,  which  causes  the  dollar  to  weaken

Gold  is  a  textbook  example  of  a  bull  market  climbing  a  wall  of  worry.  Any

Multiple factors are converging to fuel the 
rally in the global agriculture sector. Could the 
AG sector be on the cusp of a new bull market? 
What are the implications? How to invest. 
We have argued for over two decades that extreme weather, spreading drought, 
declining arable land, increasing soil erosion, and plateauing grain yields would 
significantly  impact  food  production  (see  reports).  The 13D Food Security Index 
has been testing technical support for over two years, but has rallied 27.7% from its 
April 8th low.  Factors  driving  the  recent  performance  include  growing  evidence 
that  the  agricultural  sector  is  beginning  a  cyclical  upswing.  However,  global 
food  production  continues  to  face  mounting  challenges.  With  ending  grain 
stocks near their lowest level in a decade, the global agribusiness sector could be a 
coiled spring poised to release.

Consider the following:

  Global  food  production  is  concentrated  in  a  few  key “breadbaskets”  that 
are increasingly vulnerable to climate change. Major crop-growing regions, 
including the U.S. Midwest, are on track to see sharp drops in harvests due to 
climate  change,  concludes  a  new  Nature  study.  Six  key  staple  crops  could 
see  an  average  11.2%  decline  in  output  by  the  end  of  the  century  in  a 
moderate 2–3-degree greenhouse-gas emissions scenario, even as farmers 
try to adapt. The largest production declines will be from major food producers. 

Extreme  weather  is  already  impacting  harvests  this  year.  Flooding  in 
Tajikistan  destroyed  the  rice  crop.  Severe  storms  in  the  U.S.  also  caused 
millions  of  dollars  of  damage  to  crops.  Another  study  from  the  Barcelona 
Supercomputing Center directly linked dozens of extreme weather events to 
sharp spikes in food prices, underscoring the growing vulnerability of food-
production systems.

To adapt, farmers are testing different crop varieties that are more resilient to 
climate change, adjusting the timing of planting, refining the use of fertilizers 
and water, and investing in water infrastructure, such as reservoirs.

World  corn  inventories  are  also  set  to  reach  their  lowest  level  since

  A  significant  portion  of  the  world’s  agricultural  croplands  are 
currently  suffering  from  low-moisture  content  in  soil  moisture  and 
low  groundwater  levels.  Key  areas  include  the  Americas,  Europe,  Asia, 
and parts of Africa and Australia, according to the Global Drought Monitor. 
Much of Europe, southern South America, Asia, Australia, and parts of Africa, 
experienced dry conditions in June. In the U.S., 84.3% of the West and 47.1% 
of  the  High  Plains  are  experiencing  dry-to-exceptional  drought  conditions, 
per the U.S. Drought Monitor.

  The U.S. agriculture sector is in the early stages of a cyclical upturn. The 
U.S.  Department  of  Agriculture  (USDA)  projects  net-farm  income  will  rise  by 
nearly 30% in 2025, reaching $180.1 billion, second only to its 2022 peak, and 
rebounding  from  two  consecutive  years  of  decline.  Driving  the  rise  in  farm 
income are improving commodity prices and U.S. government payments. Net-

The  overhaul  of  China’s  drug-approval  system  in  2015  marked  a  turning

channel slashing approval timeline from 60 working days to 30—a strategic 
move to position China at the forefront of high-quality pharmaceutical innovation.

Handwriting is one of the most complex 
motor skills the brain can do. What is 
lost as handwriting wanes? What are the 
implications for kids and learning? 
Both handwriting and typewriting involve moving our hands to form words on a 
page. But writing by hand involves far greater coordination between the brain’s 
motor  and  visual  systems.  This  coordination,  it  turns  out,  engages  our  brain 
more deeply in ways that enhance learning, creativity, and even how we think 
and feel. 

Researchers  have  found,  for  instance,  that  when  children  draw  a  letter 
freehand, they activate three areas of the brain that are activated in adults 
when they read and write: the left fusiform gyrus, the inferior frontal gyrus 
and the posterior parietal cortex. By contrast, children who type or trace the 
shape of a letter showed no such effect. This means it is only through the actual 
effort of writing by hand that the brain’s motor pathways engage and deliver the 
benefits of handwriting. And those benefits are life-altering indeed: children not 
only learn to read more quickly when they first learn to write by hand, but they 
also become better at generating ideas and retaining information. 

In  short,  it’s  not  just  what  we  write  that  matters—but  how  we  write—that 
lays the foundations of literacy, learning, and thought. 

We quote Stanislas Dehaene, a psychologist at the Collège de France in Paris:

When we write, a unique neural circuit is automatically activated. There is 
a core recognition of the gesture in the written word, a sort of recognition 
by mental simulation in your brain. And it seems that this circuit is contributing 
in unique ways we didn’t realize. Learning is made easier.

and childless. What are the implications? 
At first glance, this might seem like a progressive reflection of women’s growing 
autonomy, particularly in pursuit of education and career advancement. A recent 
report from Morgan Stanley confirms this trend, finding that many women are 
opting out of motherhood to prioritize personal and professional development. 
But  beneath  the  surface  lies  a  more  complex  question:  what are the long-term 
consequences  for  health  and  well-being  of  delaying  or  forgoing  partnership  and 
family life?

Partnership  and  family  formation  are  not  just  cultural  touchpoints;  they 
have  measurable  physiological  effects.  Mounting  research  shows  that 
stable  romantic  relationships  and  parenthood  are  associated  with  longer 
lifespans, better cardiovascular health, stronger immune systems, and reduced 
risks  of  depression,  anxiety,  and  chronic  illness.  If  a  growing  segment  of 
the  population  is  opting  out  of  these  life  pathways,  the  public-health 
implications could be significant. 

According to U.S. Census data, the percentage of single women is projected to 
rise by 1.2% annually through 2030. In many cases, this is a pragmatic decision. 
A 2024 study by SmartAsset estimates that raising a child now costs $36,000 per 
year on average, a figure that puts parenthood out of reach for many. Others cite 
the emotional toll of modern dating or the widespread sense of disconnection 
exacerbated  by  digital  life.  Social  media  movements  like  the  “boy  sober” 
trend,  which  encourage  women  to  prioritize  self-growth  and  avoid 
romantic  entanglements,  reflect  a  growing  skepticism  about  the  value  of 
partnership.  In  certain  circumstances,  such  skepticism  may  be  warranted.  But 
should this be a permanent stance? 

One  of  the  more  revealing  findings  from  the  Morgan  Stanley  study  is  that 
while  financial  concerns  are  often  cited  as  the  reason  for 
avoiding family formation, the average single woman spends 
more  annually  than  the  average  family  household.  Much  of 
this  spending  goes  toward  nightlife,  dining  out,  skincare,  and  clothing—
investments  that  may  reflect,  consciously  or  not,  the  desire  to  meet  a  partner. 
Moreover,  according  to  2025  data  from  the  dating  service  Match,  the  average 
cost of dating in the U.S. is now over $300 per month. 

This raises a difficult question: is the decision to remain single and childless 
always a deliberate choice, or is it increasingly a byproduct of a fragmented, 
hyper-individualized society? A 2024 Forbes piece found that 54% of men and 
68% of women aged 18 to 34 want a romantic relationship but are not in one. 
Part of the disconnect may stem from shifting priorities. Over 90% of singles say 
that physical chemistry is the most important factor in a relationship, while only 
48%  prioritize  empathy  and  just  35%  cite  shared  values.  While  attraction  is 
important,  this  disproportionate  emphasis  on  surface-level  compatibility 
suggests a broader cultural drift towards superficiality—potentially at the 
expense of long-term fulfillment.

This  has  real  consequences.  A  study  by  Harvard  Medical  School  found  that 
people  in  marriages  or  long-term  partnerships  experience  fewer  strokes 
and  heart  attacks,  are  less  likely  to  be  diagnosed  with  cancer,  and  have 
lower rates of anxiety and depression. Not only are these individuals healthier, 
but they are also more likely to survive serious health events, including surgeries 
and cancer diagnoses, than their single counterparts. 

Cardiologists have even coined the term “the marriage benefit” to describe 
this phenomenon. In a longitudinal study of 25,000 adults, married individuals 
were 14% more likely to survive a heart attack and typically left the hospital two 
days sooner than single patients. 

Why? The science points to a key mechanism: stress regulation. Individuals in 
healthy  relationships  tend  to  produce  lower  levels  of  cortisol,  the  body’s 
primary  stress  hormone.  Elevated  cortisol  is  linked  to  a  host  of  negative 
outcomes,  from  premature  aging  to  weight  gain,  high  blood  pressure,  and 
mood  disorders.  But  in  the  context  of  a  stable  relationship,  partners  act  as 
psychological  buffers—especially  through  physical  touch  which  releases 
oxytocin,  a  hormone  that  promotes  calm  and  safety.  Remarkably,  fMRI 
studies  have  shown  that  individuals  anticipating  pain  experienced  reduced 
amygdala activation—the brain’s threat center—simply by viewing a picture of 
their romantic partner. 

These  benefits  are  not  exclusive  to  young  couples.  In  fact,  marriage  after  60 
has  been  shown  to  yield  particularly  strong  health  advantages,  largely 
because  such  relationships  tend  to  be  rooted  in  emotional  intimacy  and 
companionship rather than physical appearance. Older couples also tend to 
be  more  financially  and  emotionally  stable,  making  late-life  partnership  both 
more feasible and more fulfilling. 

Parenthood  also  brings  lasting  psychological  and  physical  benefits.  Parents  tend 
to  engage  in  more  frequent  social  interactions,  whether  with  neighbors  or  other 
parents,  contributing  to  higher  levels  of  well-being.  According  to  Harvard  Health 
Publishing, people with children have lower risks of certain cancers and longer life 
expectancies. Children also provide meaning and purpose, a well-established 
buffer against depression, especially later in life. As children grow up, they often 
become vital sources of social, emotional, and practical support for aging parents.

So  if  nearly  half  of  women  will  be  single  and  childless  by  2030,  what  happens

One such innovation trend that caught our attention was asset tokenization. 

The PWG paper proposed that “Banking Agencies should clarify the circumstances 
under which tokenized assets and tokenized asset collateral would be subject to 
the same capital and liquidity treatment as the underlying asset or collateral.”

A token is a digital certificate that records ownership on a blockchain. In theory, 
any  asset  can  be  tokenized:  real  estate,  commodities,  precious  metals,  non-
fungible  items  such  as  fine  art,  intellectual  property  such  as  trademarks,  fiat 
currency (known as stablecoins), and financial instruments such as bonds, stocks 
and derivatives.

Tokenization  could  bring  a  number  of  benefits  including  greater  liquidity 
(particularly  for  more  illiquid  assets),  greater  accessibility,  increased 
transparency  around  ownership  as  well  as  lower  administrative  costs  for 
issuance,  trading  and  settlement.  Tokens  could  also  act  as  a  bridge  for  off-
chain collateral that is then used for activities in decentralized finance (or DeFi). 

As an aside, we would caution use of the term DeFi as a catch-all term. In WILTW 
August 25, 2022, we argued a more accurate term for DeFi running on Ethereum, 
let alone Solana, is “permissioned OpenFi” due to the centralizing tendencies of 
the underlying Layer-1 blockchain. By contrast, Layer-2 financial primitives built 
on top of the Bitcoin blockchain, which is credibly decentralized and censorship-
resistant can, in our view, be legitimately termed as DeFi (see WILTW March 20, 
2025 and January 20, 2022).
