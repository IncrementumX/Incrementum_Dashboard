Of  course,  some  of  this  recent  strength  is  likely  due  to  the  subsiding  of 
tariff-related  uncertainty,  which  had  intensified  during  the  summer  before 
Washington decided not to impose tariffs on refined copper imports, as well as 
Freeport’s  declaration  of  force  majeure  at  its  Grasberg  mine  in  Indonesia, 
after production was disrupted by a fatal mudslide.

The  Grasberg  problem  is  only  the  latest  in  a  series  of  weather-related 
disruptions to global commodity-supply chains that are likely to intensify, 
as  we  explain  in  section  12.  Combined  with  the  flooding  last  spring  in  the 
Democratic Republic of Congo and a tunnel collapse in Chile during the summer, 
the loss of supply at Grasberg is exacerbating an already-tight market. 

Keeping the market adequately supplied is only going to become more challenging, 
which  is  another  reason  why  copper  prices  are  likely  to  continue  rising.  And 
making  matters  worse,  the  production  outage  is  straining  relations  with 
the  Indonesian  government,  which  owns  51%  of  Grasberg  and  has  been 
pushing for even greater control, underscoring the global risk of spreading 
resource nationalism.

Another  key,  but  largely  overlooked,  factor  has  been  the  strength  in  Chinese 
Government Bond (CGB) yields, which have recently broken-out of a multi-year 
trendline, rising from a low of 1.65% during the summer to 1.9% yesterday. As 
we wrote last week, CGB yields are highly correlated with commodity prices, 
and  are  a  sign  that  China’s  anti-deflation  efforts  are  having  a  meaningful 
positive impact on metals prices. (See WILTW September 18, 2025.)

Another  bullish  development  is  the  surge  in  emerging-market  equities, 
as  we  outlined  in  WATMTU  September  21,  2025.  This  points  to  an  acceleration 
of  capital  flows  to  long-neglected  EMs  and  could  propel  a  virtuous  cycle  in 
emerging-market  fixed-asset  investment  and  economic  growth,  which  would 
also benefit copper.

The  recently-announced  $53  billion  merger  between  Anglo  American  and 
Teck Resources further underscores our bullish long-term copper thesis. If 
the  deal  is  ultimately  completed,  the  combined  company  would  produce  1.2

An  estimated  70%  of  the  combined  company’s  production  would  be  derived

Our long-held view is that declining copper ore grades, longer lead times 
and harder-to-access resources will require more consolidation among the 
industry’s players. Higher capital costs and the growing capital intensity of the 
sector will also require that mining companies increase their scale of operations. 

In its August 2025 commodity outlook, BHP Group noted that multiple copper 
projects have been subject to higher cost estimates, as a sample of brownfield 
projects in Latin America point to a 65% increase in capital intensity since 
2010.  BHP  added: “Technological  progress  can  help  at  the  margin  to  improve 
the productivity of existing operations, including innovative leaching. However, 
this  inflationary  trend  may  prove  stubbornly  persistent.  For  future  mine 
supply to be incentivised, we expect prices still need to rise from today’s levels to be 
sufficiently high to trigger the investment that will be needed.”

Electric-grid infrastructure is arguably 
the most important, yet underrated and 
underinvested, component of global 
economic growth and technological 
progress. A new supercycle of investment 
is unfolding as governments worldwide 
recognize the importance of grid 
infrastructure in staying competitive. 
This week, we are launching the 13D Grid 
Infrastructure Index to capitalize on the 
opportunity. 
Electricity has been the foundation of every major technological advancement, 
driving  social  and  economic  growth  over  the  past  century.  It  powered  the 
Second-Industrial Age, enabled the Digital Revolution, and is now at the center 
of the AI Era. 

We  have  argued  for  over  two  decades  that  much  of  the  world’s  electric 
grids are antiquated and need significant upgrades (see reports). The world’s 
electric grids are largely based on 1950s designs, with the average infrastructure 
in the U.S. and Europe being around 40 years old, and some parts dating back as 
far as 50 to 70 years. Besides replacing the ageing infrastructure, grid digitization 
and adding transmission and distribution capacity are necessary.

The  grid  infrastructure  in  the  U.S.  is  in  a  precarious  state.  The  U.S.  grid 
scored a D+ on the 2025 Infrastructure Report Card, with the American Society 
of Civil Engineers warning that reliability risks are rising across all regions. A U.S. 
government  study  in  July  warned  that  failing  to  strengthen  the  electric  grid, 
blackouts  could  increase  100-fold  in  2030,  reaching  34  days  or  more,  and

Supply-chain  bottlenecks  and  shortages  of  grid-infrastructure  equipment  and 
high-capacity  transmission  cables  are  surging  as  demand  soars,  increasing

grids represent a once-in-a-generation investment opportunity, and humanity is at 
the forefront of it.

Consider the following:

  Electricity  demand  is  soaring  at  the  fastest  pace  in  several  years.  The 
International  Energy  Agency  (IEA)  notes  that  global  electricity  demand 
is  expected  to  grow  at  4%  through  2027.  The  convergence  of  two-
contemporary  factors  drives  demand  growth:  (1)  the  electrification  of

suffer  over  $7.6  billion  in  direct  damages  annually  from  storms  and  flooding. 
However, the impacts on global trade are far greater at $81 billion annually due 
to  the  downtime  associated  with  reconstruction  and  the  time  that  ports  are 
inoperable. 

That  said,  when  economic  activity  linked  to  industry  output  and  consumption 
is factored in, the global impact of extreme weather on port infrastructure 
rises to at least $122 billion annually, according to a study in Nature Climate 
Change.

supply chain and reveals the risks of nationwide drug shortages, according to a 
new study.
