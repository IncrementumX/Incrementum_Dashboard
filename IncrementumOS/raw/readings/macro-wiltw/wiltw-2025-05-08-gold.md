Adversity is the Best Teacher. 
The  article  below  is  a  talk  by  Kiril  Sokoloff  given  on  Friday,  May  2,  2025,  at  St. 
Paul’s School in Concord, NH on the occasion of Anniversary Weekend. 

*   *   *

My  life  has  been  defined  by  my  deafness.  So  let  me  begin  with  the  lesson  of 
that  experience.  Essentially,  deafness  is  about  failure  at  the  most  basic  and 
fundamental  level  of  human  interaction—communication.  If  you  fail  over  and 
over again at something, you have two choices. 

You can give up or you can become stronger. I pledged to myself that I would 
never give up. And that I would live a normal life in every way, never saying no 
to an invitation, no matter how stressful the event might be for me.

Hellen Keller was blind and deaf. She said, “The blind are cut off from things and 
the deaf are cut off from people.”

At  15,  I  had  difficulty  understanding  the  words  in  songs.  At  18,  I  had  my  first 
hearing test and found that my high-frequency (speech) hearing was fading. 

The erosion continued and, by my mid-20s, I could no longer understand speech 
in  noisy  places.  High-powered  hearing  aids  and  phone  amplifiers  helped  for  a 
while, but could not put off the inevitable.

Increased  deafness  brings  fear.  In  the  mid-1980s,  when  my  hearing  loss 
accelerated,  I  never  imagined  I  would  be  totally  deaf  in  a  few  years.  In  the 
spring  of  1988,  I  went  skiing  in  New  Mexico.  Maybe  the  high  altitude  reduced 
circulation to my auditory nerve. 

Six weeks later, my “good ear” was totally deaf. The suddenness of the loss scared 
me profoundly. I knew that it was only a matter of time until I lost the hearing in 
the other ear. 

But that knowledge did nothing to eliminate my growing fear of the unknown 
and the mounting stress I felt as I  tried to  cope in a world that was becoming 
quieter and quieter.

Unfortunately, I was unable to hear the nuances in people’s voices. I didn’t know 
whether they were happy or angry or impatient or cold. I didn’t know when they 
finished talking. I frequently interrupted unintentionally. 

Since my assistant could not type as fast as speech, I missed a lot, sometimes an 
entire conversation. There was a long lag between a question and its appearance 
on the computer screen. But this was better than not using the phone at all.

Meanwhile,  the  deterioration  in  my  hearing  continued  relentlessly.  Then 
one  morning  I  woke  up  and  could  no  longer  hear  my  own  voice.  There  were 
hundreds of days when I didn’t think I could go on.

But I did go on.

According to a Chinese proverb: “If you get up one more time than you fall, you will 
make it through.” Or as Samuel Beckett wrote: “Ever tried. Ever failed. No matter. Try 
again. Fail again. Fail better.” Later, he put it even better: “I can’t go on…I go on.”

Rudyard  Kipling  put  it  bluntly: “We  have  40  million  reasons  for  failure,  but  not 
a single excuse.” Joseph Daniels believed that “defeat never comes to any man 
until he admits it.”

Or,  consider Vince  Lombardi’s  unforgettable  words: “The  harder  you  work,  the 
harder it is to surrender.” John Wooden inspired UCLA to the best record in the 
history  of  college  basketball  with  the  following  belief: “Success  is  never  final. 
Failure is never fatal. It’s courage that counts.”

You never know what you can endure until you have been really tested, failed, 
hit  bottom  (you  think),  staggered  to  your  feet  again,  only  to  be  thrown  to  the 
floor again. Deafness made me who I am. Deafness molded me, toughened me 
and softened me, shaped my character and the way I look at life.

the heart, a personal transformation. It is only adversity that makes you question 
who you are, how you must change, and what you need to learn. 

Suffering  deepens  and  awakens  the  soul.  Suffering  heightens  sensitivity. 
Suffering  leads  to  spiritual  development.  Suffering  makes  you  know  what’s 
really important. Suffering teaches you compassion for others. Suffering teaches 
you to forgive yourself and to open your heart. 

Suffering makes you appreciate what you have, not what you miss or think you 
want. Suffering raises your consciousness, so you feel more deeply than before 
and see beauty everywhere.

But, the greatest lesson of deafness was learning the importance of surrendering 
to the will of God. Somewhere on the road to deafness, I realized there is a force 
vastly greater than the human will. 

You stand on the beach, feet planted firmly in the sand, jaw set and determined, 
muscles tensed—and suddenly a tidal wave comes and flattens you. I came to 
accept that deafness was bigger than me. I lay down before this superior force, 
God’s will, my destiny, and swam with the current. 

This revelation was perhaps the most profound emotional experience in my life.

In the fall of 1996, I had a cochlear implant, a form of bionic ear. They surgically 
implant electrodes in your head, which stimulate the brain directly. 

You  must  learn  to  process  speech  all  over  again,  which  can  take  a  long  time, 
depending on the person. I became a classic example of neuroplasticity, a brain 
that changes itself. 

It’s  now Thanksgiving  of  1996.  I’m  in  Sun Valley  spending  the  holidays  with  Jack 
Hemingway. Jack has a big mustache and he mumbles, a devastating combination. 

I say to Jack: “All these years, I never heard a word you said.” 

“Yes, I know. It’s been very frustrating for me.” 

“Now, I can hear you.”

time in the history of the world to be deaf. What a miracle!

Deafness gave me many precious gifts. I became an even more voracious reader. 
I developed an acute intuition. Forced to become a bystander, I learned powerful 
skills of observation. Through loneliness and despair, I grew as a human being, 
and I found compassion on a large scale. 

Conquering the challenge of deafness, I traveled on a long journey of my soul.

Many  of  you  have  already  experienced  some  adversity.  As  you  get  older, 
everyone  will.  My  message  today  is  to  tell  you  to  embrace  adversity  and  not 
be fearful of it. Adversity and suffering will make you stronger. It will make you 
more  resilient.  It  will  make  you  compassionate  and  full  of  empathy  for  other 
people and for the beauty of our planet. 

I will leave you with this powerful quote from Kahlil Gibran: “Out of suffering have 
emerged the strongest souls; the most massive characters are seared with scars.”

Antimony (Sb) is particularly important due to its use in defense and other

America’s virtually non-existent rare-earth 
elements and permanent-magnets supply 
chains are now the number one national-

How to invest. 
Over the last decade, we have explored the impact of the escalating U.S.-China 
tech-arms  race,  the  rising  risk  of  single  points  of  failure,  and  the  ramifications 
of China’s control over the supply of critical minerals. This week, we argue that 
America’s  midstream  processing  of  rare-earth  elements  (REEs)  for  permanent 
magnets—“the  missing  middle”—is  a  single  point  of  failure  for  U.S.-
technology supply chains.

The  REE  industry  can  be  divided  into  three  stages:  upstream,  the  mining  of 
elements;  midstream,  the  processing  of  elements  into  separate  oxides;  and 
downstream, the manufacturing of permanent magnets and other components. 
REE  oxides  are  compounds  formed  when  rare  earths  combine  with  oxygen. 
When  processed,  rare  earths  then  make  up  the  permanent  magnets  that 
underpin  several  key  global  industries.  John  Kutsch  of  the Thorium  Energy 
Alliance summarizes: 

There is no piece of modern technology that doesn’t use rare earths or other 
technology  metals.  There  are  no  drones,  windmills,  electric  cars,  computers, 
lasers,  radar  systems,  magnets  of  quality,  or  medical  devices  which  aren’t 
100-percent reliant on China for components using their critical materials. 

Without  rare  earths,  there  are  no  permanent  magnets. 
Without permanent magnets, there is no industry.

In recent months, China has banned or restricted the export of light rare-earth 
elements (LREEs) and heavy rare-earth elements (HREEs), as well as other critical 
minerals. China’s moat around mining and processing HREEs is wide, strong, 
and difficult to replicate quickly.

What do psychologists say are the most 
important things a parent can teach their 
child? 
Parenting is one of the most demanding yet rewarding roles an individual can 
undertake. It is also one of the most scrutinized—constantly subject to outside 
opinions,  unsolicited  advice,  and  a  flood  of  conflicting  information.  Amid  this 
noise,  many  parents  understandably  turn  to  expert  guidance  for  clarity.  Here, 
we distill the ten most essential lessons that child and developmental psychologists 
identify for caregivers to impart on their children. 

1.  Curiosity

Curiosity comes naturally to young children, but it must be nurtured to flourish. 
It  drives  exploration,  problem-solving,  language  development,  and  emotional 
growth.  Curious  children  are  more  likely  to  retain  information,  engage 
actively  in  learning,  and  develop  cognitive  flexibility.  They  are  also  more 
likely to seek out and be comfortable with new and different experiences. 

Psychologist Dr. Lisa Strohman advises, “Parents can encourage their children to 
be curious by asking questions, learning with the child and showing interest in 
the learning process. Getting children to ask ‘Why’ and ‘How’ helps them to 
think critically and develop an appreciation of learning.” 

2.  Emotional regulation

Emotional  regulation—the  ability  to  manage  and  respond  to  emotions  in 
healthy  ways—is  essential  for  children  to  navigate  relationships,  stress,  and

caregiver is always there to support them.

3.  Redefining failure

Many children internalize the belief that failure is inherently negative. However, 
psychologists argue that failure is one of the most powerful learning tools 
when framed correctly. 

Dr. Angela Celio Doyle is a psychologist and vice president of behavioral health for 
the virtual eating-disorder treatment, Equip Health. She notes, “It’s natural to want 
to avoid failing, but that’s how we learn and grow. Without learning to fail, kids 
become perfectionists, never feeling good enough or too anxious to try in fear 
of failure. A growth mindset reframes failure as a stepping stone, not a setback.”

4.  Words have power

Dr. Rosemary P. Hodges, a Phoenix Children’s staff psychologist, reminds parents 
to  abandon  the  outdated  idea  that  “Sticks  and  stones  may  break  my  bones, 
but words will never hurt me.” Children must learn that words carry emotional 
weight.  When caregivers model  emotional honesty—naming hurt feelings 
rather  than  brushing  them  off—they  teach  children  to  speak  and  listen 
with care. 

If  a  child  lashes  out  verbally,  it  is  not  enough  to  dismiss  it  as  mere  acting  out. 
Parents should calmly acknowledge the impact: “That really hurt my feelings. 
Let’s  talk  about  what  made  you  say  that.”  This  approach  fosters  empathy, 
accountability, and emotional awareness. 

5.  How to give and accept apologies

Apologies are not just social niceties—they are essential tools for building and 
repairing  relationships.  Teaching  children  how  to  apologize  sincerely,  and 
how to forgive others, cultivates humility, empathy, and resilience.

6.  Empathy

Empathy—the  ability  to  understand  and  care  about  another’s  emotional 
experience—is foundational for kindness, cooperation, and moral development. 
Empathetic  children  are  more  likely  to  become  socially  responsible, 
compassionate, emotionally intelligent adults. Empathy is also the building 
block for moral development and spirituality. 

Parents  can  foster  empathy  by  modeling  compassion  and  attentive  listening. 
This  can  take  the  form  of  offering  help,  speaking  kindly,  and  listening  with 
interest to others, including strangers. Naming and validating emotions will also 
help children to label their own feelings and be more attuned to those around 
them. For instance, a parent might say, “You look frustrated, can we talk about 
what’s going on.” 

Reading books and stories also promotes children to engage in the act of 
perspective-taking, or inhabiting the mental space of someone else, even 
if  it  is  just  a  fictional  character. You  might  ask, “How  do  you  think  Little  Red 
Riding Hood felt when that happened?” or “What would you want someone to 
do if you were in his place?”

7.  Self-worth is intrinsic

In  an  era  dominated  by  social  media  and  external  validation,  children  need 
to  hear  that  their  worth  does  not  come  from  appearance,  achievements,  or 
popularity. Parents should consistently affirm internal qualities—like kindness, 
integrity, and curiosity—over traits influenced by external approval. 

8.  You are in control of your body

Children  must  learn  early  that  their  body  belongs  to  them.  This  means 
respecting their right to express comfort or discomfort, set boundaries, and 
move  through  the  world  on  their  own  terms.  Promoting  bodily  autonomy

9.  Responsibility

Responsibility  is  the  bridge  between  childhood  and  adulthood.  Teaching 
children  to  be  accountable—for  their  actions,  routines,  and  commitment—
prepares  them  for  success  in  relationships,  academics,  and  the  workplace. 
Parents  can  promote  responsibility  through  daily  routines  and  chores,  age-
appropriate independence (for example, managing homework, brushing teeth 
without reminders), and natural consequences with consistent follow-through. 

10.  Treat yourself with kindness

Children  internalize  how  they  are  treated  by  others—especially  by  parents. 
When  caregivers  are  patient,  encouraging,  and  forgiving,  children  learn 
to  treat  themselves  in  the  same  way.  A  child  who  feels  chronically  criticized 
or  dismissed  may  struggle  with  self-doubt,  anxiety,  or  even  self-harm  in 
adolescence.  By  modeling  and  teaching  self-compassion,  parents  give  their 
children the tools to maintain emotional well-being through life’s challenges. 

In  a  world  full  of  noise,  the  most  powerful  lessons  parents  can  teach  their 
children  remain  timeless:  curiosity,  compassion,  resilience,  and  a  strong  sense 
of  self.  These  lessons  are  not  taught  all  at  once,  but  day  by  day,  through 
presence, modeling, and love.

Europe is heating up, and we’re not prepared for the toll this will take on our

Heat  exposure  poses  a  serious  health  threat  in  many  urban  areas  with  more 
than 3 million heat-related deaths globally between 2000 and 2019. Increasing 
vegetation  in  urban  areas  by  30%  could  have  prevented  1.16  million  of 
those heat-related deaths, according to a new study in The Lancet.

Europe and 527,000 lives in Asia from 2000 to 2019.

Even a 10% or 20% increase in vegetation could have prevented 27% or 32% of 
heat-related deaths, respectively.

Trees,  shrubs,  and  grasses  cool  urban  areas  by  providing  shade,  reflecting 
some  of  the  sun’s  heat,  and  reducing  heat  retention.  Through  the  process  of 
evapotranspiration, plants absorb heat from the air and use that energy to 
convert water into vapor. This lowers the local air temperature and enhances 
air movement. 

Urban  areas  are  typically  hotter  than  surrounding  regions  because  roads 
and  buildings  absorb  and  radiate  heat  from  the  sun.  Activities  such  as 
road  traffic,  air  conditioning,  and  industrial  and  energy  production  generate 
additional heat. This phenomenon is known as the urban-heat-island effect. 

The Lancet study is the first to estimate the effects of increasing green space on 
heat-related  deaths. The  study  examined  data  from  830  urban  areas  across  53 
countries. Increasing green space in urban regions of South and East Asia, as 
well as southern and eastern Europe, would result in the largest reductions 
in mortality.

Nearly  one  in  every  1,000  deaths  globally  has  been  due  to  heat  exposure. 
This could rise to one in six deaths by the 2090s if the global temperature 
exceeds  3  degrees  Celsius  (C).  Preserving  and  expanding  green  spaces  in 
urban areas is an essential strategy for cities to cope with rising temperatures, 
concluded the study. 

Cities in the global south have significantly less green space and, therefore, 
less “cooling  capacity”  than  cities  in  the  north,  according  to  a  recent  study 
published in Nature Communications. Low-income cities, as well as those in sub-
Saharan  Africa,  Latin  America,  and  Southeast  Asia,  are  particularly  at  risk  from 
extreme heat but often lack the green space needed to help them cope. 

Urban greenery can cool urban areas by an average of 3 degrees C during 
summers—a vital difference during a heatwave, the study found.

Greener  cities  bring  numerous  co-benefits,  including  reduced  air  pollution,  as
