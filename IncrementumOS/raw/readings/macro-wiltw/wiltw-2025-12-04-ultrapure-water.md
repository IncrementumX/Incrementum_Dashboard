Silver  is  entering  the  early  phase  of  a  structural  short  squeeze  driven  by 
physical scarcity rather than speculative excess. For decades, bullion banks 
arbitraged between long physical holdings in London and short futures 
on COMEX, profiting from carry trades supported by ample inventory. 

That  system  is  now  breaking  down  as  London’s  free  float  approaches 
depletion.  Accelerating  U.S.  industrial  demand,  tariff  pull-forward  buying, 
inflows,  potential  sovereign  accumulation,  and  sustained 
growing  ETF 
physical  withdrawals  by  China  and  India  are  draining  available  supply. 
With  silver  unable  to  be  leased  like  gold  and  upstream  production 
increasingly  pre-sold  to  China,  banks  are  rolling  futures  positions 
to  defer  delivery,  expanding  balance-sheet  risk.  Current  stress  reflects

volume of recycled silver beyond historical trends, there would be little available 
physical supply for new investors or consumers.

With  deflation  still  lingering,  the  one-year  benchmark  savings  rate  in  the 
mainland has fallen close to 1% in recent months. There has been a shortage 
of high-yielding savings products available in the mainland. Currently, five-year, 
large-denomination  certificates  of  deposit  (CDs),  which  yield  20%-30%  higher 
than  conventional  savings  products,  are  no  longer  available  on  the  mobile 
banking apps of all the six major state-owned banks. The three-year fixed deposit 
rate at major state-owned banks has generally fallen to 1.25%. 

Facing  these  unprecedentedly  low-interest-savings  accounts,  Chinese  savers 
had been migrating to products offering higher yields, i.e., wealth management 
products  (WMPs),  which  are  yielding  2%  on  average.  During  the  first  three 
quarters  of  this  year,  household  deposits  increased  by  12.73  trillion  yuan, 
while WMPs attracted 14 million new investors and 2.18 trillion yuan in net 
inflows, pushing their outstanding wealth to a record high of 33.2 trillion yuan as 
of the end of October. 

Nevertheless,  the  trend  appears  to  be  hitting  a  limit,  because  most  of  the 
WMPs offered by major banks are invested in fixed income, primarily CGBs. 
At the end of Q3 2025, the total outstanding invested in WMPs was reported at 
32.1 trillion yuan—an all-time high. Cash and bank deposits accounted for 27.5% 
and bonds accounted for 40.4%. 

In  recent  years,  a  bull  market  in  the  CGBs—a  result  of  declining  yields—
allowed  the  banks  to  book  gains  when  they  rolled  over  their  bond 
investments.  In  other  words,  banks  have  been  using  the  CGB  price  gains  to 
pump-up the performance of their WMPs. In a bull market for CGBs, the migration 
from  savings  (yielding  less  than  1%)  to  WMPs  (yielding  2%)  could  logically 
continue unabated. 

However, in a rising-yield environment, long positions in CGBs will have to 
be  rolled-over  at  a  loss,  which  will  make  the  job  of  managing  WMPs  very 
challenging,  and  their  performance  is  likely  to  disappoint.  Therefore,  a 
sustainable bear market in CGBs could bring the yield migration to WMPs to an 
end. 

As  we  have  often  pointed-out,  since  mid-July,  Beijing’s  anti-deflation 
campaign has driven the 10-year CGB yield higher by 22 bps in five months.

Antimony  is  produced  both  from  primary  antimony  deposits  and  as  a 
byproduct  of  other  metal  operations.  In  some  districts,  it  is  mined  directly

those instances, it is recovered from concentrates or smelter products generated 
for those host metals. This ties antimony supply to the production cycles of the lead 
and gold industries. 

Global mined production is around 100,000 tons per year. China and Russia 
are home to half of the world’s mineable reserves. Mined production in 2024 was 
dominated by China, Tajikistan, and Russia. Most of Tajikistan, Myanmar (Burma), 
and Australia’s antimony exports flow to China for processing. China refines 85% 
of the world's antimony.

Ultrapure water, a key component in 
semiconductor, pharmaceutical, and 
biotech manufacturing, is poised for 
exponential growth. We reiterate our buy 
recommendation on the 13D Water Index.
Since 2001, we have authored over 250 articles arguing that water scarcity will 
exacerbate.  Climate  change,  population  growth,  and  industrialization  are 
putting increasing pressure on global water systems, intensifying supply-chain 
issues  and  threatening  food  and  industrial  production.    While  agriculture  has 
been the primary water consumer, accounting for roughly 70% of total demand, 
industrial  demand  is  rising  rapidly.  Among  all  applications,  the  increasing  use 
of  ultrapure  water  (UPW)  in  advanced  technologies  has  emerged  as  a  strategic, 
mission-critical manufacturing input. 

UPW  is  the  highest  grade  of  purified  water.  To  make  UPW,  regular  water  is 
passed through several purification stages that remove virtually everything but 
the water molecules. First, pre-treatment (which can include filters and activated 
carbon)  removes  chlorine,  organics,  and  larger  particles.  Then,  membrane 
techniques,  such  as  reverse  osmosis,  remove  most  dissolved  salts  and  many 
other  impurities.  This  is  followed  by  deionization  procedures,  such  as  ion 
exchange  or  electrodeionization,  which  remove  the  last  traces  of  ions.  Finally, 
ultrafiltration  and  UV  light  “polish”  the  water  by  filtering  out  tiny  particles, 
bacteria, and trace organic compounds.

The  final  product  is  free  of  nearly  all  contaminants,  including  dissolved 
salts,  organic  substances,  particles,  microorganisms,  and  gases.  UPW 
standards  require  total-organic  carbon  levels  below  one  part-per-billion  and 
particle  counts  below  one  part-per-trillion.  This  level  of  purity  is  essential 
for  sensitive  applications,  where  even  trace  impurities  can  impact  processes 
or  results.  Additionally,  creating  UPW  is  highly  water-intensive.  It  requires 
approximately  1,500  gallons  of  piped  water  to  produce  1,000  gallons  of 
UPW.

  Semiconductor  manufacturing  is  among  the  most  water-intensive

manufacturing is accelerating in many parts of the world to diversify supply

most fabrication units, while the U.S. is funding chip manufacturing to reduce 
its  reliance  on  foreign  sources.  India  is  also  an  emerging  manufacturing 
hotspot for new fabrication units. This necessitates massive, greenfield capital 
expenditure in UPW infrastructure.

cutting-edge fabs, even as older plants continue to improve their efficiency.

The  ancients  were  comfortable  concluding  their  conversations  in  aporia. 
They  were  trained  to  sit  with  confusion  and  with  the  possibility  that  they

Intellectual  historian,  Dr.  Russell  Hvolbek,  examines  the  decline  of  this 
pedagogical  model  in  “Teaching  as  Destructive  of  Certainty.”  He  notes  that

Micro and nanoplastic particles are disrupting 
human and animal gut microbiomes with a 
range of health impacts. These tiny plastic 
particles also affect plant growth, reducing 
crop yields by up to 360 million tons a year. 
There is no precedent for continuous uptake of foreign particles into the 
human body that does not result in some adverse health effects. 
— Rolf Halden, Director, Environmental Health Engineering, Arizona State 
University.

Micro  and  nanoplastic  particles  and  fibers  are  everywhere.  They  are  found  in 
soil,  water,  air,  plants,  and  animals.  Every  person  likely  has  billions  of  micro 
and nanoplastic particles (MNPs) circulating in their blood or deposited in 
their organs. 

For  example,  we  previously  reported  a  groundbreaking  study  that  found 
an  average  of  5  grams  of  MNPs  in  the  brains  of  deceased  individuals  in  the 
U.S.  Those  with  dementia  had  up  to  50  grams  of  MNPs—
equivalent  to  a  golf  ball—in  their  brains.  (See  WILTW  March  13, 
2025.)

MNPs  are  found  in  an  enormous  variety  of  sizes,  shapes,  and  compositions. 
As  many  as  16,000  different  chemicals  are  used  in  more  than  100,000  plastic 
formulations. At least 4,200 plastic chemicals pose a hazard to human health 
and the environment, according to a recent study in the journal Nature.

Moreover, in the environment, MNPs act like tiny magnets, attracting other 
chemicals, including toxic compounds, as well as bacteria and viruses. (See 
WILTW April 4, 2024.)

The detection and idendtification of MNPs have been very challenging and have 
required the development of new scientific tools in recent years. Assessing the 
effects  of  MNPs  on  living  organisms,  including  humans,  has  been  even  more 
difficult.

MNPs  in  our  gastrointestinal  systems  are  altering  our  gut  microbiome, 
according to a new unpublished study by researchers at the Medical University 
of  Graz,  Austria.  (Note:  This  study  was  presented  at  the  United  European 
Gastroenterology conference in Berlin, and has not been peer-reviewed.) 

The  human  gut  microbiome  is  composed  of  around  4,500  different  types  of 
bacteria,  along  with  viruses  and  fungi. The  gut  microbiome  plays  a  major  role 
in digestion, synthesizing vitamins, training and regulating our immune system, 
and more. 

When the microbiome is out of balance, it can lead to wide-ranging health 
effects, including digestive problems, obesity, type 2 diabetes, and effects 
on our immune system and mental health.

The researchers at the Medical University of Graz grew gut microbiome cultures 
from healthy volunteers and exposed them to different MNPs at levels found in 
the  human  intestine.  Exposure  to  microplastics  made  the  gut  microbiome 
more acidic and caused shifts in bacterial composition. 

These  changes  in  bacterial  composition  mirrored  patterns  observed  in 
people  with  depression  and  colorectal  cancer.  It’s  worth  noting  that  rates 
of  both  conditions,  along  with  acid  reflux  (Gastroesophageal  reflux  disease  or 
GERD), have doubled since the 1990s. The largest relative increase occurred  in 
the 25 to 35-year-old age group.

However,  this  study  does  not  prove  there  is  a  link  between  MNPs  and  these 
conditions. We quote lead author Christian Pacher-Deutsch, researcher: 

The key takeaway is that microplastics do have an impact on our microbiome. 

The  changes  in  the  gut  microbiome  caused  by  MNPs  may  lead  to  various 
gastrointestinal  disorders,  systemic 
inflammation,  and  chronic  diseases, 
according to other studies. MNPs have also been associated with lung diseases,

The health of plants and animals is also being impacted by MNPs. 

Animal studies show disruptions in gut microbiomes, with increased pathogens 
and antibiotic-resistant bacteria. Additionally, some studies indicate that MNPs 
can  thin  intestinal  tissue,  leading  to  a “leaky  gut”  that  allows  bacteria  to  enter 
the bloodstream.

For  example,  MNPs  disrupting  the  gut  microbiomes  of  cattle  affect  digestion 
and  increase  stress-related  proteins,  according  to  the  first-ever  study  in  cattle. 
This  could  decrease  feed  efficiency,  slow  weight  gain,  or  reduce  milk 
production.

We  quote  lead  author,  Jana  Seifert,  a  microbiologist  at  the  University  of 
Hohenheim, Germany:

Our  study  shows  for  the  first  time  that  microplastics  do  not  simply  pass 
through the digestive tract of farm animals.

Seifert also noted that some of the MNPs in cattle digestive systems were broken 
down  into  even  smaller  particles,  increasing  their  ability  to  penetrate  tissues, 
enter the bloodstream, and accumulate in organs and muscle. 

Agricultural  soils  contain  about  23  times  more  MNPs  than  oceans  or 
waterways,  according  to  a  scientific  review  of  soil  samples  from  30  countries 
published  in  Environmental  Sciences  Europe.  Fields  near  industrialized  areas 
contained more than 200,000 plastic particles per kilogram of soil. 

“Microplastics  are  turning  food-producing  land  into  a  plastic  sink,”  said 
lead  author  Joseph  Boctor,  a  PhD  candidate  at  Murdoch  University  in Western 
Australia.

The agricultural industry itself uses a great deal of plastic, such as silage wraps, 
polymer-coated slow-release fertilizer, in biosolids or sewage sludge, packaging, 
or from tire wear. 

MNPs in soils enter plants through cracks in root tissue or through root cell walls. 
MNPs in the air are deposited on leaves, which can enter through plant pores.
