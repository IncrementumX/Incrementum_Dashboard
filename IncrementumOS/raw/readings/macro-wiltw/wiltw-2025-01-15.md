Beijing’s removal of VAT export rebates is 
equivalent to launching the second wave of its 
anti-deflation campaign. This is corroborated 
by the Bloomberg Commodity Index’s breakout 
of its 17.5-year downward trendline. China is 
resorting to “capacity nationalism” to fight 
back against resource nationalism and tariff 
wars, reinforcing global inflationary pressures.
If resource nationalism, which has been on a secular rise worldwide, can be justified 
for  resource-rich  countries,  then  “capacity  nationalism”  can  be  justified  for  China. 
Resource nationalism entails using resources as a weapon to bargain for better 
terms in a trade relationship. Tariffs can be thought of in this way. And capacity 
nationalism entails using manufacturing capacity as a weapon to bargain for better 
terms. Every country wants better terms in their trade relationships, which 
points to more conflict and higher prices.

Until  recently,  capacity  was  a  largely-untapped  bargaining  power  that  Beijing 
has  been  very  careful  to  utilize.  Because  once  it  starts  to  use  capacity  as  a 
weapon,  it  could  backfire  by  causing  other  countries  to  establish  their  own 
capacity  and  reducing  their  dependence  on  China.  However,  with  resource 
nationalism  (pressure  from  upstream)  and  tariff  wars  (pressure  from 
downstream) on a secular rise worldwide, and with many Western countries 
already  adopting  a  “China  plus  one”  supply-chain  strategy,  there  is  now 
little concern for Beijing using capacity as a key bargaining chip. 

Now  that  Beijing  is  convinced  about  the  effectiveness  of  using  its  leverage 
over capacity, as can be seen in the cases of silver, rare earths, and aluminum, 
this  new  “capacity  nationalism”  playbook  implies  a  virtuous  circle  for 
Chinese  companies:  if  more-constrained  utilization  rates  of  capacity  can 
lead  to  higher  profit  margins,  as  can  be  seen  in  the  case  of  rare  earths 
and  aluminum,  then  it  will  use  more  capacity  leverage  in  the  future.

The  implications  sound  ominous  for  consumers  in  the  West,  who  are  already 
suffering from sustained inflation in goods prices. 

Last  Friday,  after  the  market  closed,  China’s  Ministry  of  Finance  (MOF) 
announced that it will eliminate value-added tax (VAT) export rebates for 
solar products beginning April 1, 2026. VAT export rebates for solar products 
will be fully removed from April 1st. For battery products, the export rebate rate 
will be cut from 9% to 6% between April 1 and December 31, 2026, before being 
fully eliminated on January 1, 2027. 

This marks the second major adjustment to China’s export rebate system in 
just one year. The previous round was announced on November 15, 2024, and 
implemented beginning December 1, 2024. Export rebates for solar equipment, 
batteries,  and  selected  non-metallic  mineral  products  were  reduced  from  13% 
to 9% at that time.

China’s latest move is set to lift export costs for solar and battery exporters. 
Over  the  long  term,  the  rollback  of  export-tax  incentives  is  set  to  reinforce 
Beijing’s broader industrial policy objectives, encouraging consolidation, and a 
shift toward higher-value manufacturing. This indicates that Beijing is determined 
to  continue  its  course  on  the  anti-deflation  campaign  and  is  now  very  confident 
on China’s competitiveness in such sectors, and is not afraid of losing customers if 
prices move higher.

This also suggests that prices for most renewable-energy items have made 
a second low, after their first bottom last July, following President Xi’s anti-
deflation campaign conference meeting. 

On  Monday,  lithium-carbonate  futures  traded  in  Guangzhou  opened  at 
daily limit (up 9%) and remained at that level throughout the session. And 
they gained another 7.4% on Tuesday.

The mounting fundamental and technical evidence suggests that copper’s price 
gains  are  likely  to  be  durable.  They  constitute  a  strong  market  signal  that 
new  supply  has  to  be  brought  online,  or  demand  seriously  curtailed,  in 
order to balance the market. The problem we see is that new  supply cannot 
be  brought  on  fast  enough  or  cheaply  enough,  while  global  economic  and 
technology  trends  will  require  huge  volumes  of  incremental  supply.  In  our 
view,  accessing  copper  is  increasingly  going  to  become  a 
matter of national security and governments ultimately will 
pay any price necessary to get their hands on the metal.

Last  week,  S&P  Global  Energy  &  Market  Intelligence  published  a  major  new 
study that adds to the mountain of evidence cited earlier, titled “Copper in the 
Age of AI: Challenges of Electrification.” Some of the conclusions are outlined 
below.

Primary  copper  supply  is  expected  to  peak  in  2030  and  a  10  million 
tonne  supply/demand  gap  will  open-up  by  2040.  Global  copper  demand  is 
projected to rise from 28 million tonnes currently to 42 million tonnes by 2040. 
Primary  (mine)  supply  is  expected  to  increase  initially,  from  23  to  27  million 
tonnes by its 2030 peak, but then decline to 22 million tonnes by 2040. Even 
if  secondary  (recycled)  supply  increases  by  6  million  tonnes  by  2040—which 
appears optimistic, in our view—it would still leave the world with a 10 million-
tonne shortfall by 2040.

According  to  S&P  Global:  “To  close  the  gap,  a  concerted  effort—along  with 
the  proper  policy,  technology  enablers,  and  investment—will  be  needed 
to increase primary mined supply from 23 million metric tons in 2025 to at 
least 32 million metric tons by 2040.”

The potential inroads from copper substitutes are less of a risk than feared, 
because  there  are  critical  underlying  limitations  for  alternate  materials 
(especially aluminum) that are not readily avoidable. As S&P notes: “Copper 
is unique for its exceptional electrical and thermal conductivity, making it vital 
for power systems and electronics. It resists corrosion, has natural antimicrobial 
properties,  and  is  infinitely  recyclable  without  losing  quality,  supporting 
sustainability.  This  rare  combination  of  efficiency,  durability,  hygiene,  and 
recyclability  sets  copper  apart  from  most  other  metals.  Aluminum  has  only 
60%  the  conductivity  of  copper,  which  means  wires  must  be  thicker  to 
move  the  same  amount  of  power,  and,  as  a  poorer  conductor  of  heat,  it 
sometimes requires more insulation.”

There  will  inevitably  be  some  substitution  and  thrifting  where  it  is  feasible 
and safe to do so. However, users have already done a considerable amount of 
cutting-back  already,  implying  that  the  low-hanging  fruit  has  largely  been 
picked. And for some applications, substitution is not really an option at all, as 
S&P explains:

For  some  end  uses,  such  as  data  centers,  substitution  options  are 
minimal.  While  aluminum  can  be  substituted  in  high-voltage  delivery 
related  to  utility-scale  transmission,  within  high-density  AI  cluster  facilities 
copper is described as “non-negotiable” due to space constraints and thermal 
conductivity.  Aluminum  cables  require  larger  cross  sections,  which  may 
impede airflow in dense server racks. With the move toward liquid cooling 
and  cold  plates  as  demand  for  more  efficient  cooling  grows,  copper’s 
thermal  properties  make  it  superior  to  aluminum  in  data-center  use, 
even if the aluminum is cheaper...

We have frequently noted the decline of ore-grades over the past 30 years 
across  the  mining  space,  especially  copper.  (See  WILTW  December  5,  2024.) 
The author and strategist, Shanaka Anslem Perera, wrote an insightful article on 
Substack  dated  January  9th with  the  title,  “The  Red  Metal  Singularity—Why 
Every Institutional Model Calibrated to Copper Surplus Is About to Break,”

The  average  copper  ore  grade  at  major  mines  has  declined  from  1.4

Policing or Piracy: will the next war start 
on the High Seas?
This  week  the  UK  joined  the  U.S.  in  undermining  established  maritime  law 
by  claiming  the  right  to  intercept  ships  on  the  high  seas  in  order  to  enforce 
extraterritorial national laws such as sanctions. The U.S. has now intercepted five 
oil tankers it claims were violating U.S. law, and this week the UK has asserted 
that it too has the right to intercept ships on the high seas, especially those it 
considers part of the “shadow fleet” evading U.S. and European sanctions. 
Other European nations including the Baltics are looking at this precedent 
to  begin  a  possible  blockade  of  Russian  passage  through  the  Baltic  and 
North  Seas.  With  more  and  more  tankers  registering  under  the  Russian  flag, 
and Moscow extending formal protection over them, what are the implications 
and dangers — and how does China fit in?

By the 1600s, with the discovery of the “New World” and development of ships 
capable  of  crossing  vast  open  oceans,  the  high  seas  had  become  vital  trade 
routes. Two primary schools of thought developed, that the seas did not belong 
to  anyone  and  raw  power  prevailed,  or  that  the  seas  were  a “commons”  that 
belonged  to  everyone  because  they  were  so  essential.  Freedom  of  the  seas 
gained  wide  acceptance,  especially  by  the  dominant  European  nations  due  to 
colonialism  and  imperial  expansion  that  required  safe  trade  routes.  Protecting 
these routes became a difficult task for the major powers, and over time a set of 
rules evolved. 

With  the  creation  of  the  United  Nations,  the  concept  of  res  communis,  or 
that the high seas were a shared commons, had largely prevailed. Modern 
international  law  is  fairly  clear:  states  have  no  right  to  intercept  a 
ship  validly  registered  under  a  foreign  flag  outside  their 
territorial  waters  (typically  up  to  twelve  miles)  unless  it  is 
engaged  in  piracy,  illegally  broadcasting,  is  not  flying  a 
flag, or if the interception is under a United Nations Security 
Council  (UNSC)  resolution.  To  intercept  a  foreign  vessel  outside 
these  narrow  bounds  is  considered  an  act  of  war  if  done  by  a  state,  or 
an  act  of  piracy  if  done  by  a  non-state  actor.  For  more  legal  details  on  this 
complex topic see The Interception of Vessels on the High Seas, by Efthymios 
Papastavridis.

Central to Haidt’s work is his Moral Foundations Theory (MFT), which proposes 
that  “moral  systems  are  interlocking  sets  of  values,  virtues,  norms, 
practices, identities, institutions, technologies, and evolved psychological 
mechanisms that work together to suppress or regulate selfishness and make 
cooperative social life possible.” According to this framework, morality rests on 
five core foundations: Care, Fairness, Loyalty, Authority, and Purity. But what 
do these principles actually signify, and how do they manifest in everyday life? 

MFT  begins  from  the  premise  that  human  societies  confront  recurring 
social  problems,  and  that  moral  values  emerge  as  adaptive  responses  to 
those problems. When we witness the suffering of others, we cultivate virtues 
of compassion and protection (Care). When we observe exploitation or injustice, 
we  develop  norms  of  reciprocity  and  equal  treatment  (Fairness).  Authority 
arises  from  the  need  for  social  order  within  families,  schools,  and  institutions. 
Purity  reflects  concerns  about  bodily  integrity,  self-discipline,  and  spiritual 
meaning.  Loyalty  binds  individuals  to  groups,  creating  cohesion  and  mutual 
obligation. 

These  moral  foundations,  Haidt  argues,  are  not  primarily  taught  through 
rational instruction but are rooted in human evolution. Moral intuitions—those 
immediate gut reactions that tell us stealing is wrong or cruelty is unacceptable 
without  ever  having  to  learn  them—are  not  arbitrary  feelings.  They  are 
inherited  psychological  mechanisms  shaped  over  generations  to  regulate 
selfishness and enable cooperation. 

For  instance,  Care  evolved  from  the  necessity  of  protecting  vulnerable  kin. 
Fairness  emerged  from  the  advantages  of  reciprocity,  such  as  sharing  food 
and  honoring  agreements.  Loyalty  supported  group  survival  in  competitive 
for 
environments.  Authority  established 
coordination,  while  Purity  helped  early  humans  avoid  disease  and  preserve 
bodily  health.  Together,  these  moral  foundations  form  the  psychological 
architecture  that  makes  complex  social  life  possible.  Without  them,  trust 
collapses and cooperation gives way to chaos. 

stable  hierarchies  necessary 

MFT has been especially influential because it challenges the idea that morality 
is  the  product  of  conscious  and  rational  reasoning.  Instead,  it  emphasizes 
intuitionism:  the  idea  that  we  feel  our  moral  judgments  first  and  justify 
them  later.  This  insight  helps  explain  why  moral  disagreements—especially 
political ones—are so emotionally charged and resistant to rational persuasion.

Haidt  and  his  collaborators  have  used  MFT  to  explain  political  polarization  by 
showing  that  different  groups  tend  to  prioritize  different  moral  foundations. 
Liberals  often  emphasize  Care  and  Fairness,  while  conservatives  place  greater 
weight on Loyalty, Authority, and Purity. These differences do not reflect the 
presence or absence of morality, but divergent moral emphases rooted in 
shared psychological foundations. 

Crucially,  Haidt  has  also  applied  MFT  to  the  growing  teen  mental-health 
crisis.  His  research  on  smartphones  and  social  media  shows  that  digital 
environments  disrupt  emotional  regulation,  attention,  sleep,  and  social 
development,  especially  during  adolescence,  when  the  brain  is  most 
plastic. But through the lens of MFT, the damage runs deeper than mental-health 
symptoms alone. 
Phones  displace  the  real-world  interactions  through  which 
moral  foundations  are  formed.  When  teens  spend  their  formative 
years  mediated  by  screens,  they  lose  repeated  opportunities  to  encounter 
suffering firsthand, resolve conflicts face-to-face, submit to legitimate authority, 
and participate in stable communities. The evolutionary conditions that once 
shaped moral intuition are no longer reliably present. 

As  a  result,  virtues  like  Care  become  abstract.  A  phone  cannot  replicate  the 
embodied  empathy  that  emerges  when  one  person  helps  another  in 
distress.  Fairness  becomes  distorted  in  online  spaces  governed  by  virality 
rather  than  justice.  Loyalty  is  replaced  by  fleeting  digital  groups  that  demand 
conformity without offering genuine belonging. Authority becomes incoherent 
in environments defined by anonymity, deception, and performative status. 

Haidt  also  notes  that  this  erosion  affects  boys  and  girls  differently.  Girls,  who 
tend  to  be  more  attuned  to  socially-oriented  foundations  like  Care  and

comparison  and  moral  outrage.  Online  spaces  often  simulate  compassion 
while  undermining  it,  rewarding  performative  empathy  rather  than  genuine 
care. The result is heightened anxiety, relational instability, and a fragile sense of 
self-worth. 

Boys  face  parallel  losses.  Loyalty  and  Authority  weaken  when  real-world 
challenges, hierarchies, and responsibilities are replaced by digital simulations. 
Without  opportunities  to  earn  respect,  test 
limits,  and  belong  to 
meaningful groups, many boys drift into disengagement, aggression, and 
despair.

Purity,  too,  is  quietly  undermined.  In  MFT,  Purity  is  not  merely  about

Where does all this heat come from? One ton of CO2 emitted by burning fossil

systems  and  other  advanced  computing  systems  could  soon  bypass 
traditional RSA encryption (see WILTW January 9, 2025). 

Major  internet  and  cloud  players  have  begun  rolling  out  hybrid  key-
exchange  mechanisms  that  combine  classical  algorithms  with  America's 
NIST-approved  post-quantum  cryptography,  enabling  a  gradual  migration 
to  quantum-resistant  systems  while  maintaining  compatibility  with  current 
systems.  Quantum  key  distribution  (QKD)  technology  has  also  begun  to  be 
deployed,  securing  financial  transactions,  video  links,  and  space-to-ground 
channels.
