Why are oil prices up 12% since the end 
of April, despite persistent bearish news? 
Are markets beginning to price in the 
challenges to future supply or the fading 
threat of renewables? The next major move 
higher in oil is a matter of when, not if. 
Over 20 years ago, as we were developing our bullish oil thesis, we highlighted 
the fact that the U.S. doubled-down on gas-guzzling vehicles during the 1980s 
and 1990s (see chart below), despite the massive effort to increase the average 
fuel  economy  of  the  existing  fleet  after  the  oil  crises  of  the  1970s.  (See WILTW 
April 7, 2005.)

But then, as global excess oil capacity grew following the recessions of the 
early-1980s and the subsequent collapse of the Soviet Union, oil prices fell 
sharply,  and  the  urgency  to  reduce  U.S.  oil  consumption  waned.  By  2005, 
U.S. net petroleum imports exceeded 12 million bpd, while the North Sea and 
Mexico’s Cantarell oilfield went into decline, leading to higher oil prices.

In the short term, the EIA is forecasting a market surplus, with estimated growth 
in global petroleum production of 1.8 million bpd in 2025 and 1.1 million bpd 
in 2026 (including higher OPEC volumes), offset by consumption growth of just 
0.8 million bpd in 2025 and 1.1 million bpd in 2026. The global stock-build is 
projected to exceed 1 million bpd in 2025 and 2026. 

But this begs the question: given this bearish market imbalance, why are oil prices 
moving higher from their April lows? We love anomalies such as this one, because 
they  often  lead  us  closer  to  the  truth.  The  projected  growth  in  inventories 
underscores our point that a lot of bearishness is already priced-into the oil 
market, which makes the current increase intriguing.

Capital  discipline  in  the  oil  patch  continues  to  improve.  For  instance, 
Chevron  management  recently  stated  that  it  is  going  to  cut  back  on  rigs  and 
fracking  crews  in  the  Permian  Basin,  aiming  to  keep  production  steady  at  1 
million  bpd  for  an  extended  period.  With  new  wells  mostly  offsetting  natural 
declines, Chevron expects it will generate $5 billion in annual free cash flow 
by 2027. 

Bruce  Niemeyer,  who  heads  Chevron’s  shale  operations,  explained:  “We’re 
going  from  growth  to  cash  generation.  We’re  already  in  the  earliest  phases 
of  that.  We’re  making  adjustments  to  rigs  and  the  frack  spreads  which  will 
reduce the amount of capital we’re spending on an annual basis.” This is an 
unmistakable sign of maturity of the most prolific source of U.S. oil production over 
the past decade.

In our June 19th issue, we highlighted the annual Dallas Fed survey, published 
last spring, which noted that regional oil companies need a $65 oil price to 
drill  profitably.  Subsequently,  in  its  second-quarter  survey  of  oil  companies, 
the Dallas Fed asked regional E&P companies how drilling plans have changed 
since the start of the year. While 34% expected no change, some 47% expected 
drilling  to  decrease  slightly  or  significantly,  more  than  double  the  19% 
saying drilling might increase slightly or significantly. (See charts below.)

The Dallas Fed also asked about the impact of Washington’s tariffs on the cost 
of drilling wells, and 53% of respondents said the impact would exceed 4%.

have broken-out of a 17-year downtrend line 
which began at its all-time peak in 2007. 
Beijing has just fired the first round of a 
massive anti-price war campaign and started 
the second round of supply-side reform

13D China Anti-deflation Portfolio. Part two. 
China has been the world’s single largest source of deflation over the past three-
plus years, ever since the Russia/Ukraine war broke out in early 2022. And now, 
Beijing  is  determined  to  end  deflation  as  soon  as  possible.  The  latest  “anti-
price war” effort is effectively an “anti-deflation” battle, which is the same as 
“reflation.” 

Over  the  weekend,  Premier  Li  Qiang  announced  that  China  will  start 
construction  of  the  1.2  trillion-yuan  ($170  billion)  Tibet  hydropower 
project,  which  is  claimed  to  be  the  single-largest-infrastructure  project 
ever built globally. Known as “the project of the century,” the hydropower dam 
on the Yarlung Tsangpo River was first announced in 2020 as part of China’s 14th 
Five-year Plan, and will take at least 10 years to complete. With about 300 million 
MWh, this hydropower project in Tibet is more than three times the size of the Three 
Gorges Dam (88 million MWh). 

The Tibet hydropower project is likely to be just the tip-of-the-iceberg for 
additional  mega-projects  to  be  announced  by  Beijing.  The  Fifteenth  Five-
year  Plan  (covering  2026  to  2030)  is  still  being  drafted  and  more  such  mega-
projects are likely to be announced between now and next March, a few months 
after approval by the CPC Central Committee this fall.

Compared to their counterparts in the West, the Chinese central government 
is  significantly  under-leveraged  and  has  plenty  of  room  to  re-leverage 
to  spend  its  way  out  of  deflation.  Besides  the  Tibet  hydropower  projects, 
other  mega  projects  include:  South-to-North Water  Diversion  Project; West-to-
East  Power  Transmission;  Desert,  Gobi,  and  Wilderness  New  Energy  Base;  and 
Xinjiang-Tibet Grand Corridor. These are all in the early planning stages and are 
not  likely  to  be  announced  until  next  March’s  Congress  Meeting.  But  to  those 
who  invest  in  commodities,  the  existence  of  such  mega-project  plans  is  a  key 
consideration before entering any significant short positions. 

Beijing’s  latest  anti-deflation  moves  have  ignited  animal  spirits  in  the 
mainland. This is very similar to the U.S. building the Hoover Dam during 
the Great Depression and will accelerate the end of deflation. 

The  speculators  who  have  taken  large  short  positions  on  some  of  the  China-
dominant  commodities  are  now  being  squeezed  in  the  mainland’s  exchanges 
and are incurring huge losses. Polysilicon  (+9%)  and  silicon  metal  (+7%)  futures 
traded  in  Guangzhou  closed  at  daily  up  limits  again  yesterday. The  squeeze  is 
spreading  to  other  commodities,  as  well.  Prices  of  coking  coal  (mainly  used 
in  iron  and  steel  production) futures reached daily limits both Monday and 
Tuesday. Coking coal futures rose another 11% on Wednesday. 

Meanwhile, there are signs that the 53-month-long bull market in CGBs, which 
is the longest Chinese-bond bull market over the past half century, is coming to 
an end. Since President Xi’s anti-deflation conference held in early July, the 
10-year CGB yields have increased from 1.656% to 1.7551%, an increase of 
10 bps. 

If we are proven correct on the bottoming of CGB yields, it would also suggest 
a significant market bottom in commodities and Chinese stocks is already 
behind  us.  Daily  turnover  of  A-shares  reached  1.93  trillion  yuan  on  Tuesday, 
which is the highest daily turnover in more than four months. 

More  importantly,  the  CSI  Upstream  Index  (SH000961  Index,  CNY  3,570.93), 
which tracks a group of listed commodity producers situated upstream of their 
respective  sectors,  has  broken  out  of  a  17-year  downtrend  line  since  its 
all-time  peak  established  in  the  fourth  quarter  of  2007.  In  essence,  Chinese 
commodity-driven  stocks  have  sensed  reflation  forces  from  Beijing  and  are  now

Accelerating uranium demand intersects 
with shrinking supply, setting the stage for 
the structural-supply deficit to worsen in 
the near term. What are the implications? 
How to invest.
Since  2018,  we  have  argued  that  uranium  is  transitioning  from  more  than  a 
decade-long  bear  market  into  a  super-cycle  bull  market.  The  uranium  market 
is  driven  by  a  severe  structural-supply  deficit  and  a  global  push  toward 
decarbonization  and  improved  energy  security  (see  reports).  Bolstered  by 
uranium's  inelastic  demand,  investments  in  the  uranium  market  provide 
insulation  against  macroeconomic  volatility  and  substitution  risks.  Ultimately, 
the  only  way  to  close  the  supply/demand  gap  will  be  higher  uranium  prices 
to incentivize miners to invest in new capacity. The uranium market is tight and 
poised to outperform as fundamentals continue to brighten. 

Consider the following:

  Long-term contract volume by utilities to secure uranium remains below 
the replacement rate. Utilities have been consistently purchasing uranium 
at  levels  below  their  annual  replacement  rate,  steadily  drawing  down  their 
inventories  for  a  decade.  Last  year,  primary-uranium  production  by  miners 
totaled  roughly  150  million  pounds  (Mlbs),  which  may  inch  up  to  160Mlbs 
this year. This is still well below the annual replacement rate of 190-200Mlbs for 
the existing global-reactor fleet of 440 reactors. 

Year-to-date, utilities have signed long-term contracts for less than one-
fifth of global annual demand. Procurement delays amid policy uncertainty 
and  price  volatility  contributed  to  a  low  contracting  rate  in  the  first  half  of 
2025. However, the combination of rising uncovered requirements between 2028 
and 2035, coupled with increasing geopolitical risk, current low-inventory levels, 
and contracting lead times falling to just 2.6 years, points to pent-up demand, 
notes a recent analysis by Sprott.

Defined-benefit  pension  schemes  guarantee  a  retiree  a  certain  amount  of 
capital. Under the defined-contribution pension scheme, the amount the retiree 
receives  depends  on  investment  returns.  Each  member’s  pension  contribution 
is  allocated  to  their  individual  pension  pot,  allowing  workers  to  adjust  their 
exposure to risk assets to plan for their retirement.

IORPs  allocate  nearly  40%  of  their  assets  to  investment  funds.  Of  this,  33%  is

While  we  have  examined  in  these  pages  the  dangers  of  excessive  screen  time 
for  children,  a  growing  body  of  research  suggests  that  devices  are  not  just 
harming kids’ attention spans—they are fundamentally altering how children 
and  teens  communicate  with  their  parents.  The  constant “electric  noise”  of 
screens is displacing human voices in the home, while social media has birthed 
a  new,  rapidly-evolving  vocabulary  that  many  parents  and  educators  struggle 
to  understand.  The  long-term  implications  of  these  shifts  are  only  beginning  to

A  recent  study  published  in  JAMA  Pediatrics,  led  by  researcher  Mary  Brushe, 
explored  the  importance  of  raising  infants  in  language-rich  environments—

digital  devices.  As  one  commentator  on  the  study  noted,  “technology-based 
interference, dubbed ‘technoference,’ wedges itself in between parents and 
children hungry for changes to listen and learn.” 

The  consequences  were  severe:  higher-screen  exposure  correlated  with 
language  delays  and  speech-development  disorders.  Even  in  less-extreme

The economics of battery-powered vans, light and medium-duty trucks without 
subsidies are rapidly improving and will approach or surpass those of diesel by 
2030 in many countries, according to BNEF. This will also be the case for heavy-
duty and long-haul operations in some regions. 

Electrification  of  road  transport,  including  two  and  three-wheel  vehicles, 
displaced  two  million  barrels  of  oil  a  day  by  2024.  More  than  five  million 
barrels per day will be displaced by 2030. 

Source: BloombergNEF

Moreover, the pace of oil displacement is accelerating as the adoption of electric 
vehicles  continues  to  increase.  It  took  six  years  of  e-vehicle  sales  between 
2018  and  2024  to  displace  one  million  barrels  of  oil  per  day  annually.  By 
2029,  one  year  of  e-vehicle  sales  will  be  enough,  according  to  forecasts  by 
BNEF.

Seeing the signals through the noise from 
events in Europe last week.
The  week  of  July  7th  saw  a  flurry  of  news  that  when  examined  in  detail  are 
further  symptoms  of  the  trends  we  have  been  watching,  specifically  the 
centrality  of  logistics  in  military  affairs,  the  decline  of  the  Western  military 
deterrent,  the  growing  dangers  of  a  new  nuclear-arms  race,  and  the  coming 
collapse of Ukraine. 

On  July  14th,  with  great  fanfare  President  Trump  announced  major  policy 
changes with respect to Ukraine. This came in response to his frustration that the 
Russian President was "not serious" about ending the war. The announcement 
was  touted  by  allies  such  as  Sen.  Lindsay  Graham  as  a  harsh  response  to 
Putin, consisting of two major elements: a threat of 100% tariffs on third parties 
doing business with Russia if it does not agree to a peace plan within 50 days, 
and a plan to "speed" advanced weapons to Ukraine. The details of the tariffs, the 
threat of which was met with derision in Moscow, have yet to be defined and are 
not expected to take effect until September 1st in any event. The weapons plan 
is  somewhat  better  defined,  but  has  been  met  with  skepticism  in  Europe 
and so far does not seem like it will result in any major new weapons being 
shipped to Ukraine in the near future.

The  Trump  plan  to  arm  Ukraine  consists  of  promising  to  expedite 
replacements  of  any  U.S.  weapons  shipped  to  Ukraine  by  NATO  allies. 
However,  there  is  a  catch  –  Europe  must  pay  for  these  replacements.  This 
flies in the face of the stated objectives of the recent EU initiatives to improve 
domestic military production. In addition, the Trump plan isn’t really a promise 
of  support  for  Ukraine.  As  the  EU  foreign  policy  chief  Kaja  Kallas  said, “If  you 
promise to give the weapons then say that somebody else is going to pay for it, it's 
not really given by you, is it?" 

Other European leaders were also skeptical. French officials said they would 
be  focusing  on  buying  arms  for  Ukraine  produced  domestically,  with  Italian 
and  Czech  leaders  having  a  similar  response.  The  Polish  Foreign  Minister  was 
more  positive  but  said  the  money  should  come  from  frozen  Russian  assets  – 
something most European leaders are reluctant to pursue due to the legal and 
economic implications.

Only  Germany  is  enthusiastically  supporting  the  Trump  plan,  however,  the 
critical problem remains that of production. Germany has promised to send 
five Patriot batteries, each consisting of an average of 6 launchers (a total of 35 
launchers), a radar, and control unit. However, it takes from 12 to 24 months to 
build a single battery. Only by “re-prioritizing” deliveries and diverting systems 
promised  to  other  users  such  as  Switzerland  will  the  German  systems  be  able 
to  be  replaced.  In  addition,  Russia  has  probably  destroyed  at  least  40  of  the 
72 Patriot missile launchers already delivered, along with ten radars – in other 
words,  the  equivalent  of  the  promised  five  batteries.  As  we  have  reported, 
Russia  is  destroying  these  expensive  assets  (a  battery  costs 
between $1.5 and $2 Billion) faster than they can be replaced, 
by targeting them using cheaper mass produced drones and 
short-range ballistic missiles. 

Therefore  it  is  unlikely  the  Trump  plan  will  result  in  a  significant  increase  in 
arms  for  Ukraine.  No  matter  who  pays  for  it,  Western  weapons 
production simply isn’t able to match the attrition rate. While 
they may prolong the war, these additional arms, sanctions, and tariff threats are 
unlikely to change the outcome, and will likely only result in Russia demanding 
harsher terms in the end.

The  decline  in  the  conventional  military  power  of  the  West  is  forcing  a 
reconsideration of the role of tactical-nuclear weapons, with new deployments 
reversing the post Cold War trends and making confrontations with Russia and 
China  potentially  more  dangerous.  The  U.S.  deployed  a  number  of  B61-12 
nuclear  bombs  to  RAF  Lakenheath  last  week,  marking  the  return  of  U.S. 
nuclear weapons to British soil after an absence of nearly twenty years. This 
move is seen by Russia as a significant escalation, adding to the tactical nuclear 
weapons  the  U.S.  already  has  based  in  Belgium,  Netherlands,  Germany,  Italy,  and 
Turkey. While often portrayed as a response to Russian actions in Eastern Europe, 
in  fact  plans  for  the  rehabilitation  of  these  Cold War-era  storage  sites,  and  the 
design  and  manufacture  of  several  new  classes  of  tactical  nuclear  weapon  to 
stage there, began well before the Russian invasion of Ukraine.

The reopening of the Lakenheath facility is controversial in the UK for a number 
of  reasons,  especially  over  the  issue  of  control.  At  present  these  weapons 
are  solely  for  use  by  the  U.S.  Great  Britain  has  had  an  independent  nuclear

All of this is further bad news for Ukraine, where external pressures and internal

to supply its weapons needs, and Russia is not deterred by conventional Western 
military capacity, a capacity the years of war in Ukraine have depleted. Rhetoric 
aside,  it  remains  unlikely  that  even  the  most  rabid  anti-Russian  elements 
in Europe would risk nuclear war over Ukraine – and it is almost certain the 
U.S. would not trade a major American city for Kiev.
