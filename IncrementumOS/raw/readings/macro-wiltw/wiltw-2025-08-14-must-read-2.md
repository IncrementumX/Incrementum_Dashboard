Making  matters  worse,  this  morning’s  wholesale  inflation  report  showed  that 
the  headline  July  PPI  rose  3.3%  y/y,  up  from  2.4%  in  June—yet  another 
indication  that  inflation  pressures  are  making  their  way  through  the  domestic 
supply chain.

As we wrote in WILTW August 7, 2025, after the weak payroll report two weeks 
ago, the Fed now has a brief window to cut the Fed Funds rate next month, 
before  the  latest  round  of  tariffs  begins  to  hit  product  shelves  in  Q4. 
Financial markets appear to confirm this view, with the DXY breaking below the 
98 level on Tuesday, in anticipation of lower short-term interest rates. However, it is 
hugely noteworthy that 10-year bond yields, which bottomed-out at 4.18% 
shortly after the weak payroll report, have been fluctuating at higher levels 
between 4.2% and 4.3%. 

According  to  Bianco  Research  founder  and  CEO,  Jim  Bianco,  during  the  last 
40  years,  there  was  only  one  time  that  the  Fed  cut  rates  when  the  core 
CPI was above 3% and the three-month change was above 0.3%, and that 
was during the recession that began in 4Q-1990. This was shortly after Iraq’s 
invasion of Kuwait caused oil prices to double and triggered rising uncertainty 
and corporate layoffs, all compounded by a commercial-real estate crunch. This 
begs  the  same  question  we  asked  last  week:  could the economy be in recession 
now? We will not know the answer until after the fact.

Interestingly,  during  a  Bloomberg  interview  yesterday, Treasury  Secretary  Scott 
Bessent  went  a  few  steps  further  when  he  said:  “There’s a very good  chance 
of a 50 basis point rate cut. We could go into a series of rate cuts here, starting 
with a 50 basis point rate cut in September.” He also said that, “if you look at any 
model” rates “should probably be 150, 175 basis points lower.”

Bloomberg’s  John  Authers  commented  this  morning,  as  follows:  “With  the 
current  effective  Fed  Funds  rate  at  4.33%,  he  is  suggesting  that  it  should  be 
about  2.6%.  Over  the  last  70  years,  the  rate  has  never  been  that  low  with 
inflation as high as it currently (with the core reading above 3%).” It is worth 
adding that the Taylor Rule points to a Fed Funds target over 150 basis points above 
the current level.

If  the  Fed  cuts  rates  with  inflation  at  current  levels,  one  could  reasonably 
argue  that  the  2%  inflation  target  is  finally  dead.  As  we  wrote  in  WILTW  May 
16, 2024, underlying trends “point to the coming end of the Fed’s arbitrary 
2%  inflation  target.”  In  August  1987,  when  Paul Volcker  retired  as  Fed  Chair, 
the PCE inflation rate was 3.4% y/y and the CPI rate was 4.3% y/y. (These levels 
were considered benign at the time, and the 2% target only became a publicly-
stated goal after the GFC.) We added then: “If we are entering an inflationary 
regime—which  we  think  we  are—then  inflation  rates  closer  to  3%-4% 
could eventually become the new benchmark.”

Making  matters  worse  is  the  receding  inflow  of  capital  to  support  dollar-
denominated assets. As we wrote in WILTW April 24, 2025, “foreign accumulation 
of  U.S.  ‘portfolio  assets,’  mainly  stocks  and  bonds,  has  more  than  tripled 
over the past 15 years, from $10.5 trillion at year-end 2009 to $33.1 trillion 
at year-end 2024.” An estimated 20% of the U.S. equity market and 30% of the U.S. 
Treasury market are held by foreigners. 

It is never wise to count on the kindness of strangers. A July 18th Bloomberg 
article, headlined “BofA Says Share of U.S. Stocks in Global Flows has Plunged in 
2025,”  warned:  “U.S.  stock  funds  attracted  just  under  half  of  total  flows  so 
far this year, compared with 72% in 2024, the BofA team said in a note, citing 
figures  from  EPFR  Global.  Foreign  inflows  slowed  to  less  than  $2  billion  in 
the past three months from $34 billion in January. President Donald Trump’s 
erratic  trade  policy,  a  ballooning  fiscal  deficit  and  a  slumping  dollar  have 
strained  investor  enthusiasm  for  American  assets.  While  the  S&P  500  has 
recovered from an April low after Trump paused the harshest tariffs in a century, 
the benchmark still trails international peers this year.”

In his new book entitled Our Dollar, Your Problem, Harvard economics professor 
and  former 
IMF  chief  economist,  Kenneth  Rogoff,  wrote  the  following: 
“American  arrogance  and  exceptionalism  have  sometimes  served  U.S. 
interests, but they also helped trigger the Great Inflation of the 1970s and 
the  2008-2009  Global  Financial  Crisis.  One  can  hope  for  a  long  period  of 
strong growth and low inflation, but it would be folly to ignore the many “this 
time  is  different”  Pax  Dollar  assumptions  built  into  today’s  markets  that  may 
well be upended over the next decade, if not much sooner.” (See WILTWs July 10, 
2025 and June 14, 2012.)

China is leading the global shift to 
6G-wireless technology. 6G networks 
provide a competitive edge for running AI 
on end-user devices and in combat zones. 
China is well-positioned to capitalize on 
its first-mover advantage, along with its 
control over the supply chain of strategic 
minerals crucial for 6G performance. What 
are the implications? How to invest.
We have tracked the disruptive potential of wireless technology since the mid-
1990s,  publishing  over  200  articles  since  then.  We  have  argued  since  2016  that 
wireless  technologies,  such  as  5G,  form  the  nervous  system  for  the  adoption  of 
exponential  technologies.  A  nation  that  deploys  them  faster  at  scale  will  have  a 
tremendous first-mover advantage in boosting economic growth. 

6G is a step change in wireless connectivity. It will enable the next generation 
of applications, such as humanoid robots, smart cities, autonomous vehicles, and 
the Internet of Battle Things (IoBT), among others. China, the U.S., South Korea, 
and Japan are pushing forward the development of 6G. However, China is once 
again demonstrating its growing technological strength and widening the 
lead with the rest of the world in developing 6G technology. The incentives 
for investing in 6G early are greater than for any other wireless technology to date. 

The  first  nation  to  lead  in  6G  technology  will  gain  not  only  a  significant 
economic  advantage,  but  also  a  crucial  national-security  edge.  The  key 
question  is  whether  the  U.S.  will  act  in  a  timely  and  decisive  manner  to 
compete.

Consider the following:

  A  robust  5G  and  5G-Advanced  (5G-A)  infrastructure  is  fundamental  to 
6G  integration.  China  has  established  a  formidable  5G  infrastructure  and 
is the clear winner of the 5G race. As of June 2025, the nation has installed 
roughly  4.5  million  base  stations.  In  contrast,  the  U.S.  is  estimated  to  have 
installed up to 270,000 5G-base stations. A 2024 KPMG report estimated that 
China has installed 206 base stations per 100,000 people, while the U.S. has 
77.  The  Ministry  of  Industry  and  Information  Technology  announced  the 
second “Set  Sail”  plan  to  expand  5G  coverage  by  2027,  aiming to increase 
the number of base stations to 380 per 100,000 people. 

  China  has  made  rapid  progress  in  deploying  5G-Advanced  networks,  a 
precursor to 6G. 5G-A offers higher speeds and lower latency compared to 
5G.  5G-A  enables  AI/ML  integration  into  the  network  core  while  improving 
overall energy efficiency. 

China Mobile, the nation’s and world’s largest mobile-network operator, 
has  deployed  5G-A  networks  in  more  than  300  cities.  China  Mobile  has 
allocated an investment of CNY 9.8 billion ($1.4 billion) this year to upgrade 
400,000 base stations to 5G-A. China’s other large carriers, China Telecom and 
China Unicom, are also collectively investing CNY 10 billion this year to deploy 
5G-Advanced. 

  6G is a game-changer for the integration of exponential technologies in 
everyday  life  and  making  AI  a  general-purpose  technology.  Upcoming 
6G networks will utilize the terahertz spectrum, with frequencies above 100 
gigahertz,  providing  speeds  100x  faster  than  5G  and  latencies  below  100 
microseconds,  compared  to  1  millisecond  for  5G,  notes  an  analysis  by  the 
New York State Wireless Association. Li Huidi, executive vice president of China 
Mobile,  claimed  that  its  5G-Advanced  network  already  supports  a  peak 
download speed of 4.2 gigabits per second (Gbps). 

While  5G  and  5G-A  offer  faster  speeds  and  broader  connectivity,  6G 
promises applications such as augmented reality, the Internet of Things, 
holographic communications, and advanced AI integration. 6G will allow 
AI to process huge datasets in real time, including simulations and digital twins, 
and to make decisions instantly.

  6G’s  sensing  and  communication  feature  is  particularly  noteworthy. 
Early  wireless  networks  transmitted  data,  while  radar  detection  and 
tracking  employed  distinct  systems  with  their  hardware  and  frequencies. 
6G’s  Information  Sharing  and  Analysis  Center  (ISAC)  feature  merges  both 
capabilities into a single, unified system. 

ISAC  allows  6G  networks  to  “observe”  the  physical  world  with  high 
accuracy.  This  integration  enables  cellular  infrastructure  and  devices  to 
collect  environmental  data  such  as  location,  object  movement,  proximity, 
and imaging. (see chart below). 

5G America recently reported that a prominent use of ISAC is UAV/drone 
detection,  where  the  primary  focus  is  object  detection,  followed  by

role models, and unwavering sources of support—they are more likely to show 
positive  behavior,  form  healthy  relationships,  experience  less  mental-health 
struggles, and excel academically.

convictions  even  when  it  is  difficult.  However,  a  hero  is  also  someone  that 
others  look  up  to—an  individual  whose  actions,  character,  and  virtues 
inspire admiration in others.

parenting.  A  caregiver  shoulders  the  burden  of  their  children’s  safety,  growth, 
and  happiness,  fighting  for  them  in  ways  often  invisible  to  the  child.  But 
even  more  essential,  a  parent-hero  inspires  admiration  and  trust  in  their

The  challenge  is  that  as  kids  mature,  their  admiration  often  gives  way  to

fearing. 

Hero parents model “grace under pressure,” which was Ernest Hemingway’s 
definition  of  courage  and  a  behavior  he  considered  the  most  important

the absence of struggle, but the choice to live through it with composure and 
integrity.  By  facing  challenges  openly,  parents  teach  children  how  to  navigate 
life’s difficulties without fear.

That is true heroism.

Parents  embody  this  same  spirit  when  they  press  forward  for  their  children’s 
welfare  despite  struggles,  doing  so  without  protest.  With  courage  comes 
responsibility  to  practice  what  one  preaches:  a  parent-hero  is  stable, 
reliable,  and  consistent.  They  never  tell  their  children  one  thing  in  private 
and model the opposite in public. Their moral code remains constant, and their 
actions align with their words. This reliability and honesty become lessons their 
children internalize for life. 

True success is often rooted in having someone who genuinely believes in 
you. Psychology even has a name for this: the Pygmalion effect—when one 
person’s belief and high expectations for another lead to improved performance, 
creating a kind of self-fulfilling prophecy. 

A  related  concept,  RISE  (relation-inferred  self-efficacy),  describes  the  way 
another’s  confidence  in  our  abilities  can  enhance  our  own  self-belief,  or 
self-efficacy. When someone believes in us, it strengthens our conviction that 
we  can  achieve  our  goals,  often  triggering  transformative  effects  on  personal 
and professional growth, boosting motivation, resilience, and ambition. 

History  is  full  of  examples.  Consider  the  young  and  struggling  Albert  Einstein, 
whose  family  friend  Max Talmud  took  an  interest  in  him  and  shared  advanced 
reading  material,  treating  him  as  fully  capable  of  grasping  it.  That  simple  act 
of  belief  helped  set  the  foundation  for  one  of  the  greatest  mathematicians  in 
history. This is the power that a supportive parent, or mentor, can have on a 
young mind. 

But parental wisdom and judgment is also important. As William Penn once 
wrote, “If knowledge is treasure, then wisdom is the treasurer. He that hath more 
knowledge than judgment is made more for another man’s use than his own.” In 
hero parenting, both wisdom and knowledge are essential. A wise parent with 
excellent  judgment  will  always  be  sought  out  by  his  or  her  children  for  their 
lifetime. 

Essential  to  the  hero  parent  is  unconditional  love:  a  parent’s  consistent, 
genuine  care  and  emotional  presence  creates  the  trust  and  security 
necessary  for  admiration  to  grow.  Love  allows  children  to  see  that  their 
parent’s guidance and example come from a place of genuine compassion, not 
obligation. Children will take this feeling of love with them for the rest of their 
lives  as  unconditional  emotional  support  is  the  most  essential  feature  in 
building their own self-assurance and confidence.

In  The  Road  to  Character,  psychologist  David  Brooks  writes:  “The  parental 
relationship  is  supposed  to  be  built  upon  unconditional  love—a  gift  that 
cannot be bought and cannot be earned.” He contrasts this with meritocratic 
love, which is based on achievement and performance, and leads to anxiety and 
a sense of conditional acceptance in children. 

Brooks  echoes  this  in  The  Art  of  Seeing  Others  Deeply  and  Being  Deeply  Seen 
where  he  writes: “It  should  be  so  simple.  We  all  want  children  to  feel  safe,  to 
know that love is constant and unconditional, that they are worthy. The problem 
is that we as parents still carry, often unconsciously, the wounds and terrors of 
our own early years, which were, in turn, caused by the wounds and terrors of 
our parents’ early years, and so on and on” (See WILTW November 30, 2023). The 
lesson is clear: if parents do not confront their own pain, their ability to be 
a hero to their child is limited. 

Indeed,  Fyodor  Dostoevsky  dramatizes  the  challenge  and  the  power  of  loving 
without  conditions.  True  heroism  is  often  quiet,  lived  out  in  constancy,  in 
love that persists even when the world—or those we care for—fail us. It is 
the steadfastness of love that shapes character (See WILTW October 1, 2020 
for an incomparable tale of unconditional love). 

Kiril Sokoloff, our founder and chairman, captured this idea in a talk he delivered 
at St. Paul’s School: “Adversity and suffering will make you stronger. It will make 
you  more  resilient.  It will make you compassionate and full of empathy for 
other  people  and  for  the  beauty  of  our  planet.”  (See  WILTW  May  8,  2025). 
Children who are taught that all suffering is purely negative may never pause to 
look for its lessons. 

It  is  during  struggle,  when  life  feels  unrelenting,  that  those  who  stand  by  us 
become  our  heroes.  Aldous  Huxley  distilled  the  secret  of  enduring  human 
connection:  “It’s  a  little  embarrassing  that  after  45  years  of  research  and 
study, the best advice I can give people is to be a little kinder to each other.” 
Kindness and love are not abstract virtues; they are forged in the trials of life and 
passed on through example. 

Consider Abraham Lincoln, who credited his mother, Nancy Hanks Lincoln, with 
instilling in him a love for learning: “All that I am or hope to be, I owe to my angel 
mother.” Or reflect on author Pam Brown’s insight about fatherhood: “Dads are 
most ordinary men turned by love into heroes, adventurers, story-tellers, and 
singers of song.”

The soaring demand for battery storage can be met, in part, 
by  repurposing  old  EV  batteries  for  about  half  the  cost  of 
new batteries. 

For example, second-life batteries are being used to store energy from rooftop 
solar  systems  on  homes  and  businesses,  decreasing  their  reliance  on  the  grid 
and cutting their electric bills.

An  EV  is  an  energy-storage  system  on  wheels.  An  EV  battery’s  useful  life  is 
between 10 and 15 years, as its storage capacity diminishes over time, reducing 
an EV’s range.

However, a 15-year-old EV battery will still retain 80 to 85% of its capacity. 
In other words, a 100-kilowatt (KW) EV battery as found in a Tesla Model S will 
still be able to store 80 to 85 KW of power for several more years. The battery can 
then be recycled for its valuable materials after five to ten years of its second life.

Reusing  EV  batteries  maximizes  their  value,  reduces  the  need  for  mining  new 
battery metals, contributes to a circular supply chain, and minimizes hazardous 
waste.

More than a million EVs and their batteries are reaching the end of life this year, 
adding to an estimated two million batteries previously retired, according to a 
study. Around 75% of these can be repurposed to help reduce the costs of 
energy storage by up to 50%. 

Consider these data points.

  An  AI  data  center  in  Nevada  is  now  powered  by  large-scale  solar  and 
hundreds  of  retired  GM  EV  batteries,  providing  63  megawatt-hours 
(MWh)  of  storage. This  is  currently  the  most  extensive  second-life  battery 
energy  storage  system  in  the  world  and  the  largest  microgrid  in  North 
America.

  AI infrastructure company Crusoe and California-based Redwood Materials, a 
battery recycler that collects 250,000 used EV batteries annually, plan to use 
similar microgrids for Crusoe’s modular AI data centers. Many companies are 
eager to operate data centers powered by renewable energy.

  B2U  Storage  Solutions  operates  two  grid-connected  solar-power  plants  in

Utilizing  used  EV  batteries  presents  some  technical  challenges  due  to  the 
varying EV battery configurations. By collaborating with automakers, companies 
like B2U and Element Energy have developed methods to plug entire EV battery 
packs into cabinets the size of shipping containers with minimal disassembly. 

If  a  battery  pack  fails  inspection,  other  companies  can  disassemble  it  to  the 
module or cell level. These can be reassembled into new battery modules.

Proprietary  power-management  software  monitors  the  repurposed  battery 
discharge and recharge as well as its safety. 

Several efforts are underway to automate battery repurposing. For example, Los
