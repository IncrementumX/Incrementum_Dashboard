Investors  have  many  reasons  to  redirect  capital  toward  EMs.  For  instance, 
the trust that foreign investors always placed in the U.S.-led international order 
has  suffered  near-irreparable  blows  in  recent  weeks,  undermining  one  of  the 
key  drivers  of  U.S.  dollar  hegemony.  With  geopolitical  tensions  intensifying, 
Canada's  prime  minister,  Mark  Carney,  made  the  following  declaration  at  the 
World Economic Forum in Davos this week: “The old [rules-based] order is not 
coming back. We should not mourn it. Nostalgia is not a strategy. But from 
the fracture, we can build something better, stronger and more just.” (See 
section 7.)

It  is  hugely  significant  that  the  rupture  between  Washington  and  Europe  is 
happening  when  the  U.S.  share  of  global  market  capitalization  is  at  an 
extreme  high  of  64%,  up  more  than  20  percentage  points  since  the  GFC. 
(See  left-hand  chart  below.)  During  that  period,  the  EM  share  was  largely 
stagnant at 10%-12%. Ironically, however, the U.S. accounts for around 
26% of global GDP–less than half of its MSCI weighting–while 
EMs are expected to account for 65% of GDP growth through 
2035, according to S&P Global. 

U.S.-listed companies may derive large percentages of their revenue outside the 
U.S.  (25%  to  40%+,  depending  on  the  index  used),  but one could reasonably 
question  whether  the  extreme  concentration  of  market  cap  and  earnings 
in asset-light technology shares makes sense in light of the changing world 
order.  (See  right-hand  chart  below.)  Our  position  is  that  the  world  is  moving 
toward  placing  a  higher  value  on  the  importance  of  natural  resources  and  hard 
assets, which could dampen sentiment toward the kinds of stocks that have driven 
U.S. markets in the past decade.

We  have  often  argued  that  a  sustained  bull  market  in  commodities  will  drive 
faster  convergence  of  EM  incomes  and  living  standards  with  the  developed 
world.  Commodity  production  and  consumption  are  often  driven  by 
emerging and developing economies, therefore reinforcing the connection 
between  EM  economic  growth  and  commodity  prices.  (See  WILTW  May  30, 
2024.)

Below,  we  graphically  show  how  GDP  per  capita  in  low-  and  middle-income 
countries  (a  proxy  for  EMs)  moved  higher  in  relation  to  the  OECD  since  1990. 
The  most  significant  increase  occurred  during  the  post-2000  EM  and 
commodity boom, when the CRB Index rose from a nadir below 150 to a high 
of  470-plus  in  2008,  before  easing  to  300-plus  in  2014.  (Note  that  some  OECD 
members,  such  as  Chile  and  Mexico,  are  also  considered  EMs  under  MSCI’s 
market classification.)

During this period, the GDP per capita of low- and middle-income countries 
relative  to  the  OECD  more  than  doubled,  from  5.1%  in  2000  to  11.7%  in 
2014,  when  the  beginning  of  the  oil  price  crash  ushered-in  a  global  deflation 
in  commodities.  Since  2014,  the  percentage  has  moved  relatively  little,  but  this 
could change if capital starts moving toward the EM space more aggressively in the 
coming years.

Our EM/commodity investment case was further buttressed by a May 2024 white 
paper published by the Centre for Economic Policy Research titled: “Commodity 
Prices  and  the  Global  Financial  Cycle,”  written  by  IMF  senior  economist 
Luciana Juvenal and University of Turin economics professor, Ivan Petrella. The 
authors  state:  “Emerging  markets  and  developing  economies  (EMDEs)  are 
particularly exposed to the global economy's fluctuations, primarily due to 
their reliance on primary commodity exports. This dependency makes them 
vulnerable  to  commodity  price  volatility  and  ties  their  economic  performance 
closely to the global financial cycle.” 

But timing that cycle is important: if we are heading into a global “inflationary 
boom,”  then  emerging  markets  are  likely  to  be  the  major  beneficiaries. 
Analyzing  data  from  54  EMDEs,  the  authors  outlined  a  clear  empirical 
relationship between export prices and GDP (see left-hand chart below) and U.S. 
monetary  policy  changes,  reflected  in  Baa  yield  spreads  (see  right-hand  chart 
below).

This  gap  underscores  the  urgent  need  for  investment  in  grid  modernization, 
creating opportunities for China’s power-equipment producers. China already 
supplies over 60% of the global transformer market and leads the world in 
ultra-high-voltage  (UHV)  transmission  technologies.  Chinese  manufacturers 
are among the few capable of delivering the scale, speed, and technology required 
for  this  global  upgrade  cycle.  This  positions  them  as  key  beneficiaries  of  the 
accelerating build-out of modern power systems both domestic and overseas. 

China  itself  is  becoming  the  largest  market  for  grid  equipment  as  it 
embraces  a  major  expansion  of  renewable  energy.  In  the  first  three 
quarters of last year, wind and solar generation accounted for more than a 
fifth of total electricity output, a milestone for the world’s largest industrial 
economy. 

Yet,  this  remarkable  growth  has  exposed  a  critical  bottleneck:  the  existing 
grid  infrastructure  struggles  to  accommodate  the  surging  and  variable  supply 
of  renewable  energy.  This  challenge  reflects  both  the  intermittent  nature  of 
renewables  and  China’s  regional  imbalance,  with  generation  concentrating 
in the resource-rich North and West of the mainland, while demand is soaring 
in  the  industrialized  East  and  South  China.  We  wrote  on  this  topic  in  WILTW 
February 6, 2025, as follows:

[R]enewables  are  also  compounding  grid  complexity.  Unlike  fossil-
fuel  that  connect  to  grids  via  single-stage  voltage  conversion,  renewable 
projects  require  multi-stage  transformer  systems  to  step-up  power 
from  dispersed,  low-voltage  generation  sources  to  the  high-voltage 
levels  needed  for  transmission  and  grid  integration.  In  addition,  most 
utility-scale renewables are built in resource-rich but grid-distant areas, 
requiring additional transformers for long-distance transmission. 

Chinese  policymakers  have  responded  with  decisive  policy  frameworks 
to  accelerate  grid  modernization.  The  Action  Plan  for  Accelerating  the 
Construction  of  a  New-Type  Power  System  (2024-2027),  alongside  the  15th 
Five-Year  Plan  proposal,  emphasizes  building  a  flexible,  resilient  power 
system capable of accommodating high shares of renewable energy.

Supporting  these  policy  goals,  China’s  two  major  grid  operators—State 
Grid Corporation of China (SGCC) and China Southern Power Grid (CSPG)—
have  launched  a  record-breaking  investment  program.  SGCC  recently 
announced  that  its  fixed-asset  investment  during  the  15th  Five-Year  Plan  period 
(2026–2030)  will  reach  4  trillion  yuan—40%  higher  than  under  the  14th  Five-Year 
Plan. This provides long-term demand visibility for grid-equipment suppliers.

At  the  heart  of  China’s  grid  modernization  effort  is  its  world-leading  UHV 
transmission  network,  designed  to  resolve  the  country’s  geographic  energy 
imbalance.  By  2025,  China  had  commissioned  45  UHV  projects,  extending

Unique  climatological  conditions  in  the  1930s  produced

Those +90-year-old summer-heat records will be broken in the 2030s, if not 
sooner. More importantly, today’s hotter America is situated in a hotter global 
climate. The country is experiencing far more severe-weather events with wider 
and more lasting impacts on America than the Dust Bowl.

What are parents most afraid of for their 
children today?
It  is  no  longer  only  about  safety  or  success.  Increasingly,  parents  worry  about 
something  more  elusive  and  far  harder  to  diagnose:  that  their  children  are 
growing up unmoored. Therapists describe a shift in the anxieties parents bring 
into  the  therapy  room. Yes,  there  are  concerns  about  screens,  school  pressure, 
and  fitting  in.  But  beneath  these  lies  a  deeper  unease  about  motivation, 
meaning, and values. Parents fear not just that their children might fail, but 
that they might not know what is worth striving for in the first place. 

Modern Parenting Solutions, a mental-health therapy organization for children 
and  teens,  recently  identified  four  core  fears  that  appear  most  frequently  in 
parent  sessions.  These  include  waning  motivation,  non-traditional  family 
structures,  and    technological  dependence.  But  above  all  else,  it  is 
the  fear  that  children  lack  a  moral  compass  in  the  modern 
era. Why have these particular anxieties risen to the surface, and what do they 
reveal about the pressure shaping parenting today?

The  psychologists  at  Modern  Parenting  Solutions  work  with  what  they  call 
the  “modern  parent:”  someone  navigating  a  world  defined  by  social 
media,  digital  addiction,  AI,  and  an  erosion  of  social  connection  that  has 
profoundly  altered  childhood  and  adolescence.  Beyond  technology  itself, 
parents  worry  about  the  diminishing  depth  of  teen  friendships,  increased 
isolation, and a loss of spirituality or shared meaning. 

As  one  psychologist  put  it,  “I  know  that  today’s  modern  parents  are  unfairly 
being stereotyped as being lazy and incompetent, but from what I’ve seen, most 
modern parents out there are going above and beyond to figure out how to 
raise kids that are interesting, personable, and moral in today’s world.” The 
problem is not a lack of effort. It is uncertainty about how to respond to challenges 
that feel historically unprecedented.

something that genuinely matters to them. Motivation, therapists emphasize, 
is not a personality trait. It is a skill that develops when children experience 
meaning, agency, and competence over time.

Taking Greenland: Is Trump serious? And 
what can Europe do about it if he is?
President  Trump’s  renewed  push  to  gain  control  of  Greenland  has  further 
disrupted  the  already  strained  relations  between  the  U.S.  and  Europe.  The 
obsession  of  the  Trump  administration  with  obtaining  full  ownership  of 
Greenland  seems  extreme  given  the  potential  costs  of  pressing  forward  with 
that  objective.  However,  it  appears  that  Trump  is  intent  on  taking  over 
the  island  for  three  primary  reasons:  Security,  Economics,  and  Prestige. 
Are  these  reasons  valid,  can  Europe  do  anything  about  them,  and  what  are  the 
implications of an American takeover of Greenland?

Psychology and Prestige

Although  much  is  made  of  the  personality  of  the  President,  prestige  and 
the  underlying  philosophies  of  both  the  President  and  his  advisors  are  likely 
significant  factors  in  this  process.  Most  European  leaders  supported  Trump’s 
political opponents in all three of his Presidential runs, and philosophically are 
closer  to  the  Democratic  Party  in  both  domestic  and  foreign  policy.  Grudges 
aside, simply put, many in the administration do not either like or trust the 
majority of the current generation of European leaders. This is a major factor 
in the security and economic discussions. 

The  issue  of  prestige  is  also  important  to  Trump,  and  he  often  cites  past 
Presidents who expanded the territory of the U.S. as being among the greatest. 
Acquiring  Greenland  would  be  the  largest  territorial  acquisition  in  U.S. 
history, at 836 thousand square miles it would be slightly larger than the 828 
thousand square miles of the Louisiana Purchase. Trump appears increasingly 
concerned about his legacy. That undoubtedly plays a role in this issue.

Security

While the importance is sometimes exaggerated, Greenland is still an important 
location  with  respect  to  global  security.  It  sits  astride  important  sea  lanes  and 
with Iceland controls access to the North Atlantic. Greenland is an important 
command  and  control  location  for  polar  orbiting  satellites  which  are  the 
primary  orbits  of  intelligence  satellites  as  well  as  navigation  satellites  such 
as  GPS.  In  recent  years,  with  the  growth  of  low-earth  orbiting  communication 
networks  such  as  Starlink,  locations  in  the  far  north  or  south  are  important  to 
enable regular direct communications and monitoring of these satellites as well. 

Greenland  was  a  vital  component  of  the  Cold  War  era  radar  and  missile-
detection system. Although that has largely been decommissioned and replaced 
by  early  warning  satellites,  ground-based  systems  would  be  an  important 
component  for  a  missile-defense  system  such  as  the  President’s  proposed 
“Golden Dome” (originally called The Iron Dome for America) system. Greenland 
would be a key location for both radars and ground-based interceptors for 
a comprehensive missile defense system. 

While  the  1951  treaty  between  Denmark  and  the  United  States  gives  the  U.S. 
broad powers to ensure the security of Greenland, and would almost certainly 
allow  the  U.S.  to  unilaterally  establish  a  missile  defense  system  on  the  island, 
the  administration  believes  that  treaty  is  not  enough  assurance  for  a 
vital  trillion-dollar  defense  investment.  The  Greenland  Defense  Treaty  runs 
concurrently  with  the  NATO  treaty.  If  the  U.S.  were  to  withdraw  from  NATO, 
or  NATO  were  to  collapse  for  whatever  reason,  U.S.  rights  in  Greenland  would 
expire  as  well.  The  Greenland  independence  movement  is  another  factor 
the administration is considering, as the  future of the treaty would likely be 
subject  to  negotiation,  and  it  is  unlikely  a  future  Greenlandic  government 
would  want  to  give  away  the  extensive  rights  that  post-World  War  II 
Denmark was willing to agree to.

As  noted  in  WILTW  January  8th,  President  Trump  and  his  administration  have 
little regard for international law and treaties. In their view these agreements are 
not binding if they conflict with essential U.S. interests, therefore, they assume 
that others will not respect them either. In a Truth Social post on January 20th, 
Trump  wrote:  "It's  shocking,  but  our  'brilliant'  NATO  ally,  the  UK,  is  going  to  give

Economics

As  with  its  military  significance,  the  economic  potential  of  Greenland  is 
sometimes  exaggerated.  Nevertheless,  it  is  still  potentially  significant.  As 
discussed  in  WILTW  in  January  of  last  year,  the  U.S.  has  had  a  long  interest  in 
acquiring  Greenland  for  both  security  and  economic  reasons.  Most  recently, 
the  potential  for  critical  minerals  in  Greenland  has  reached  the  forefront, 
especially  given  Chinese  attempts  to  acquire  the  mining  rights  to  sites  on  the 
Island.

One  of  the  limitations  on  sites  for  large  data  centers,  computing  complexes, 
and  next  generation  infrastructure  for  AI  is  cooling.  Despite  the  significant 
challenges,  several  high-tech  leaders  such  as  Elon  Musk  have  floated  the 
idea  of  basing  data  centers  in  Greenland.  The  U.S.  would  want  access  and 
control  these  technologies  as  well  as  ensure  the  revenues  remained  within 
the  U.S.,  and  investors  would  not  want  the  much  more  stringent  European 
environmental,  privacy,  and  other  regulatory  limitations  to  interfere  with  their 
development. An American acquisition of Greenland fits both imperatives.

Energy  resources  and  the  ongoing  competition  for  the  Arctic  sea  floor 
are  another  big  factor.  Despite  being  an  Arctic  nation  due  to  the  ownership 
of  Alaska,  as  shown  on  this  map,  U.S.  claims  in  the  Arctic  are  rather  limited 
compared to Russia, Canada, and, through Greenland, Denmark.

One  key  area  of  contested  claims  is  the  Lomonosov  Ridge.  This  ridge  may

as  these  reserves  will  not  be  commercially  viable  at  prices  acceptable  to  the 
administration. But denying these resources to Russia and China could be a 
significant incentive to acquire Greenland.

Implications

In total, the Administration’s desire to take control of Greenland must be taken 
seriously, the reasons are not entirely specious. Trump has committed significant 
political capital to the effort, and will want to have some solution before the 
mid-term  elections  in  November. Therefore,  it  is  essential  to  understand  the 
potential implications and European responses.

Europe is in an almost impossible position both militarily and economically, 
and its ability to respond is rather limited. Having decisively cut themselves 
off from Russia, energy is likely the limiting issue over any economic retaliation 
against the U.S. Even if Europe wanted to resume natural gas flows from Russia, 
and  Russia  was  willing  to  provide  them,  there  has  been  significant  damage 
to  the  infrastructure.  Most  of  the  pipelines  through  Ukraine  are  inoperative, 
and  of  course  the  Nordstream  pipeline  was  severely  damaged  in  September 
2022. Therefore,  Europe  is  now  dependent  on  LNG  from  the  U.S.  or  close  U.S. 
allies  like  Qatar,  giving  the  Americans  leverage  in  any  attempts  by  Europe  to 
use economic pressure such as threatening a closer relationship with China. For 
further analysis on the economic aspects of this conflict see section 5 and 6 in 
this issue. 

Although  President  Trump  said  military  force  would  not  be  used,  from  a 
military  perspective  it  is  inconceivable  that  any  individual  or  even  a  group  of 
all  of  the  other  NATO  countries  could  intervene  in  the  short  run  to  prevent 
an  American  hostile  takeover  if  one  was  ordered.  Due  to  NATO  integration, 
the  communications,  intelligence,  and  logistics  of  European  militaries 
are  utterly  dominated  by  the  Americans.  As  we  discussed  last  year  (WILTW 
March  13,  2025),  most  European  weapons  systems  have  explicit  or  implicit 
“kill switches” and could be triggered by the U.S. in a direct conflict. It will 
take many years, perhaps a decade, of concentrated effort for Europe to create 
a  credible,  independent  defense  system.  Europeans  would  not  want  to  face 
Russia alone in their present state of preparedness, making them more likely to 
accommodate Trump in some way, no matter how painful.

In  the  past  the  President  has  expressed  skepticism  over  NATO,  feeling  Europe 
takes  advantage  of  the  U.S.  by  hiding  behind  the  American  military,  enabling 
them  to  limit  their  own  defense  spending  while  committing  the  U.S.  to 
defending  them  even  if  that  is  not  to  America’s  advantage.  However,  the 
vast  majority  of  the  U.S.  Congress  strongly  supports  NATO, 
and opposes the administration’s heavy-handed attempts to 
acquire Greenland.
