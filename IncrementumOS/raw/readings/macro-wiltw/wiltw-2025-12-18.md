The White  House  is  pushing  for  additional  Fed  policy-rate  cuts  as  it  continues 
to reshape the central bank with doves. But the market—not the Fed—ultimately 
controls  the  direction  of  intermediate-  and  long-term  interest  rates.  (See  WILTW 
July 10, 2025.) Not surprisingly, the bond vigilantes are getting louder. 

Since the Fed’s current rate-cutting cycle began in September 2024, the Fed 
Funds rate has been reduced by 175 basis points (bps), but 10 and 30-year 
UST yields have risen by 50 and 87 bps, respectively. And, over the past three 
months, the Fed Fund’s rate has fallen 75 bps, but the 10-year and 30-year rates 
have risen by 12 and 18 bps, respectively. Long rates are not following the Fed’s 
path for short-term rates, and they are staying elevated despite increasing signs of 
weakness in payrolls, energy prices and the like. (See chart below.)

Source: Apollo Global Management

Point:  Corporate  and  personal  debt  is  nowhere  near  levels  which  precipitated 
the last crisis. Counterpoint: private-sector balance sheets were largely repaired 
via  15-plus  years  of  loose  fiscal  and  monetary  policies.  But  those  policies  have 
pushed  sovereign  debt  to  unsustainable  levels,  with  an  associated  debt-service 
burden which will eat into underlying economic vitality in the coming years.

Aggregate  global  debt  has  increased  over  45%  during  the  past  decade, 
to nearly $320 trillion last year (and to $340-plus trillion so far this year), 
while sovereign net-interest costs are projected to triple between 2020 and 
2030, to nearly $4 trillion annually. This is money that will be diverted from 
other, income-generating long-lived investment that would offset some of the 
productivity gains from AI.

Source: IIF, The Wall Street Journal

Point: Markets are optimistic that Fed rate cuts will stimulate economic growth. 
Counterpoint:  The  Fed  will  increasingly  be  constrained  by  the  “Hobson’s 
choice” between boosting growth and triggering inflation on the one hand, or 
restraining  inflation  and  triggering  a  recession  on  the  other.  It  is  noteworthy 
that the Fed not only ended QT this year, but has announced a new short-
term  asset  purchase  program  amounting  to  $40  billion/month  initially, 
aiming to ease liquidity strains in the banking sector. 

Whether we call this “QE” or “not QE”, the fact is that the Fed is expanding its 
balance sheet again, as we predicted last year (see left-hand chart below). More 
important,  the  restart  of  asset  growth  is  likely  to  weigh  on  the  U.S.  dollar 
going forward (see right-hand chart below.) As we have argued for years, short 
of a major austerity program to get deficits and entitlement spending under control, 
the  Fed  is  ultimately  going  to  be  forced  to  cap  yields  (YCC),  which  will  eventually 
open a trap door under the dollar.

While we follow dozens of long-term investment themes, only our highest-
conviction themes are summarized in the body of this report. Several of our 
key themes are listed below.  

1.  Metals  and  mining—particularly  gold,  silver,  copper  and  other  EV 
metals and their related equities. Bottlenecks in the supply of key metals 
and  minerals  will  delay  the  energy  transition  far  longer  than  markets  and 
policymakers  realize.  The  clean-energy  transition  will  require  four  to 
six  times  more  metal  and  mineral  inputs  than  today.  However,  most 
deposits are located on or near indigenous lands. And many of these 
are in regions with water shortages, conflict conditions, or food insecurity, 
increasing supply risks. 

2.  Uranium  is  emerging  from  a  12-plus  year  bear  market  and  is  at  the 
front-end  of  a  new  super-cycle  bull  market.  A  nuclear  renaissance 
is  crucial  to  reach  zero  emissions,  with  over  30  countries  considering 
making nuclear power central to their decarbonization efforts.

3.  The  scarcity  and  concentration  of  critical  minerals  is  fueling  a  new, 
global,  multi-year  investment  super-cycle  to  secure  supply.  We  believe 
that  sourcing  critical  minerals  will  take  center  stage  in  the  new  U.S. 
administration, given their importance in defense and strategic industrial 
applications.    

4.  Artificial  intelligence  (AI),  machine  learning,  robotics,  the  IoT,  and  3D 
printers  are  key  component  technologies  of  our  automation  theme, 
which we believe is an unstoppable megatrend, spurring innovation and 
enhancing  human  productivity.  Generative  AI  represents  a  step  change 
in how artificial intelligence is used and is supercharging the global tech 
landscape.

5.  A global arms race is accelerating as  new technologies and weapon 

systems, such as drones, could alter the balance of power.

6.  As long ago as WATMTUs December 4, 2022 and December 11, 2022, we 
explained our thesis that most commodity-sectors and related stocks 
continue to be excellent candidates for long-term “core holdings.”

In WILTW December 4, 2025, we wrote a section headlined “Is silver entering its

The chart of silver’s takeoff from the 46-year cup-and-handle is something 
we’ve  only  seen  once  before  in  market  history—and  that  was  after  the 
triple-top  formed  in  the  DJIA  between  1966  and  1973,  Nixon  closed  the 
gold window and OPEC initiated an oil embargo. These events propelled the 
nominal silver price from less than $1.50/ounce in 1971 to over $50/ounce at its 
intraday all-time high in early 1980.

During  the  five  years  ended  December  2024,  gold  outperformed  silver  73% 
to  62%.  However,  since  year-end  2024,  silver  has  outperformed  gold  by 
over  60  percentage  points.  Silver’s  outperformance  of  gold  demonstrates  that 
the  commodity  bull  market  is  durable  and  sustainable,  because  silver  has  both 
industrial uses and is an inflation hedge. 

Compounding the unfolding squeeze on physical metal has been the persistence 
of supply deficits. This year marks silver’s fifth consecutive year of structural 
deficit.  (See  WILTWs  November  24,  2022;  May  11,  2023  and  May  1,  2025.) The 
key  factors  behind  these  persistent  deficits  have  not  been  ameliorated  to  any 
significant  degree.  Mine  supply  has  been  flat  for  three  years,  between  810 
and  815  million  ounces,  which  remains  80-90  million  ounces  below  the 
2016  high. The  incremental  supply  from  recycled  silver  has  offset  only  half  of 
the mine-supply loss during that time, rising only 40 million ounces since 2016. 

Meanwhile,  industrial  demand  has  increased  a  whopping  174  million 
ounces since 2016—more than twice the volume erosion of mine supply.

The  only  way  to  close  the  structural  supply  gap  of  30-  to  50-million  pounds 
for  the  existing  reactor  fleet,  not  to  mention  the  incremental  demand  from

record lows in the West.

represents  only  4%-8%  of  a  reactor’s  operating  costs  versus  80%-90%  for  oil 
and gas in thermal plants. Consequently, higher uranium prices are unlikely to 
curtail demand.

What Makes Us Human
This  quarter,  we  explored  how  modern  life  is  reshaping  family  dynamics, 
relationships,  psychological  well-being,  and  the  future  of  education.  Our

“Orwell feared those who would ban books. Huxley feared that there would 
be  no  reason  to  ban  a  book  because  there  would  be  no  one  who  wanted 
to read one.” With literacy rates in freefall, what are the implications? This 
line was written by media theorist Neil Postman in his book, Amusing Ourselves 
To  Death.  Published  in  1985,  the  book  is  a  sharp  critique  of  the  television  age 
as well as a prophecy for the revolution we are living through now, with social 
media and AI. Writing and reading are dying at the hands of smartphones, short 
videos, and LLMs that can do our writing and thinking for us. Adults are reading 
less. Teenagers are reading a lot less. Children are being read to less, if at all. 

As author and historian Niall Ferguson notes: “When people stop reading, they 
stop  being  able  to  read.  And  when  people  stop  being  able  to  read—to  make 
sense of the meaning of text on a page—they also lose the ability to make sense 
of the world.” 

According to a 2022 survey, 52% of Americans hadn’t read a 
book in over a year. One in 10 hadn’t read a book in more than 10 years. 
Postman warned:

When a population becomes distracted by trivia, when cultural life is redefined 
as  a  perpetual  round  of  entertainments,  when  serious  public  conversation 
becomes  a  form  of  baby-talk,  when,  in  short,  a  people  become  an  audience 
and  their  public  business  a  vaudeville  act,  then  a  nation  finds  itself  at  risk; 
culture-death is a clear possibility. (WILTW October 16, 2025)

Disruption and Innovation
We  have  studied  the  implications  of  disruptive  and  innovative  technologies 
for over three decades, publishing over 1,500 articles. In 4Q25, we published 
six  articles  on  the  theme.  Topics  covered  include  quantum  computing, 
revolutionary  advances  in  regenerative  medicine,  the  potential  for  GLP-1 
therapies to be the first true “longevity drugs,” India’s emerging deep-tech 
ecosystem, the quest for artificial superintelligence, and the increasing risk 
in AI-circular deals.

Our  theme  is  based  on  Joseph  Schumpeter’s  economic  theory  of  creative 
destruction,  which  holds  that  new  technologies  constantly  evolve,  overtaking 
the old, and replacing previous economic and technological systems to facilitate 
progress.  Since  2006,  much  of  our  technology  research  has  been  framed 
within Ray Kurzweil’s Law of Accelerating Returns. 

A central tenet of the Law of Accelerating Returns is that technological progress 
advances at an exponential pace, never slows down, and is unaffected by wars, 
economic recessions, or depressions. One key lesson from our three decades of 
tracking  disruptive  technologies  is  that  the  sector  may  periodically  fall  out  of 
favor  with  investors.  However,  technological  advances  and  economic  inroads 
continue relentlessly.

Since  2011,  our  recommendations  have  generated  an 
average  return  of  486.4%  versus  204.1%  for  the  S&P  500 
Index and 154.0% for the MSCI World. The recommendations made 
over the last three years have outperformed the benchmark S&P and MSCI by a 
substantial margin (27.0% vs. 13.1% and 14.4% respectively).
