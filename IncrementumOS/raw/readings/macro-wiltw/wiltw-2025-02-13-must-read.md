Probably  outperformance  by  Chinese  and  Hong  Kong  stocks,  which  NO  ONE

So  far  this  year,  gold  has  risen  nearly  11%  versus  2.9%  for  the  S&P  500.  Over

NASDAQ 100 as well is lagging, with a 22% gain over the past 12 months. Market 
participants will find it hard to believe but gold soared 850% over the past 25 
years against 320% for the S&P. 

As  so  often  happens,  gold  is  leading  the  way,  breaking  out  of  key  levels  and 
hitting all-time highs in every currency.

Even  more  interesting,  over  the  past  seven  weeks,  the  Mag-7  ETF  has 
underperformed  China  equities  (FXI),  gold  miners  (GDX),  copper  miners 
(COPX),  energy  sector  (XLE),  commodities  (CRB  Index),  and  small-caps 
(SML), among others. In a few instances, the pace of the downtrend in the Mag-
7 ETF relative strength measures is gathering some downside speed, particularly 
versus FXI.

Our  take  is  that  a  protracted  trade  war,  and  particularly  its  unintended

Three key points stand out for consideration. First is the decades-long uptrend in

access  new  markets  that  could  generate  more  rapid  growth 
that would not have been possible in the U.S. alone. In so doing,

The conventional wisdom has long outlined the many unintended consequences 
of  offshoring  of  U.S.  manufacturing,  such  as  depopulation  in  rust-belt  regions 
and a rise in opioid addiction, among other challenges that were described in 
great  detail  in  U.S.  Vice  President  J.D.  Vance’s  book,  Hillbilly  Elegy.  (See  WILTW 
August 25, 2016.) But it is less appreciated just how much trade liberalization 
has  contributed  to  the  “everyday  low  price”  mantra  that  all  U.S.  citizens 
have become accustomed to.

The effects of widespread inflation have created significant stress among a wider 
swath of consumers, and raising domestic tariffs while reducing the availability 
of low-cost imports is likely to spread even more pain. It is worth adding here 
that  our  work  on  market  action  suggests  that  the “inflation  markets”  are 
coming alive, which implies that the inflation trend has legs. (See WATMTU 
January 26, 2025.)

What  about  the  potential  impact  on  U.S.  competitiveness?  Harvard  economist 
Lydia  Cox  authored  a  white  paper  in  2022  titled  “The  Long-Term  Impact  of 
Steel Tariffs on U.S. Manufacturing,” which studied the impact of steel tariffs 
imposed by President George W. Bush in the early 2000s. 

Cox’s  study  found  that  upstream  steel  tariffs  carry  “persistent  negative 
impacts  on  the  competitiveness  of  downstream  producers”  and  that  drops 
“in the competitiveness of U.S. exports due to the tariffs are highly persistent—
global  market  share  remains  depressed  relative  to  pre-tariff  levels  for  at 
least 8 years after the tariffs are lifted.”

Cox also found that “the steel tariffs induced a restructuring of global trade 
flows  away  from  downstream  producers  in  the  United  States  toward  the 
United States’ top competitors.” 

In  addition,  the  escalating  trade  friction  could  incentivize  investors  to  look 
elsewhere  for  faster  growth  and  a  more  stable  political  environment.  Since 
2010,  the  U.S.  Net  International  Investment  Position  (NIIP)  has  expanded 
from  -2.8  trillion  to  -23.6  trillion.  (See  WILTW September  17,  2020.)  The U.S.’s 
present course risks triggering a reversal in this metric.

Declining trade with the U.S. will spur America’s trade partners to diversify 
their FX holdings away from the USD, while also raising the attractiveness 
of  gold  as  a  more  trustworthy  reserve  asset.  Gold  continues  to  break  out 
to  new  highs,  as  physical  delivery  of  metal  accelerates  and  foreign  countries 
continue  to  raise  their  purchases,  as  we  wrote  last  week  and  which  is  also 
discussed in section 8.

Yesterday’s  U.S.  CPI  report  was  just  the  latest  evidence  that  secular  inflation 
is  likely  to  exceed  expectations,  underscoring  our  oft-stated  thesis.  (See  our 
Publisher’s  Letter  dated  December  19,  2024,  headlined  “Is  the  groundwork 
being laid for the next wave of inflation?”)

The 10-year UST yield has reversed its recent decline, and appears poised 
to  catch-up  to  the  TIP-to-IEF  ratio,  which  has  already  broken-out  to  new 
highs. (See first chart below.) And gold appears ready to break-out of the upper 
boundary  of  its  upward-sloping  parallel  channel,  which  would  be  very  bullish.

Artificial intelligence is becoming the next 
general-purpose technology. The U.S.-
China quest for technological supremacy 
will be waged and won based on who 
integrates AI throughout economic sectors 
more quickly and efficiently. What are the 
implications? How to invest.
Since  2016,  we  have  argued  that  an  escalating  tech-arms  race  could  shift  the 
balance of power (see reports). For over a decade, we have also made the case 
that  AI  will  become  pervasive,  improving  human  abilities,  raising  productivity, 
and  creating  value  (see  reports).  The  rapid  development  and  deployment  of 
generative AI (GAI) is a step change, representing a significant shift in the impact 
of technology. AI's widespread availability, ease of accessibility, cost declines, and 
performance  improvements,  combined  with  its  potential  to  transform  almost  all 
economic sectors—akin to electricity and the internet—make it a general-purpose 
technology (GPT).

A  Federal  Reserve  Bank  of  St.  Louis  study  notes,  "The  wide  variety  of  tasks 
for  which  people  use  generative  AI,  combined  with  the  widespread  adoption  of 
generative  AI  across  many  demographics  and  occupations,  suggest  that  it  is  a 
general-purpose technology."

AI is converging with many other exponential technologies to turbocharge 
innovation  and  become  a  GPT.  In  contrast  to  a  single-purpose  technology, 
a  GPT  is  distinguished  by  its  socioeconomic  pervasiveness  and  synergistic 
impact  on  other  innovations,  resulting  in  significant  productivity  and  financial 
gains when combined with complementary technologies. For example, single-
purpose technologies like medical devices, pharmaceuticals, EVs, batteries, and 
advanced manufacturing machines do not impact AI’s progress. Conversely, AI 
underpins the innovation of these technologies.

Jeffrey Ding, a Professor at George Washington University, argues in his recently 
published  book,  Technology  and  the  Rise  of  Great  Powers:  How  Diffusion  Shapes 
Economic Competition, that GPTs don't transform economies overnight. Their 
real  power  comes  not  from  early  innovation  but  from  long-term  diffusion. 
It requires continuous complementary innovation to integrate GPT across other 
industries,  upskill  the  workforce,  and  support  infrastructure  development  and 
government policies.

The diffusion of AI across industries is underemphasized, mainly in the U.S., 
which  is  disproportionately  focused  on  leading-edge  frontier  research. 
We  have  also  made  a  case  that  China  has  a  competitive  advantage  due  to  its 
capability  to  mobilize  resources  quickly  and  experience  in  rapidly  integrating 
innovations  across  industries.  BYD,  China’s  largest  automobile  company,  has 
already  introduced  the  “God’s  Eye”  system,  an  intelligent  driving  technology 
powered by DeepSeek’s AI. 

The world stands at the cusp of a substantial transformation, with AI poised 
to drive technological breakthroughs to boost productivity, the economy, 
and  human  lifespan  and  help  solve  global  grand  challenges.  As  a  true 
general-purpose  technology,  AI  will  shape  the  most  significant  economic  and 
societal shifts of the 21st century. Nations will massively invest in AI adoption, 
workforce training, and strategic policies. Leaders in this transformation will shift 
the balance of power in their favor.

Consider the following:

  Generative AI's significant innovation growth over the previous two to 
three years demonstrates its promise as a GPT. Previous GPT revolutions

measurements  show  that  the  polar  region’s  rivers,  particularly  in  the 
permafrost  zones,  release  about  2  billion  tons  of  carbon  dioxide  (CO2) 
annually. That’s equivalent to Russia’s annual CO2 emissions.

If  global  warming  stays  around  1.5  degrees  C  in  the  years  ahead,  25%  of  the 
world’s  carbon-  and  methane-laden  permafrost  will  thaw,  releasing  billions  of 
tons of carbon and further raising temperatures.

The Arctic is becoming unrecognizable. We quote Dirk Notz, a climate scientist 
at the University of Hamburg: 

This  will  probably  be  the  first  landscape  that  disappears  because  of  human 
activities.

There  is  broad  scientific  consensus  that  the  global  temperature  rise  will  be 
higher  than  1.5  degrees  C  and  reach  2.0  degrees  C  or  more.  If  temperatures 
rise  further  to  2.7  degrees  C  by  2100,  as  expected  with  current  country 
pledges  to  reduce  emissions,  then  half  of  the  world’s  permafrost  would 
thaw,  according to the Science study. 

Losing  half  of  the  world’s  permafrost  would  add  an  estimated  430  billion 
tons  of  CO2  into  the  atmosphere  before  the  end  of  the  century.  Various 
climate-tipping  points  will  be  triggered  with  catastrophic  impacts,  as  we  have 
warned. (See WILTW January 13, 2022, January 19, 2023, and March 21, 2024.)

As  for  Greenland,  at  2.7  degrees  C,  ice  loss  likely  quadruples,  raising  sea 
levels 20 centimeters (8 inches) by 2100 and several meters more after that. 
This is another irreversible tipping point.

A  temperature  rise  of  2.7  degrees  C  may  occur  much  sooner  than  2100.  New 
evidence suggests that the oceans no longer absorb as much heat from the 
atmosphere. 

The  oceans  absorb  more  than  90%  of  the  excess  heat  produced  by  climate 
change. For example, the oceans absorbed 15 zetajoules of heat energy in 2023, 
moderating the impacts of climate change. (One zetajoule equals 10 times the 
world's electricity generation in 2020.)

Over  the  last  two  decades,  the  amount  of  heat  transferred  from  ocean’s 
surface  waters  into  its  depths  has  rapidly  declined.  This  is  a  fundamental 
change  in  the  Earth’s  important  heat-transfer  mechanism  that  regulates  the 
global temperature, according to a new study in Nature Climate Change. 

The weakening of the ocean’s heat-transfer mechanism keeps more heat in the 
upper 200 meters. This explains why sea surface temperatures have reached 
record highs in recent years. It also explains why marine heat waves have been 
more  intense  and  last  longer  and  why  coral  reefs  are  dying  worldwide.  (See 
WILTW October 27, 2022, October 26, 2023, and May 9, 2024.)

ocean’s ability to mitigate global warming will likely accelerate the rise of global

Temperature acceleration is well underway. The rate of warming has increased 
50% since 2010, with an astonishing surge of 0.4 degrees C in the last two 
years, according to a study published last week. In the last 50 years, the rate of 
warming has been 0.18 degrees C per decade. 

Increased frequency and severity of extreme weather will be the near-term 
consequence  of  accelerated  warming.  More  powerful  tropical  storms  and 
thunderstorms, extreme flooding, increases in the intensity of heat waves, and 
droughts are coming, according to the study and as we have also warned. (See 
reports.)

The  meltdown  of  the  Arctic  and  Greenland  will  also  accelerate,  dumping 
enormous  volumes  of  cold  fresh  water.  Unless  emissions  are  drastically 
reduced, a shut down of the Atlantic Ocean Overturning Circulation (AMOC) 
is likely within 20 to 30 years, the study concludes. 

The AMOC moves warm water from the tropics to the northern Atlantic, which 
moderates temperatures in the UK and western Europe as well as eastern North 
America.  It  also  prevents  a  build-up  of  heat  in  the  tropics. We  have  previously 
written: 

A substantial slowdown or shutdown of the AMOC might duplicate conditions

America, and parts of Asia between the 14th and 19th centuries. This period 
was characterized by harsh winters, crop failures, disease outbreaks, conflict, 
and  mass  migration.  (See  August  26,  2021,  January  19,  2023,  and  July  27,

If AMOC is allowed to shut down, it will lock in major problems including sea

Reforming the Defense Department: 
Lessons from history.
The sprawling and expensive Department of Defense (DOD) is always a target for 
reformers. Elon Musk, tasked with leading the new Department of Government 
Efficiency (DOGE), is the latest asked to try his hand at this enormous task.  Musk 
has  already  suggested  sweeping  changes  at  DOD,  such  as  replacing  manned 
aircraft with drone swarms.  Successful businessmen enter the specialized world 
of  military  affairs  thinking  they  can  apply  the  kinds  of  leadership  skills  and 
analysis  tools  that  served  them  well  in  the  commercial  sector.   Yet  the  record 
of  luminaries  in  the  business  world,  even  those  with  military  experience,  has 
not been a good one.  What lessons can be learned from the experiences of two 
men  considered  the  best  and  brightest  of  their  times,  Robert  McNamara  and 
Donald Rumsfeld, and that of Harold Brown, an academic and President Carter’s 
Defense Secretary, in attempting to reform the DOD?

Perhaps  the  most  famous  businessman  to  take  over  the  DOD  was  Robert 
McNamara.    McNamara  served  in World War  II,  and  was  a  pioneer  in  the  field 
of systems analysis.  After the war, McNamara was part of the “Whiz Kids,” who 
applied  and  expanded  some  of  the  new  management  techniques  developed 
during the war to reform the Ford Motor Company.  When appointed Secretary

growing  involvement  in  Vietnam  along  with  the  ongoing  cost  of  the  nuclear

stressing quantitative-systems analysis as decision-making aids.

McNamara’s  tendency  to  reduce  everything  to  numbers  has  come  to  be 
known  as  the  McNamara  Fallacy.    War  was  reduced  to  a  math  equation: 
increase enemy casualties while minimizing friendly losses, and the war would 
be  won.   When  told  by  General  Edward  Lansdale  that  he  was  not  considering 
the feelings of the Vietnamese people, McNamara reportedly replied that if he 
could not measure it, it must not be important.  

In  the  area  of  consolidation,  some  of  his  attempts  were  successful,  such  as 
forcing the Air Force to adopt the Navy’s F-4 Phantom II, but others, such as the 
TFX  project  were  not.    By  trying  to  mesh  the  different  requirements  of  the 
Air  Force  and  Navy  into  a  single  platform,  the  result  was  an  aircraft  that 
did not meet Navy requirements, and contained features the Air Force did 
not need, although the F-111 later became somewhat successful as an Air Force 
bomber.  Nevertheless, while intended to save money it was expensive, costing 
far  more  to  build  and  operate  in  the  long  run  than  the  dedicated  Air  Force 
designs it replaced.  

Forty years later, another successful businessman with military experience was 
brought  in  as  SECDEF.    Donald  Rumsfeld  began  his  Washington  career  as  a 
Congressman and during the Gerald Ford administration was briefly appointed 
as SECDEF, becoming the youngest Secretary in U.S. history.  Although he served 
in various advisory positions during the Reagan years, Rumsfeld spent most of 
the    twenty-four  years  between  the  Ford  and  George W.  Bush  administrations 
in the business world, turning around failing companies like Searle and General 
Instruments, while gaining a reputation as a hard charging administrator.  

Rumsfeld was tasked by Bush with reforming a military that was seen as adrift 
after  the  end  of  the  Cold  War.    He  came  into  the  position  with  strong  views 
on  the  changes  in  military  affairs  resulting  from  rapid  technology  advances 
such  as  stealth  aircraft,  reconnaissance,  and  precision  munitions.    In  his  view, 
known  as  the  Rumsfeld  Doctrine,  wars  should  be  fought  and  won  using 
overwhelming air power, high-tech combat systems, and small, fast ground

Unlike  McNamara  and  Rumsfeld,  Harold  Brown  was  an  academic  and

to  reach  the  position,  leaving  his  post  as  President  of  the  California  Institute 
of Technology  to  take  the  job  at  the  request  of  President  Jimmy  Carter.    Prior

related research his entire career, including directing the prestigious Radiation 
Laboratory at the University of California. Like McNamara and Rumsfeld, Brown 
was  tasked  with  reforming  and  reorganizing  the  Defense  Department.    While 
all three of these Secretaries oversaw increases in military budgets, only Brown 
did so during nominal peacetime.  He took a more measured approach than 
McNamara  or  Rumsfeld,  starting  programs  that  would  take  time  to  reach 
maturation rather than rushing through extensive changes.  

In pondering the legacies of these three men in the light of conflicts of the last 
two decades, three lessons emerge:

Lesson One: Duplication and peacetime inefficiency are sometimes a good thing.

In  a  conflict  with  a  near-peer  adversary,  war  becomes  a  logistical  battle  of 
production  as  much  as  a  test  of  technology  and  battlefield  tactics.    Military 
production is an expensive process, requiring complex manufacturing facilities 
and  a  skilled  work  force,  both  of  which  take  considerable  time  and  money  to 
develop.    During  peacetime  when  needs  are  minimal  or  moderate,  paying 
for the extra overhead to keep underutilized production lines going seems 
wasteful.  However, when war comes, the lag in trying to boost production 
can be catastrophic.  We are seeing this being played out in the Ukraine conflict 
with ammunition production, as production lines were consolidated at the end 
of the cold war to lower costs, and additional production is proving difficult to 
ramp up.  Consolidation of slightly different platforms may seem more efficient, 
but as was seen with the F-111 and now again the F-35, those savings can prove 
elusive, and result in a less-capable weapon system.

Lesson Two: Advanced, even superior technology isn’t always the answer.

In the Ukraine conflict, high-tech weapons can be effective, but are proving to 
have a short life cycle as the balance between offense and defense evolves. The 
most devastating weapon in combat is one that would be recognizable by any 
soldier of the First World War. Artillery still reigns supreme on the front lines.  
In  the  Second  World  War  Nazi  Germany  redirected  critical  resources  into  the 
latest wonder weapons, resources that would have been better used to create 
more  of  their  already  reliable,  state-of-the-art  technology.    As  General  Hugh 
Shelton, Chair of the Joint Chiefs of Staff under Rumsfeld put it, Rumsfeld was 
so  focused  on  deploying  new  technologies,  he  was  creating  short-term 
vulnerabilities  while  they  were  being  implemented,  vulnerabilities  that 
became apparent during the wars in Iraq and Afghanistan. While AI powered 
drones  seem  revolutionary  and  are  likely  the  wave  of  the  future,  replacing 
“obsolete”  manned  aircraft  with  these  new  technologies  too  quickly  will  be 
disruptive, and likely contain unanticipated vulnerabilities.

Lesson Three: Effective change takes time.

It  is  ironic  that  Ronald  Reagan,  who  had  a  plaque  on  his  desk  saying  “there 
is  no  limit  to  what  a  man  can  do  or  where  he  can  go  if  he  doesn’t  mind  who 
gets  the  credit,”  is  often  given  credit  for  rebuilding  America’s  military  after 
the  disorganization  and  loss  of  morale  caused  by Vietnam.   While  the  Reagan 
Administration  deserves  credit  for  seeing  them  through  to  completion,  it 
was  under  Harold  Brown  when  the  foundation  for  rebuilding  the  U.S.  military 
was  laid.    Key  programs  such  as  stealth  research,  Multiple  Independent  Re-
entry  Vehicles,  and  cruise  missiles  that  came  to  fruition  under  the  Reagan 
Administration all began under Secretary Brown.  Because of the time needed 
for research and development, procurement, and deployment, one of the most 
painful realizations of appointed government service is that your successor 
will likely get credit for most of your best work.

helpful, the military enterprise has different metrics and driving forces than the 
business world.  In a speech at the University of Michigan shortly after leaving
