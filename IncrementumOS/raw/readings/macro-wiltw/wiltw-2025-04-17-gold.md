PERFORMANCE OF HIGH CONVICTION IDEAS

Since  September  2020,  we  have  strongly  and  repeatedly  argued  that  a  classic 
paradigm  shift  into  the  new  market  leaders  was  starting,  which  would  feature 
commodities,  commodity-related  and  inflation-sensitive  sectors,  and  many  of  the 
long-depressed markets.

In October 2020, with UST 10-year yields at 0.78%, we warned that bond yields would 
rise significantly and for a long time as the 40-year bull market in bonds transitions to a 
secular bear market.

As the Fed began raising interest rates in March 2022, we became extremely cautious, 
worrying the Fed could overdo it, causing a recession or hard landing. However, we did 
not think the Fed would be successful in defeating the secular inflationary forces so we 
retained our core positions in hard assets.

As  time  went  on,  based  on  market  action,  we  gradually  began  adding  to  those 
positions. Since summer of 2024, market action has increasingly supported this view. 
Accordingly,  we  further  increased  our  position  in  hard  assets,  specifically  gold,  silver 
and  their  mining  stocks;  copper;  large-cap  global  commodity  producers;  the  oil  and 
gas sector.

For the past two years, the great question among Western investors has been: “Is China 
uninvestable?” As long-term contrarians, that is music to our ears! We believe Chinese 
stocks are in the early stages of a secular bull market.

Stagflation  risks  are  increasing.  An  April  12th  article  published  by  CNBC, 
headlined “Trump  tariffs  on  China  will  soon  bring ‘irreversible’  damage  to 
many  American  businesses,”  warned  that  canceled  orders  and  abandoned 
freight from China are becoming increasingly frequent, especially with respect 
to home furnishings, toys, apparel, and footwear, among other imported goods.

The article also quoted Stephen Lamar, CEO of the American Apparel & Footwear 
Association, as follows: “With prohibitively high tariff levels on U.S. imports from 
China,  many  companies  have  no  choice  but  to  cancel  orders.  The  constant 
switchbacking  means  new  tariff  costs  are  not  accurately  presented  or 
predictable  until  the  goods  arrive  at  the  port,  and  the  high  rates  are 
generating bills that can’t be paid. That is not a risk or burden small business 
can sustain.”

Although  the  potential  economic  damage  has  deflationary  implications,  the 
longer-term inflationary implications are perhaps more significant. This includes 
both  the  higher  cost  of  imported  goods  subject  to  tariffs  and  the  increasing  costs 
associated with establishing new domestic manufacturing facilities, especially labor 
and  materials.  Stockpiling  in  advance  may  also  confuse  market  signals,  as 
the recent strength in retail sales appears to have been partly driven by people 
buying big-ticket durable goods, especially autos, before tariffs kick-in.

We  have  been  warning  for  years  that  the  aging  population  is  contributing 
to  labor  shortages,  and  that  this  portends  higher  inflation  in  the  future. 
(See WILTW October 28, 2021.) A recent CNBC survey of 380 respondents from 
businesses  across  the  supply  chain  revealed  that  57%  said  that  cost  was  the 
biggest impediment to reshoring supply chains, followed by 21% who cited 
the shortage of skilled labor. 

For every five baby boomers that retire from skilled trades, there are only 
two  replacements  from  younger  generations—a  huge  mismatch  that  will 
worsen  as  immigrant  deportations  accelerate.  The  lack  of  home-grown 
replacements  largely  reflects  changes  in  social  and  educational  norms  that 
placed a large premium on getting a college degree for service-based industries, 
as opposed to learning hands-on trades and working in factories—tasks which 
historically were dominated by men. 

The  problem  is  particularly  acute  in  mining.  The  number  of  graduates  from 
U.S.  mining  programs  dropped  nearly  40%  between  2016  and  2022,  and 
the  nation’s  mining  and  engineering  schools  graduate  fewer  than  350 
people  per  year.  There  were  only  600  students  enrolled  in  these  programs  in 
2022, down from 1,500 in 2015. According to the Society for Mining, Metallurgy 
and Exploration, over 200,000 U.S. mining workers are expected to retire by 
decade’s end, resulting in a tremendous loss of skills and experience, and 
far fewer people to replace them. (See WILTW May 5, 2022.)

In  1980,  the  male  labor-force  participation  rate  was  over  77%,  but  it  has 
been on a decades-long decline and now stands below 68%. This comes with 
an interesting contradiction: while labor-force participation among men is near 
its  historic  low  registered  during  Covid  (see  blue  line  in  the  chart  below),  the 
number  of  job  openings  in  manufacturing  and  construction—while  down 
from its post-pandemic high—is still higher than it was during most of the 
decade before Covid. (See green dotted line.)

In  other  words,  there  are  ample  jobs  in  manufacturing  and  construction 
available  but  few  people  who  actually  want  to  work  them,  or  are  willing 
to  move  to  take  advantage  of  them,  or  are  qualified  to  fill  them.  As  Jay 
Timmons, head of the National Association of Manufacturers, told CBS News in 
January 2023: “We have more jobs than we have people for…I never thought 
I'd be able to say that…But now it's kind of an all hands on deck. We've got to 
fill these jobs that are open.”

Making  matters  even  worse,  the  relative  tightness  of  available  labor  is 
compounded  by  the  risk  of  job  loss  to  automation.  In  the  CNBC  reshoring 
survey  discussed  above,  over  80%  of  respondents  said  that  automation 
would be employed more than humans in any new facilities. Therefore, from 
a worker’s perspective, not only are these jobs less desirable—especially if they 
involve repetitive tasks or physically-demanding conditions—but they are likely 
to be eliminated by technology improvements. 

In  addition  to  the  inflationary  impact  of  tighter  labor  supply,  domestic 
construction costs are also becoming prohibitive. As the following left-hand 
chart shows, manufacturing construction costs rose 64%, to $373 per square foot, 
between  2019  and  2024,  and  are  poised  to  go  up  even  further  after  Washington’s 
newest  tariffs  on  aluminum  and  steel.  Note  that  the  PPI  for  iron  and  steel 
already gained 9% between January and March alone, as shown in the right-
hand chart below. Think of what happens to these costs if multiple companies 
decide to reshore operations at the same time.

In the CNBC reshoring survey, 65% of respondents said that the cost of building 
a new domestic supply chain would be double or more than double the current 
cost. Some 61% of respondents said that it would be more cost-effective to 
move  supply  chains  to  lower-tariffed  countries  rather  than  move  them  to 
the U.S.

In  addition  to  rising  construction  costs,  lead  times  for  constructing  plants  run 
anywhere  from  three  to  ten  years—plenty  of  time  for  trade  policy  to  change 
again.  As  Mark  Wakefield,  partner  and  managing  director  of  the  AlixPartners 
consultancy, told the Financial Times: “The building of a plant will take years 
and then it’s going to be around decades so you have to have a very long 
term view of where regimes will go.”

These  trends  are  occurring  in  an  environment  that  has  already  seen  costs  for 
food  staples  rising  sharply.  The  loss  of  honeybee  colonies  is  likely  to  add 
even more pressure to this trend, as discussed in section 6.

Asset  allocators  have  plenty  to  think  about  as  these  new  trade  dynamics  unfold. 
Although  many  market  watchers  have  dismissed  the  recent  spike  in  Treasury 
yields  as  the  fault  of  hedge-fund  repositioning,  the  underlying  inflationary 
impact  of  the  new  trade  policy  is  likely  contributing  to  the  rise  in  yields, 
which has been going on since early March. (See WILTW April 10, 2025.)

We have frequently pointed out that despite not paying any income, gold has 
outperformed  U.S.  Treasuries  for  several  years.  Yet,  North  American  investors

As the trade war intensifies, the risks of holding USTs will become more apparent. 
Even if foreign countries merely back-away from purchasing USTs, the effect could 
be  devastating,  because  Washington’s  debt  rollovers  require  continued  inflows 
to keep interest rates from spiking higher. But if countries like China decide to 
diversify  their  FX  holdings  to  avert  potential  sanctions  from Washington, 
the impact on USTs could potentially be catastrophic. Caveat emptor.

This  risk  of  owning  USTs  is  likely  to  intensify  as  the  White  House  exerts 
pressure  on  Jay  Powell  to  cut  interest  rates,  even  though  doing  so  would  fuel 
inflationary pressure. But if the economy decelerates sharply, after the pre-
tariff  inventory-boost  fades,  the  Fed  may  have  little  choice  but  to  loosen 
monetary policy, as Hobson’s choice continues to play out.

And we anticipate China will continue placing restrictions on exports or rare earths 
and  other  specialty  metals  and  minerals,  which  could  be  hugely  disruptive  and 
inflationary in their own right. (See section 4.) China has already restricted exports 
of  antimony,  which  we  have  discussed  in  prior  issues.  (See  WILTW  January  9, 
2025.) This underscores the rationale behind our favorite junior gold mining 
stock,  Southern  Cross  Gold  Consolidated  (SXG  CN,  C$4.40;  SX2  AU,  A$5.57), 
which is why we have given it a very high weighting of 41% in the 13D Gold 
HGAX Index.

What are the implications?

“Have humans passed peak brain power?” John Burn-Murdoch, The Financial 
Times’ journalist, wondered?

It’s a poignant, worthy question. But it’s also one that assumes that mathematical 
skills  and  reasoning  are  the  sum  total  of  human  intelligence.  It’s  true  that 
mathematical  reasoning  and  problem-solving  are  highly-prized  skills  in 
our  society—important  for  doing  well  on  SATs,  getting  into  top  colleges, 
and landing prestigious white-collar jobs in finance, consulting, and tech. 

But  they’re  also  the  very  skills  most  at  risk  of  being  replaced  by  AI. 
Algorithms  and  doom-scrolling  have  already  chipped  away  at  our  cognitive 
abilities,  as  the  charts  above  indicate.  AI  will  pour  gas  on  this  trend—a

This year’s staggering loss of domestic bees follows years of escalating losses.

The annual overwintering loss of U.S. bee colonies has surged from an average

Florida  beekeepers  experienced  losses  of  up  to  90%,  with  neonicotinoid

quality habitat, hotter temperatures, and parasites. There is also new  evidence 
that  increasingly  ubiquitous  LED  lighting  may  be  disrupting  the  circadian

One  of  the  most  significant  variables  in  this  landscape  is  artificial  intelligence.

freedom  from  preconception. This  attitude  is  difficult  to  maintain  in  a  culture 
that rewards expertise and identity rooted in mastery. People become wedded 
to what they are best at. Yet letting go of fixed expectations allows for fresh 
insight, deeper learning, creativity, and innovation. In this sense, the most 
valuable educational posture is a willingness to not know.

In  a  world  that  demands  constant  adaptation,  cultivating  a  beginner’s  mind

As  technology  accelerates  and  the  future  becomes  harder  to  predict, 
openness—not expertise—is the foundation of real success.

Possible?
Europe 
is  gearing  up  for  a  massive  campaign  of  military  rearmament, 
allocating  hundreds  of  billions  of  euros  to  the  task  (WILTW  April  10,  2025). 
While financing is of course a necessary component of a military build up, it is 
only one of the required elements of a successful military expansion. A nation 
or  alliance  must  have  the  production  capacity  —  a  combination  of  traditional 
heavy  manufacturing  and  high-tech  industries  that  increasingly  drive  modern 
weapons.  It  must  also  have,  or  obtain  access  to,  the  raw  materials  to  fuel  and 
feed  those  industries.  Finally,  it  needs  trained  personnel  —  both  to  make  the 
weapons  and  use  them. The  rearmament  push  is  largely  driven  in  response  to 
the perceived threat from Russia and fears of abandonment by the U.S., and is 
intended to make Europe a world power in the field of defense. Even if Europe 
can adequately fund this campaign, can it organize the industry, resources, 
and personnel to successfully compete with Russia in the military sphere?

and/or  combat  effectiveness  is  vastly  improved,  Europe  would  have  to 
spend upwards of 10% of GDP  — nearly $2 Trillion — on defense to have 
any hope of competing with Russia on its own. 

Raw Materials

One of the first challenges facing Europeans in rearming are the raw materials 
and  energy  needed  to  power  the  industry  it  takes  to  transform  them  into 
weapons.  In  the  past  century,  European  nations  such  as  France  and  the 
UK  had  extensive  overseas  colonies  to  provide  both  raw  materials  and 
manpower. With the loss of these colonies over the last century, Europe must 
resort  to  domestic  extraction  and  production,  or  obtain  these  resources  on 
the  increasingly  restrictive  and  costly  global  markets.  While  in  1900  Europe 
accounted  for  40%  of  global  metal  ores,  today  that  figure  is  under  3%.  The  high 
cost  of  labor  and  extensive  environmental  laws  has  driven  many  companies 
out  of  business.  Even  for  the  most  basic  and  common  ores,  such  as  iron  ore, 
European  production  has  crashed,  and  former  colonies  are  less  interested  in 
exporting raw materials, preferring instead to reserve them to support domestic 
manufacturing and creating higher value goods for export.

Industry and Technology

As with energy and mining of raw materials, heavy manufacturing has been on 
the decline all across Europe for decades for a variety of reasons. Until recently 
Germany  maintained  its  position  as  a  manufacturing  leader,  however,  the  loss 
of cheap energy sources has increased the cost of manufacturing. German steel, 
the heart of European production, has dropped significantly since 2022. Before 
military production can ramp up, core industries will need to be restarted 
or  restored  from  scratch,  and  lower  cost  energy  sources  will  need  to  be 
secured. It is unlikely that “Green” energy will be able to supply the high energy 
needs  of  manufacturing,  therefore  the  only  options  are  nuclear,  coal,  or  fossil 
fuels – all of which have extensive opposition and/or limited availability across 
Europe. 

But there is another issue: the technology of war is specialized, and experience 
in  commercial  production  may  not  transfer  to  the  military  world.  A  recent 
headline  in  Spiegel  says  it  all:  “German  weapons  systems  apparently 
only  partially  capable  of  war.”    Many  European-produced  systems  appear 
formidable on the surface, but in practice have proven ineffective in the field. 

A good example are the European artillery systems that allow a single artillery 
piece  to  rapidly  fire  several  rounds  within  a  few  seconds,  using  the  angle  of 
fire  and  travel  time  such  that  they  all  arrive  on  the  target  simultaneously. This 
is  an  impressive  capability  on  paper,  allowing  a  single  self-propelled  artillery 
system  to replace multiple other  units. The problem is that the barrels in such 
systems are expensive, wear out rapidly, and cannot be replaced in the field. In 
addition, in the era of drones, these larger systems are easier to see and target, 
and  have  had  short  survival  times  on  the  front  lines.  Russia  is  using  larger 
numbers  of  simpler,  robust,  often  towed  artillery  systems  to  accomplish 
nearly the same thing with higher reliability, easier servicing, and reduced 
vulnerability at a lower cost. Although it has learned from the Ukraine conflict, 
Europe has a steep learning curve with respect to systems designed for modern 
war.

compared to their numbers at the height of the Cold War. 

The  Bundeswehr  (German  military)  is  both  shrinking  in  size  and  increasing 
in  average  age  despite  increasingly  aggressive  recruiting.  From  a  Cold  War 
strength  of  500,000,  the  Bundeswehr  is  down  to  only  181,000  today,  of  which

today are equal to roughly two months of men volunteering to serve in the 
Russian army. There has been discussion of the need to re-institute a draft, but 
conscription would overwhelm the available training sites and equipment, and 
the majority of Germans oppose resumption of mandatory military service. 

Other European nations are in similar dire straits, although the eastern European 
nations  such  as  Poland  are  faring  better.  Poland  is  the  third  largest  military  in 
NATO  (behind  the  U.S.  and Turkey),  with  over  216,000  personnel.  Last  month, 
Poland announced a large-scale program with the goal that every adult male in 
the country will have at least some military training. But elsewhere the situation 
is grim with respect to being able to field combat ready forces any time in the 
near future.

Summary

It  will  be  extremely  difficult  for  Europe  to  create  a  cost-effective  arms 
industry  and  military  force  without  drastic  spending  increases  and  even 
more  drastic  restructuring  of  its  military  forces  and  approach  to  defense. 
The  fragmentation  created  by  over  30  nations  each  with  separate  armed 
forces  and  defense  industries  presents  severe  problems  with  respect  to  force 
structure  and  production  efficiency  that  money  alone  cannot  solve.  As  Robert 
A Heinlein wrote, “The most expensive thing in the world is a second best military 
establishment,  good  but  not  good  enough  to  win.”    Unless  it  can  address  the 
many  structural,  resource,  and  technology  problems  it  faces,  Europe  may  find 
itself having spent billions with marginal improvement in their military position. 
Ultimately, Europe must ask itself if massive arms spending is the best path 
to security.

In 2024, fueled by surging copper and gold output and disciplined cost controls, 
Zijin posted record-breaking results, with revenue hitting 303.6 billion yuan and 
net profit rising 51.8% year-on-year to 32.1 billion yuan. 

The  copper  business  remains  the  cornerstone  of  the  company’s  portfolio,

copper output has grown at a staggering compound annual growth rate (CAGR)

Gold  is  equally  critical  to  Zijin’s  portfolio.  Zijin  produced  72.9  tons  of  mined

sixth-largest gold producer globally. In terms of the gross profit contribution, 
gold mining contributed more than 35% of its total gross profit in the first quarter. 

Zijin has set ambitious production growth goals: producing 1.15 million tons 
of copper and 85 tons of gold in 2025, with plans to reach 1.6 million tons of 
copper (up over 50% from 1.07 million tons in 2024) and 100-110 tons of gold 
by 2028 (up over 50% from 73 tons in 2024). These targets are underpinned by a 
diverse portfolio of world-class assets entering their prime. 

The  Kamoa-Kakula  copper  mine  in  the  DRC,  where  the  Phase  III  expansion, 
completed  in  June  2024,  will  boost  annual  output  to  over  600,000  tons.  This 
positions the mine as the world’s third-largest copper operation. Kamoa-Kakula 
is 39.6% owned by Zijin and 39.6% owned by Ivanhoe Mines (IVN CN, C$12.45).

making Julong the world’s largest high-altitude copper operation, proving 
that even low-grade deposits can be profitable with advanced technology.

Zijin’s rise to prominence owes much to its contrarian acquisition strategy.
