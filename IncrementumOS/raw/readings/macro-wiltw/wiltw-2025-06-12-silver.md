China’s export restrictions on permanent 
magnets have become the sword of 
Damocles hanging over Western industries. 
What are the implications? How to invest?
Restrictions  are  still  effectively  in  force  and  are  causing  widespread  panic, 
particularly  across  the  automotive  industry.  Even  in  the  unlikely  scenario  of 
completely  restoring  the  availability  of  permanent  magnets,  trust  in  the  supply 
chain has disintegrated. 

We have written for years about the potential impact of the rare-earth and 
permanent-magnet  supply  squeeze  (see  reports).  The  supply  squeeze  is  now 
here, and it is here to stay. Chinese customs data shows a 51% drop in the nation's 
permanent-magnet exports in April, the first month of restrictions.

It  is  becoming  increasingly  clear  that  without  access  to  China’s  supply  of 
permanent  magnets,  developed  nations  will  risk  systemic  collapse  within 
months.

In WILTW May 29, 2025, we argued that disruptions to the West's supply of REEs 
originating  in  China  could  lead  to  a  commodity  shock  affecting  several  key 
industries, quickly bringing production to a halt. Since then, several European 
and  U.S.  automotive  industry  associations  have  publicly  stated  there  is  a 
serious and real-time risk that the industry is collapsing. 

Several auto plants have shut down already. Ford has stopped production of the 
Explorer  SUV,  while  Suzuki  has  suspended  production  of  the  Swift.  BMW  has 
also reported disruptions across its network. One European rare-earth importer, 
Noble Elements, warns: “There is no solution for the next three years except to come 
to an agreement with China.”

Aerospace manufacturers, semiconductor companies, military contractors, 
and  consumer  goods  manufacturers  are  also  vulnerable  to  this  ongoing 
supply  squeeze.  According  to  the  New  York  Times, “Five  American  industrial  or 
technology  companies  said  they  would  run  out  of  rare  earth  materials  and  face 
disruption to their operations in less than a month and four more in less than three 
months.”

The  Geneva  truce  of  May  12th  looks  to  be  broken  on  both  sides.  Earlier 
this  week,  Sino-U.S.  trade  talks  were  held  in  London.  After  concluding  late  on 
Tuesday night, U.S. Secretary of Commerce Howard Lutnick  announced that a 
“framework  for  implementation”  had  been  reached,  adding:  “we  do  absolutely 
expect that the topic of rare earth minerals and magnets… will be resolved in this 
framework implementation.” There has been no formal readout of the meeting, 
nor formal ratification by both Presidents Trump or Xi. 

However,  the  extent  to  which  China  has  eased  restrictions  on  critical 
minerals  and  which  technology  exports  the  U.S.  will  resume  remains  to 
be  seen.  It  is  not  in  Beijing’s  interest  to  hand  out  licenses  for  rare-earths  and 
permanent magnets. Rare-earth export licenses are their leverage in this escalating 
tech-arms war.

Domestic  political  pressure  is  mounting  on  the  Trump  Administration 
to  reach  an  agreement  with  Beijing.  Several  leading  companies  in  four  key 
industries reliant on permanent magnets – defense, aerospace, automotive, and 
robotics – have significant operations in Republican states. 

A  Bloomberg  article  noted  that  the  Trump  Administration  is  willing  to 
ease  restrictions  on  some  tech  exports,  specifically,  some  categories  of 
jet  engine  parts,  and  nuclear 
semiconductors,  chip-design  technology, 
materials,  in  exchange  for  reliable  REE  shipments. The  quick  change  in  stance 
is noteworthy, given that President Trump retaliated to China’s restrictions last 
month by revoking visas for Chinese foreign students and banning the export of 
U.S.-chip electronic design automation (EDA) software to Chinese firms.

The  world,  especially  the  U.S.,  is  beginning  to  realize  that  circumventing 
China  in  fulfilling  its  REE  and  permanent-magnet  demand  is  nearly 
impossible. China has the “ace in hand,” and is playing it well.

early  2010s.  Tens  of  thousands  of  jobs  were  lost,  and  Chinese  companies 
stepped  in.  By  the  early  2020s,  95%  of  Germany’s  solar  industry  was  Chinese-

difficult.  At  any  point,  regardless  of  U.S.-China  negotiations  this  week, 
China can stop the flow of rare-earths and permanent-magnets to Western 
industries.  Maximum  leverage—a  full  ban—would  have  catastrophic 
consequences for Western societies.

  History  demonstrates  that  dependence  on  a  single  supplier  for  vital 
resources,  like  oil  or  rare  earths,  can  lead  to  severe  economic  shocks. 
The  1973  OPEC  oil  embargo  sparked  stagflation  and  industrial  decline  in 
the  U.S.,  particularly  in  the  automotive  industry.  Similarly,  following  China's 
restricted rare-earth exports in 2010, Japan’s urgent diversification of its supply 
chain  underscored  the  importance  of  securing  alternative  sources  to  prevent 
future disruptions. As a result, Japan’s import-reliance on China for rare earths 
has dropped from 90% in 2010 to 60% today. 

  America  still  lacks  the  midstream  separation  and  processing  capacity 
to  onshore  its  rare-earth  supply  chain.  We  have  also  argued  that  the 
midstream  processing  capacity—the  “missing  middle”—in  the  U.S.  and 
European  Union  is  the  key  to  addressing  this  critical  single  point  of  failure 
(see reports). 

Will the U.S. be able to scale up its rare-earth midstream before the sword 
falls? According to the Center for Strategic International Studies: “It’s a perfectly 
solvable  problem  and  one  the  U.S.  is  working  at  warp  speed  to  address.  It's  not  a 
forever problem.” However, even at an accelerated rate, if everything goes right, it 
will literally take years for the West to stand up an end-to-end rare-earths supply 
chain independent of China. Until then, China will maintain the upper hand, and 
companies will pay a premium to secure access to critical minerals.

Has silver entered its “high-speed zone?” 
Silver has broken-out to a 13-year high, and 
if history is any guide, future breakouts will 
come frequently and rapidly, as physical 
investment demand picks up. Consolidation 
in silver mining is gaining steam, pointing 
toward greater capital discipline in the sector. 
We  have  argued  repeatedly  that  when  gold  is  in  a  bull  market,  silver  eventually 
follows  the  yellow  metal  higher—perhaps  with  a  later  start—but  silver  usually 
outpaces gold’s gain in percentage terms. As we outlined in WILTW November 7, 
2024,  from  1974  to  1980,  silver  rose  1,419%,  which  was  double  the  721% 
increase  posted  by  gold.  From  2001  to  2008,  silver  rose  413%,  versus  gold’s 
gain  of  292%.  And  from  2007  to  2012,  silver  rose  441%,  while  gold  rose 
167%.

With  this  history  in  mind,  we  found  this  chart  from  last  Sunday’s  WATMTU 
hugely noteworthy, because if silver is entering a “high-speed zone,” it could 
increase at a pace that shocks market participants, as we saw when it went 
from $5 in 2003 to almost $50 in 2011. As Fred Hickey, author of The High-Tech 
Strategist  newsletter,  posted  on  X  on  June  6th:  “Silver  can  become  a  wild 
animal once it ‘breaks out.’”

The  fundamental  structural  mine-supply  deficit  has  been  building  for  years, 
as we have often argued. The following chart nicely summarizes the data from 
The  Silver  Institute  we  published  in  May.  The  key  messages  to  take  from  this 
are  1)  the  secular  historical  decline  in  silver-mine  production,  which  has 
fallen 80 million ounces since 2016; 2) the persistent shortfall of mine supply 
relative  to  total  supply  (which  has  long  been  a  feature  of  the  market);  and  3) 
the narrowing gap between mine supply and industrial demand, in which 
electronics and solar/photovoltaics (PV) are the fastest-growing segments.

Industrial demand is expected to be equivalent to over 81% of total mine supply this 
year, up sharply from 54.6% in 2016. And since industrial users tend to be the most 
price-inelastic,  the  fundamental  rationale  for  higher  silver  prices  is  baked  into  the 
cake, short of a global recession.

In  its  2025  World  Silver  Survey,  the  Silver  Institute  elaborated  on  the  recycling 
outlook, as follows: “With near-market stocks of high-grade material in many 
markets  now  depleted,  jewelry,  silverware  and  coin  scrap  could  stagnate 
this  year.  Together  with  ongoing  losses  in  photography  and  a  slight  dip  for 
industrial recycling, global scrap this year may flatline despite the price rally.”

Over  20  years  ago,  we  frequently  wrote  about  the  risk  and  opportunity  that 
digital photography posed for silver. (See September 3, 2003 report and WILTW 
September  25,  2003.)  Digital  photography  gradually  displaced  traditional 
photography, a key end market for silver—but this also undermined a key 
source  of  recycled  silver.  Now  that  traditional  photography  has  been  pushed 
down  to  a  negligible  level,  under  30  million  ounces,  that  critical  source  of  scrap 
supply has practically disappeared.

We  suspect  that  industrial  users  of  silver  are  going  to  become  very  interested 
in  scrap  supply  moving  forward,  because  growth  in  mine  supply  is  going  to 
become increasingly challenging to generate. As we wrote in the May 1st WILTW: 
“Because more than 70% of mine supply is derived as a byproduct of gold, 
copper,  lead  and  zinc  mining,  the  secular  downward  trend  of  investment 
in  those  metals,  which  we  have  warned  of  for  years,  will  also  have  great 
bearing on the amount of silver that is ultimately produced.”

The  blue  line  in  the  following  chart  illustrates  that  the  percentage  of  silver 
output  from  primary  silver  mines  is  in  secular  decline,  falling  from  over 
32% a decade ago to less than 28% last year. These trends spell tight supplies 
for industrial users far into the future.

Thorium  is  a  naturally  occurring  element  that  cannot  initiate  a  nuclear 
reaction  by  itself.  To  use  thorium  in  nuclear  reactors,  a  small  amount  of 
fissile material, such as uranium, is needed to kickstart the reaction. Thorium 
is  more  plentiful  than  uranium,  and  when  utilized  in  this  type  of  reactor, 
it  generates  less  long-lived  radioactive  waste  (see  WILTW  August  22, 
2024).  Once  thorium  technology  matures,  which  may  take  years,  nuclear 
power could become the mainstay energy generation source for the world, 
potentially increasing total uranium demand.

When can the U.S. President legally send in 
the troops to an American city?
The  unrest  over  the  push  for  stronger  immigration  enforcement  by  the Trump 
Administration has raised questions over the use of the U.S. Military inside the 
United  States.  Most of the laws and Supreme Court rulings in this area are 
from  the  early  days  of  the  Republic,  with  little  clarification  since  the  Civil 
War. Under these laws, the President has broad authority, which the courts and 
Congress have been reluctant to restrict in the modern era, so while there have 
been calls for reform, the U.S. President has significant power to “send in the 
troops.”  

A  key  campaign  promise  by  President  Trump  was  to  deport  illegal 
immigrants.  Enforcement  raids  across  the  country  have  triggered  protests  in 
recent  days,  some  of  which  have  become  increasingly  disruptive  and  violent. 
Although  they  are  spreading  across  the  country,  the  focal  point  is  Southern 
California,  where  the  Governor  of  California  and  Mayor  of  Los  Angeles  have 
refused  to  cooperate  with  the  Federal  enforcement  and,  according  to  the 
Administration,  even  interfered  with  them.  This  refusal  by  local  officials  has 
placed Federal and local law enforcement on opposite sides of the issue. To provide 
protection for Federal immigration officers and facilities, a task that would 
typically  be  the  responsibility  of  local  authorities,  the  Administration  has 
turned to the U.S. Military to provide additional support.

The  use  of  military  forces  for  most  domestic  law  enforcement  purposes  in  the 
U.S. is governed by a complex set of laws that over time have accumulated many 
exceptions  and  provisions.  Yet,  the  core  authority  of  the  President  to  deploy 
troops inside the U.S. is still governed by laws largely defined in the late 1700s 
through  the  American  Civil  War  and  Reconstruction  era  that  ended  in  1871. 
To  understand  what  the  President  can  and  cannot  do,  we  must  first  look  at  the 
structure  of  the  U.S.  Military.  The  U.S.  Military  has  three  components,  two  of 
which are under Presidential authority, and one of which is normally under

The  third  component  of  the  military  is  the  National  Guard.  Each  of  the  fifty 
states, Puerto Rico, and other territories, has its own National Guard under the 
control of the Governor. Supplied and trained by the Defense Department, they 
are  under  the  control  of  their  Governor  unless  “Federalized;”  in  other  words, 
transferred  to  Presidential  control.  A  governor  can  use  the  National  Guard  for 
any law enforcement purpose within his own state at will,  drawing  on  the  police 
powers  granted  by  state  or  territorial  constitutions. “Federalization”  is  solely  at 
the discretion of the President, and does not require permission of the governor. 
But  once  “Federalized”  the  National  Guard  is  subject  to  the  same  laws 
and  regulations  that  govern  the  Active  Duty  and  Reserves.  This  is  a  core 
argument  of  California,  that  the  Administration  has  no  legal  right  to  use  the 
California Guard or Federal Troops for law enforcement. 

The use of the Active Duty or Reserve Military (and Federalized National Guard) 
for  anything  other  than  repelling  an  invader  is  controlled  by  two  sections 
of  Federal  Law. The  first  law  is  known  as  the “Posse  Comitatus  Act.”    Passed  in 
1878,  18  U.S.  Code  §  1385    is  surprisingly  short  for  a  Federal  law  and  is  only 
one  sentence  long.  Posse  Comitatus  prohibits  using  Federal  troops  to 
enforce  the  law  unless  specifically  authorized  by  Congress.  Note  that  this 
only applies to Federal troops; the National Guard may be used by a governor 
to enforce a State law, but once “Federalized,” the Posse Comitatus Act applies 
and they can no longer be legally used in that role. The Act is also vague with 
respect to what is meant by “the law” – Federal, state, or both? However, there 
is a major loophole. 

The  second  section  of  Federal  Law  is  commonly  called  the “Insurrection  Act.”  
This is somewhat of a misnomer, in modern U.S. law the Insurrection Act of 1807 
and other laws have been updated to modern legal language and consolidated 
under Sections 251 to 255 of Title 10 of the United States Code. These provisions 
are an amalgam of laws passed between 1792 and 1871. The original law goes 
back  before  1807,  to  the  earliest  days  of  the  Republic  and  its  first  President, 
George Washington. Passed in 1792 as the Militia Acts, they were originally 
designed to allow the President to call out the Militia and place them under 
Federal  control.  President    Washington  used  the  acts  in  1794  to  suppress 
the Whiskey  Rebellion,  and  over  time  these  acts  were  reworded  as  the  Militia 
evolved into the modern National Guard.

example of how the divided American system of government that split powers

strained to meet the challenges of the 21st.

The importance of teen love.
Teen  relationships  are  often  dismissed  by  adults  as  fleeting,  overly  emotional, 
or  inconsequential. Yet,  research  in  developmental  psychology  tells  a  different 
story—one  that  underscores  the  formative  power  of  romantic  connection 
during adolescence. Teenagers are in the midst of constructing their identities, 
and  being  in  a  relationship  provides  an  essential  mirror.  Through  emotional 
feedback, negotiation, and shared experience, teens gain insight into who 
they  are  and  how  they  relate  to  others.  Far  from  being  a  distraction,  teen 
love  serves  as  a  training  ground  for  intimacy.  It  helps  build  emotional  literacy, 
communication  skills,  and  relational  resilience—tools  that  are  increasingly 
scarce  in  a  world  of  digital  disconnection.  It’s  time  we  stop  minimizing  teen 
relationships and start recognizing their psychological value. 

As  of  2015,  35%  of  U.S.  teens  reported  having  been  in  a  romantic 
relationship. However, a 2024 survey by the Survey Center on American Life 
revealed that 40% of Gen Z men had no romantic experience during their 
teen  years.  A  2025  article  in  The  Atlantic,  titled, “Teens  Are  Forgoing  a  Classic 
Rite of Passage,” underscored this shift. “It’s scary,” the article notes, “but it can be 
interesting, and beautiful when it’s good, and sometimes formative even when 
it doesn’t stay good.” 

Indeed, not all teen relationships are healthy. Adolescents are emotionally 
inexperienced,  and  their  relationships  can  be  intense,  volatile,  or  even 
harmful. Some experiences may leave lasting psychological scars. Parents and 
caregivers  must  remain  alert  to  signs  of  toxic  or  abusive  dynamics,  especially 
given the social pressures teens face, including pressure to engage in behaviors 
they  may  not  be  emotionally  ready  for.  Still,  it’s  important  to  resist  the 
impulse  to  forbid  dating  entirely.  When  teens  are  met  with  authoritarian 
restrictions,  they  are  more  likely  to  rebel.  Open  dialogue,  built  on  trust  and 
respect, is far more effective than blanket prohibitions. 

Empirical  research  shows  that  when  teen  relationships  are  healthy,  they  can 
offer  significant  long-term  benefits.  Studies  have  found  that “the  quality  of 
adolescent  romantic  relationships  can  have  long-lasting  effects  on  self-
esteem  and  mental  health.  Youthful  relationships  shape  personal  values 
toward romance, intimate relationships, and sexuality.” A 2019 meta-analysis of

Losing yourself to be liked: the cost of 
people-pleasing.
People-pleasing  is  a  behavior  that  most  of  us  engage  in  to  form  and  maintain 
social  bonds.  It  stems  from  a  desire  for  connection,  approval,  and  harmony. 
But  like  any  psychological  tendency,  it  can  become  maladaptive  when  taken 
too  far.  While  people-pleasers  are  often  well-liked,  their  approval-seeking 
can  come  at  a  significant  cost—neglecting  their  own  needs,  boundaries, 
and  authenticity.  Over  time,  this  self-suppression  can  lead  to  resentment, 
burnout,  and  even  fractured  relationships.  So  where  is  the  line  between 
healthy cooperation and harmful self-abandonment? What are the long-term 
psychological  consequences  of  chronic  people-pleasing,  and  how  can  we 
begin to reclaim agency and self-respect? 

If you have ever felt an overwhelming need for others to like you, found yourself 
over-apologizing  for  things  beyond  your  control,  or  struggled  to  express  your 
honest  opinions  for  fear  of  upsetting  someone,  you  may  be  a  people-pleaser.

to  others’  moods  while  suppressing  their  own.  But  people-pleasing  can

Three  years  of  continuous  fires  in  Canada  and  Russia’s  northern  forests

earlier and is often drier and hotter, creating ideal conditions for wildfires. 

Around the world, wildfire seasons are now 27% longer than they were 40 
years ago, primarily due to hotter temperatures, according to a 2022 study.

We quote Matt Hansen, a remote sensing scientist at the University of Maryland:

We’re  seeing  unprecedented  forest  loss  from  fire.  This  will  severely  test  our 
ability to maintain intact forests within a warming climate. 

Tropical and boreal forests store approximately 1.5 trillion tons of carbon. Last 
year’s  wildfires  in  South  America,  Canada,  and  Russia  emitted  4.1  billion 
tons of CO2, surpassing India’s 2.9 billion tons in 2023. 

Canada’s  record  wildfire  year  in  2023  emitted  at  least  2.4  billion  tons  of  CO2. 
That is four times more than Canada’s annual emissions. 

Wildfire  has  become  akin  to  a  top-five-CO2-emitting  country.  And,  in  an 
ever  hotter,  drier  climate,  wildfire  emissions  will  grow,  and  will  be  very 
difficult to curtail. 

Forests help regulate the weather, recycle water, and provide homes for millions 
of species of plants and animals. For instance, the Amazon Rainforest drives the 
South  American  Monsoon,  which  brings  much  of  South  America  its  rain.  The 
monsoon is declining largely because 20% to 25% of the Amazon has been 
deforested or degraded. 

Decreased  rainfall  due  to  the  declining  monsoon  results  in  more  fires  in  the 
Amazon, further degrading the forest in a feedback loop, as we have previously 
noted. (See WILTW October 12, 2023.)

In  2021,  more  than  100  world  leaders,  representing  over  85%  of  the 
planet’s forests, agreed to halt and reverse forest loss by 2030. Annual forest 
loss would have to fall by 20% each year from 2024 levels to reach that goal.

Clearing of tropical forest for agriculture has long been the main driver of 
deforestation and was the second-largest cause in 2024. Forest loss needs to 
be decoupled from the increased production of food, energy, minerals, as well 
as urban growth, to meet the goal, according to the World Resources Institute.

Reducing  illegal  logging,  mining,  and  agricultural  conversion  is  also  essential. 
At the same time, more funding for forest protection and restoration is needed. 
Brazil  will  propose  a  Tropical  Forest  Forever  Facility,  a  proposed  $125  billion 
international  fund  to  reward  countries  for  conserving  and  restoring  tropical 
forests at the upcoming climate COP 30 later this year.

Finally,  significantly  more  investment  is  needed  to  prevent  and  combat 
wildfires in the pyrocene era.
