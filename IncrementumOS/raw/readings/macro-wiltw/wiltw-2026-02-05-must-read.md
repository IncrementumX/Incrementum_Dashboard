Prior periods of productivity growth were characterized by strong growth 
in  payroll  employment,  which  is  not  the  case  at  present.  GDP  growth  is 
a  function  of  labor-force  growth  and  productivity  growth,  and  labor-force 
growth is a function of demographics and immigration. During the 1947-1973 
productivity boom, nonfarm payrolls grew by 1.3 million/year, or 2.2% annually, 
off a small starting base of 44.6 million. During the IT boom between the mid-
1990s and the GFC, nonfarm payrolls grew by 1.7 million, or 1.4% annually, off a 
larger base of 116.2 million. This growth helped drive real GDP gains and higher 
living standards during those periods.

But  the  labor  situation  is  far  different  today,  as  the  combination  of 
immigration  constraints,  retiring  baby  boomers  and  the  under-20 
demographic  cliff  create  a  perfect  storm.  (See  WILTW  September  4,  2025.) 
If  labor-force  growth  slows  to  a  trickle,  it  will  have  negative  consequences  for

but  because  it  has  developed  a  uniquely-resilient  model  for  accessing  power 
at  a  massive  scale  and  with  both  affordable  and  predictable  costs.  Since  2017, 
Beijing  has  imposed  a  strict  capacity  cap  of  45  million  tons  on  aluminum. 
China’s primary-aluminum industry has since entered a fundamentally-new stage. 

This policy is working. By the end of 2025, China’s electrolytic aluminum capacity 
had  reached  44.997  million  tons  per  year  (mtpa),  effectively  touching  Beijing’s 
capacity ceiling. Official data show that primary aluminum output reached 45.02 
mtpa in 2025, implying that the industry is already operating at full capacity. 

The  implications  are  profound.  Previously,  China  often  acted  as  the  marginal 
supplier,  rapidly  expanding  output  when  prices  rose  and  thereby  capping 
upside. That dynamic no longer applies. With capacity limits strictly enforced 
nationwide,  China’s  aluminum  output  has  become  inelastic.  This  will 
become a key factor driving the global aluminum market onwards. 

While  China’s  supply  growth  is  capped,  overseas  aluminum  supply  is  also 
constrained by conditions even more binding: electricity availability. In Europe, 
the  energy  crisis  of  2021–2022  permanently  altered  the  economics  of  aluminum 
smelting. Electricity prices are much higher than pre-crisis norms, making capacity 
expansion difficult for energy-intensive industries such as aluminum smelting.

In  the  U.S,  the  challenge  is  not  only  price  but  competition  for  electricity. 
The  rapid  expansion  of  AI-driven  data  centers  is  fundamentally  reshaping  the

A guide for the perplexed younger person 
seeking meaning today. 
Young  people  today  are  more  disoriented,  sad,  anxious,  and  confused  than  at 
any  point  in  recent  memory.  Technology  and  social  media  exacerbate  these 
conditions  by  placing  teens  and  young  adults  in  a  state  of  constant  self-
surveillance,  where  goodness  and  kindness  are  replaced  by  metrics  such  as 
likes,  views,  or  shares.  Moral  and  spiritual  life  have  thinned  dramatically.  Trust 
in  institutions  has  eroded,  trust  in  one  another  has  weakened,  and  many 
young  people  lack  anything  to  which  they  can  tether  a  durable  sense  of 
moral  or  existential  meaning.  They  feel  ungrounded  from  the  world  and  are 
quietly crying out for depth, direction, and connection. 

We  recently  read  Sir  John  Templeton’s  The  Worldwide  Laws  of  Life,  a  work  in 
which  he  outlines  hundreds  of  enduring  spiritual  principles  and  forms  of 
practical  wisdom  oriented  toward  human  growth.  Drawing  from  philosophy, 
science,  psychology,  and  sacred  scriptures,  the  book  attempts  to  distill 
what  civilizations  across  time  have  independently  discovered  about  how 
to  live  well  and  flourish.  Yet,  the  people  who  might  benefit  most  from  such 
a  text—teens  and  young  adults—are  perhaps  the  least  inclined  to  pick  it  up. 
And yet, for some, it may be among the most important books they never knew they 
needed. 

Many  teens  lack  a  sense  of  meaning—not  in  the  sense  of  structure  or 
occupation,  but  in  the  deeper  sense  of  moral  and  spiritual  orientation.  They 
attend  school,  enter  the  workforce,  and  meet  expectations,  yet  still  feel 
unmoored.  Some  cultural  critics  have  suggested  that  this  condition  arises 
from  a  society  in  which  suffering  is  to  be  avoided  at  all  costs,  rather  than 
interpreted or integrated into a broader understanding of life. Templeton’s 
work echoes this concern. Meaning, for him, does not emerge from comfort or 
distraction, but from how one orients oneself toward reality. 

Indeed,  Templeton  spent  decades  studying  diverse  civilizations,  religious 
traditions,  and  philosophical  systems,  searching  not  for  novelty  but  for 
convergence.  The  Worldwide  Laws  of  Life  represents  a  distillation  of  those 
observations. While the book contains far more than can be addressed here, its

1.  Love as Self-transcendence.

For  many  young  people  today,  the  self  has  become  an  endless  project—
optimized,  branded,  monitored,  and  performed.  Love  interrupts  this  loop. 
Templeton treats love as an active orientation away from obsessive self-concern 
and toward the reality of others. 

Love  matters  because  it  breaks  the  closed  circuity  of  self-preoccupation.  A  life 
devoted  entirely  to  self-understanding  eventually  collapses  under  its  own 
weight;  balance  requires  self-transcendence.  As  Templeton  succinctly  put  it: 
“Love is the most powerful force in the world.” 

2.  Gratitude as Realism. 

Gratitude,  for Templeton,  is  a  kind  of  realism.  It  is  the  recognition  that  life  is  a 
gift, a miracle. Gratitude quietly undermines both entitlement and despair. 
It  resists  the  illusion  of  total  self-authorship  and  acknowledges  the  countless 
people, circumstances, and contingencies that have contributed to one’s present 
existence. In doing so, it restores a sense of balance between effort and gift. 

For  young  people  raised  in  highly  meritocratic  environments—where  worth 
often  feels  as  though  it  must  be  constantly  proven—gratitude  destabilizes 
the  belief  that  value  depends  entirely  on  performance.  It  is  not  a  mood,  but 
a  stance  toward  reality.  Gratitude  reminds  us  that  meaning  often  begins 
where control ends.

Ultimately,  to  be  a  reserve  currency,  the  issuing  country  needs  to  have 
something  that  others  want  to  buy.  In  the  case  of  the  U.S.  dollar,  countries

The  same  logic  applies  today  with  Beijing.  If  countries  want  to  build  a  modern 
military running on 6G, to develop domestic renewable-energy capabilities and 
to upgrade their grid infrastructure, then they will need the refined metals and 
critical  raw  minerals  which  Beijing  controls.  In  essence,  if  countries  want  to 
follow in China’s footsteps towards economic development, then they will 
need RMB to do so.

How is the RMB’s emergence as a potential reserve currency likely to unfold?

Two lessons from history are worth bearing in mind.

First, monetary transitions are more evolutionary than revolutionary. The decline 
of one reserve currency is a multi-decade process as the need for the currency 
gradually  declines  as  the  global  balance  of  power  changes,  confidence  in  the 
issuer  wanes  and  the  incumbent  power  retreats  from  imperial  over-reach.  For 
example,  in  the  case  of  sterling  during  the  20th  century,  while  its  share 
of  central  bank  foreign  exchange  reserves  peaked  in  1903,  its  strong 
network  effects  ensured  relevance  for  decades  to  come.  It  was  not  until 
1963  –  following  the  end  of WW2  and  the  run  on  sterling  following  the  Suez 
Crisis debacle in 1956 – that sterling’s role as a global reserve currency became 
permanently diminished.

This medicine does exist, but it doesn’t come from Big Pharma. Instead, it comes 
in  the  form  of  a  “social  prescription”—a  doctor’s  referral  to  community-
based,  ancient  forms  of  healing,  such  as  art,  music,  exercise,  nature,  and 
volunteering. It’s more than just a recommendation to exercise or socialize. A 
social  prescription  is  customized  to  a  patient’s  specific  interests:  what  brings 
them joy, purpose, awe, flow and childlike curiosity. And it has the potential to 
transform public-health outcomes globally, but especially in the U.S.

We  quote  Julia  Hotz,  author  of  the  book  The  Connection  Cure:  The  Prescriptive 
Power of Movement, Nature, Art, Service, and Belonging: 

It all starts with flipping the script from what’s the matter with you to focusing 
on what matters to you. What are your activities that you love? What gets you 
out of bed in the morning?

In  other  words,  the  medicine  at  the  heart  of  a  social  prescription  is  to  help 
people  find  their  purpose.  What  more  powerful  way  to  treat  an  unmoored 
teenager, a boy in crisis, or someone who is suffering from loneliness, grief 
or  trauma,  than  to  help  them  find  a  sense  of  meaning  in  life?  (See  WILTW 
January 22, 2026). As it turns out, the biochemistry of kindness and love can also 
help treat mental illness, chronic disease and pain. (See articles).

Social  prescriptions  are  not  necessarily  meant  to  replace  pills  or  therapies. 
But  there  is  growing  evidence  that  this  approach  to  medicine  can  have 
transformative public-health impacts: more than 80% of our health outcomes 
are  driven  by  social  and  environmental  factors,  while  only  16%  are  related  to 
clinical  care.  This  means  that  in  order  to  be  healthy,  we  need  access  to  basic 
resources,  such  as  clean  air  and  fresh  food,  as  well  as  activities  that  give  us  a 
sense of purpose.

And  yet,  for  most  Americans  in  particular,  the  routine  course  of  treatment 
is  to  prescribe  someone  a  pill.  Nearly  half  of  America  takes  at  least  one 
prescription drug, and at least one in six takes a psychiatric drug, according 
to  the  CDC,  even  though  some  of  the  most  commonly-prescribed  drugs,  like 
antidepressants, show a mixed bag of results and side effects (see WILTW March 
21, 2024). In the U.S., healthcare spending is heavily skewed toward clinical 
care,  with  about  56  cents  spent  on  social  services  for  every  $1  spent  on 
health  services.  This  is  a  stark  contrast  to  other  OECD  nations  that  average 
$2  in  social  spending  for  every  $1  in  health  care.  Roughly  31%  of  U.S.  health 
expenditures  go  to  hospitals  and  20%  to  physicians/clinics,  leaving  minimal 
investment in the social aspects of health.

Social  prescriptions  offer  an  alternate  course  of  treatment—one  that  has  been 
around since ancient times. One review of 86 social prescriptions in the UK found 
patients  who  received  them  experienced  not  only  a  decrease  in  anxiety  and 
depression, but also a boost in self-esteem, confidence, and positive mood. 
This  complements  decades  of  research  demonstrating  how  exercise  reduces 
depression, and spending time in the woods reduces heart disease and stress.

Studies  in  the  UK,  which  routinely  uses  social  prescribing,  show  that  social 
prescriptions also save money.

U.S.-India Trade deal: Where is the oil 
coming from? And at what price?
Although India has not officially confirmed it as of yet, President Trump said that 
India  had  agreed  to  a  new  trade  deal  that  included  stopping  the  purchase  of 
oil  from  Russia.  "He  [Prime  Minister  Modi]  agreed  to  stop  buying  Russian  oil  and 
to  buy  much  more  from  the  United  States  and,  potentially,  Venezuela."  Assuming 
this is correct (and previous announcements by the Trump administration that 
India was stopping purchases of Russian oil proved premature), there are several 
major problems with the U.S. supplying India with oil. Oil is not as fungible as 
many  outside  the  industry  believe;  Indian  refineries  are  designed  to  handle 
Russian  medium  grades  and  have  to  be  modified  to  handle  U.S.  (light)  or 
Venezuelan  (very  heavy)  grades.  While  certain  grades  of  Saudi  or  Iraqi  oil 
would work, they are already under contract. So where is the oil coming from? 

The  actual  provisions  of  the  India-U.S.  deal  remain  somewhat  vague,  and 
there  has  as  of  yet  been  no  published  documents.  Aside  from  the  extremely-
sensitive  subject  of  oil,  the  public  statements  from  the  two  sides  agree  that 
U.S.  tariffs  will  drop  to  18%,  whereas  there  will  be  no  tariffs  or  “barriers”  on 
American goods entering India. Indian opposition leaders immediately railed 
against  the  deal,  accusing  Prime  Minister  Modi  of  selling  out  to  the  U.S.,  and

Switching  to  U.S.  or  Venezuelan  oil  will  not  be  a  fast  process  for  Indian

Given existing contracts it would be at best April before such a move could even

modifications to change sources. Oil comes in a wide variety of grades, and 
is  not  as  interchangeable  as  many  outside  the  industry  think.  Russian  oil, 
along with oil from the Persian Gulf, occupies a coveted niche, being of medium 
density and moderate sulfur content. This is in contrast to most U.S. oil (which 
tends to be lighter and nearly sulfur-free) and Venezuelan oil (which is extremely 
dense). While it is technically possible to change sources, refineries tend to be 
tuned  to  their  sources,  and  some  products  are  difficult  or  more  expensive  to 
produce  from  alternate  oil  grades.  While  sulfur  content  is  a  negative  in  some 
aspects  of  petroleum  processing,  it  is  also  needed  to  produce  lubricants  and 
fertilizer.

Another factor will be cost. India is receiving Russian oil at below market cost, 
typically $16/bbl below market rates. Unless the U.S. intends to match 
that  discount  by  subsidizing  oil  producers,  which  is  highly

may cause Prime Minister Modi and his BJP party domestic political problems, and 
make Indian refined products more expensive on the open market.

Then  there  is  the  question  of  whether  or  not  the  U.S.  can  even  supply

since  COVID,  oil  exploration  and  the  drilling  of  new  wells  have  been  at  recent 
record lows. The break-even price for oil is approximately $70/bbl for most fields, 
but recent reports indicate that the break-even price for new production will be 
on the order of $95/bbl by 2035. Oil is currently trading at near $65/bbl.

Interference  in  the  oil  markets  for  geopolitical  reasons  carries  significant  risk. 
The  global  oil  markets  are  highly  interconnected.  While  some  have  argued 
there is a glut of oil on the market, and Russian oil can be excluded with little risk 
to the markets, this is probably a misconception. The oil markets are designed 
to stay in an equilibrium between market supply and demand, with the OPEC+ 
countries  in  particular  working  together  to  ensure  balance  at  a  consistent, 
reasonable price. If prices are too high the global economy suffers, reducing 
demand. Too low and new wells are unprofitable, reducing supply. Another 
major factor is the unrest and uncertainty over Iran, a major source of medium-
density oil. Any disruption to Iranian outputs, much less a closure of the Straits 
of Hormuz in combination with restrictions on Russian oil, will cause shortages 
and sharp price rises worldwide. 

In conclusion, while a deal to make India switch to U.S. controlled oil would be a 
major trade win for the Trump Administration, it may not benefit Ukraine. Even 
if India does move completely away from Russian oil, it will not likely have 
enough  impact  on  Russian  revenues  to  cause  them  to  change  policy  on 
Ukraine  or  rush  into  an  unfavorable  peace  deal.  Unless  the  U.S.  intends  to 
risk a world war and attempt to physically interdict Russian shipments, someone 
will  buy  the  oil,  especially  since  the  proposed  deal  will  cause  prices  to  rise, 
and Russian oil will probably remain attractive to others and reach the market 
somehow.  The  result  may  be  that  the  unintended  effects  of  this  deal  will  backfire 
and harm both India and the U.S., with Europe again caught in the crossfire.
