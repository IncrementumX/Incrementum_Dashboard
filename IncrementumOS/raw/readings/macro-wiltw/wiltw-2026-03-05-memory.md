China is sanctioning Japan’s rearmament program. In October, Japan’s newly-

budget  by  the  end  of  this  month.  However,  following  her  remarks  on  China’s

Japan’s military and any end-users that contribute to Japan’s military capabilities. 

Embedded in the announcement is an extraterritorial re-export clause that 
prohibits any third party from re-exporting such items to Japan. This is now 
part of a pattern. In April 2025, Beijing also threatened sanctions against South 
Korean companies that re-exported rare-earth products to the U.S.

Sino-Japanese relations continue to deteriorate. On Tuesday 24th February, China’s 
Ministry of Commerce imposed further export controls on 20 Japanese companies to curb 
Japan’s  remilitarization.  These  regulations  again  included  an  extraterritorial  clause 
that bans overseas organizations or individuals from transferring dual-use items to 
the listed companies. As per the January announcement, international companies that 
re-export dual-use items to Japan will be legally liable under domestic Chinese law.

The realities of the “romantic recession” 
may be leading to a generation of acute 
psychological shock, deepened distrust in 
institutions, and a massive revaluation of 
human relationships. 
We  are  drifting  into  an  era  of  mass  singleness.  The  Economist  recently  warned 
that  we  are  living  through  a  “relationship  recession,”  with  100  million  more 
single  people  worldwide  than  if  coupling  rates  had  held  steady  since  2017.  In 
the U.S. alone, half of men and 41% of women between the ages of 25 and 
45 are not living with a partner; double the rate of 50 years ago. What are 
the implications?

Across the Atlantic, a British survey reported by The Times found young men at 
a “crisis point.” Nearly half say they are contemplating giving up on love entirely. 
Forty-four percent admit they are “unwilling to undertake the compromise 
and  self-improvement  that  relationships  require.”  That  phrase  is  telling. 
Romance has always required friction—negotiation, sacrifice, growth. To opt out 
of  those  demands  is  not  merely  to  avoid  heartbreak;  it  is  to  avoid  one  of  the 
primary mechanisms of adult maturation. 

The  pandemic  accelerated  this  drift.  Courtship  moved  onto  screens.  Since 
then, the rise of violent pornography and AI companions has begun to reshape 
desire  itself.  Commentator  Poppy  Sowerby  argues  that  the  “intoxicating 
accessibility”  of  online  porn  has  drained  men’s  motivation  to  pursue  real 
partners  and  distorted  their  expectations  about  sex  and  intimacy.  When 
hyper-sexualized  fantasy  becomes  the  norm,  ordinary  human  bodies  and 
ordinary  tenderness  can  feel  like  a  downgrade.  Normal  bodies  cannot  compete 
with an infinite scroll of optimized stimulation. 

But it isn’t only men who are recalibrating their standards. Women’s preferences 
are  shifting  too.  Across  the  OECD,  women  now  far  outpace  men  in  higher 
education  by  wide  margins.  In  2019,  51%  of  women  aged  25-34  held  a

A  recent  The  Wall  Street  Journal  article  covered  the  inheritor  of  the  Marriage 
Pact:  a  new  AI-driven  matching  system  called “Stanford  Date  Drop,”  in  which

and-desist  letter  from  The  Marriage  Pact,  student  enthusiasm  suggests 
something  revealing:  AI-backed  connection  feels  more  promising  than 
organic encounter. Several students told the Journal that at Stanford, genuine 
conversation  and  spontaneous  connection  are  so  rare  that  any  structured 
opportunity for interaction, whether romantic or platonic, feels meaningful. 

The rise, and now fatigue, of online dating signals something deeper than 
technological  churn.  It  reflects  a  collapse  of  public  spaces  in  which  people 
once met organically: religious institutions, civic groups, extended workplaces, 
neighborhood associations. Participation in communal life has declined across 
the  board.  Baseline  trust  among  strangers  is  lower.  Social  fragmentation  is 
higher. Dating apps emerged as substitutes for lost civic infrastructure. 

But  apps  also  represent  the  marketization  of  intimacy.  Profiles,  swipes, 
engagement  metrics,  algorithmic  rankings—romance  was  translated  into  a 
competitive marketplace. Abundance without commitment became the norm.

“parenting-traps” she sees in her clinic:

2.  Prevent sustained frustration.

3.  Complete  tasks  on  behalf  of  the  child,  even  when  the  child  is  capable  of

4.  Confuse validation with gratification. 

5.  Finance excess to avoid conflict. 

6.  Defend reflexively. 

7.  Re-negotiate basic boundaries out of fear of emotional rupture.

mechanism.

writing,  Louv’s  groundbreaking  work  examined  the  physical  and  behavioral 
fallout of a world where children and adults were increasingly stripped of daily 
exposure  to  nature.  Louv  called  this  gap  a “nature-deficit  disorder”  and  his 
book influenced educational policies and paved the way for a new generation 
of  research  that  connected  unstructured  outdoor  play  to  reducing  obesity, 
lowering stress, and improving ADHD symptoms.

“The postmodern notion that reality is only a construct—that we are what we 
program—suggests limitless human possibilities,” Louv writes. “But as the young 
spend  less  and  less  of  their  lives  in  natural  surroundings,  their  senses  narrow, 
physiologically  and  psychologically,  and  this  reduces  the  richness  of  human 
experience.”

Writing  at  a  time  when  the  internet  was  still  a  maturing  force  and  before 
the advent of the smartphone, Louv’s book was very prescient, forecasting 
a world that would increasingly call for us to remain indoors.

The new research his work helped inspire comes at an important inflection point 
for the globe as A.I. threatens to further upend our relationship with the physical 
world. Meanwhile, communities continue to become more urbanized.  By 2050, 
it’s estimated that approximately 68% of the global population will reside 
in cities. 

The  impact  of  this  extends  far  beyond  the  make-up  of  societies.  It  may  also 
determine how our brains are wired. Last year, a team of neuroscientists from 
King’s  College  London  and  the  University  of  Melbourne  published  a  first-
of-its-kind study that examined how exposure to the natural world impacts 
brain development. They then looked at how those changes may influence 
academic and mental-health outcomes.

To  do  so,  the  researchers  sifted  through  a  variety  of  data  sets  from  more  than 
7,000 ethnically diverse students from across the United States. The information 
analyzed  included  residential  green  space,  socioeconomic  status,  mental  health 
and  academic  performance,  as  well  as  neuroimaging  to  examine  associations 
between green space exposure and brain structure over different periods of time.

As we’ve written about earlier, unsupervised play is an essential component in 
childhood  development  (see  WILTW  September  4,  2025).  But  as  unstructured

children  in  the  United  Kingdom  fell  by  almost  30  percent.  In  the  United

and social media have only exacerbated this trend. 

How can we expect our children to be resilient to growing pressures and anxieties of 
the digital age, when we limit their access to one of the most important things that 
can help them navigate this period?

Some of the most important work currently being done on this topic is from Dr. 
Mariana Brussoni of the School of Population and Public Health at the University 
of British Columbia. Her research draws a direct link between risky outdoor 
play and the positive impact it has on creativity, social skills, and resilience. 
Her  argument  is  simple  but  profound:  By  taking  calculated  chances,  kids 
build a “resilience buffer” that they can draw on in other parts of their lives, 
from peer pressure to academic failure, to reduce anxiety levels.
