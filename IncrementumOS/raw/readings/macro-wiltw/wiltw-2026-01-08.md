How does great music reach and sustain 
humanity, even in its darkest hours?
Throughout  history,  music  has  possessed  a  profound  and  enduring  power. 
It  is  not  only  a  manifestation  of  culture  and  identity,  nor  merely  a  form  of 
entertainment,  but  also  a  source  of  healing,  meaning,  and  peace.  Yet,  music 
is  not  confined  to  these  aesthetic  or  expressive  roles  alone.  Contemporary 
neuroscience  increasingly  suggests  that  great  music  (though  not  all  music) 
activates  deep  neurobiological  mechanisms  that  support  emotional  resilience, 
psychological strength, and collective survival. 

Harvard  Medical  School’s  Dr.  Patrick  Whelan  studies  the  intricate  relationship 
between  the  mind  and  culture.  More  specifically,  he  has  observed  that

listening  to  music—particularly  in  live  performance  settings—has  a 
uniquely-powerful  emotional  impact.  Listening  to  live  music  is  a  visceral 
experience: tones, rhythms, and vibrations are felt throughout the entire body. 
As  Dr. Whelan  told  Harvard  Medicine  Magazine, “There’s  an  incredible  complex 
sound  signature  all  around  you. The  brain  has  to  sift  through  all  the  ambient 
noise in a concert hall. It’s a much more primitive form of listening compared to 
a focused conversation.” 

This “more primitive form of listening,” Whelan argues, reflects the evolutionary 
foundations  of  human  auditory  perception.  Early  mammals  were  primarily 
nocturnal. As a result, hearing functioned as a defensive mechanism, enabling 
early mammals to detect threats and avoid predation in darkness. 

Harvard  Medicine  explains:  “the  modern  experience  of  listening  to  live 
music  can  be  viewed  as  a  vestige  of  that  primeval  adaptation.”  In  this 
sense,  live  music  activates  ancient  sensory  pathways  associated  with 
safety, vigilance, and belonging. Whelan speculates that this may explain why 
people  are  drawn  to  communal  musical  experiences  such  as  church  services, 
where shared sound fosters connection and collective presence. Conversely, the 
absence of communal listening—for instance, during the COVID-19 pandemic—
may  have  contributed  to  heightened  anxiety  and  depression  by  depriving 
individuals of this deeply-rooted sensory and social experience. 

At  the  neurological  level,  music  directly  influences  the  brain’s  wave 
patterns,  which  regulate  memory  recall,  attention,  emotional  processing, 
and  motor  coordination.  Different  genres  produce  distinct  rhythmic  and 
harmonic  structures  that  modulate  the  nervous  system  in  different  ways. 
Slower  music,  for  instance,  has  been  shown  to  reduce  heart  rate,  blood  pressure, 
cortisol levels, and breathing pace, thereby promoting relaxation. More energetic 
symphonic  or  orchestral  music,  by  contrast,  can  be  stimulating  and  improve 
attention.  Research  from  Stanford  University’s  Center  for  Computer 
Research  in  Music  and  Acoustics  has  demonstrated  that  brain  waves 
synchronize with musical rhythm: slower beats encourage  meditative states, 
while  faster  tempos  are  associated  with  heightened  alertness  and  cognitive 
engagement.

There is also a well-documented difference between the neurological effects of 
familiar  versus  unfamiliar  music.  Listening  to  familiar  songs  reliably  lowers 
cortisol  levels  and  calms  the  nervous  system.  One  study  found  that  when 
individuals  listened  to  music  they  already  enjoyed,  both  respiratory  patterns 
and  heart  rate  became  more  regulated.  This  effect  occurs  because  familiarity 
allows  the  brain  to  predict  what  comes  next,  reducing  cognitive  load  and 
hypervigilance.  When  uncertainty  diminishes,  the  nervous  system  can  settle 
into a more stable and restorative state. 

Because  music  touches  neural  circuits  that  are  often  less  accessible 
through conversational means, neurodiverse individuals may be especially 
responsive  to  its  benefits.  For  individuals  with  autism  or  ADHD,  traditional 
educational  or  social  environments  can  present  challenges  for  memory 
retention  or  emotional  regulation.  Music,  however,  can  regulate  brain-wave 
activity in ways that bypass these difficulties, offering an alternative pathway to 
focus, expression, and emotional balance.

Classical  music,  by  contrast,  has  been  shown  to  regulate  brain  waves,  reduce 
physiological stress, and foster emotional integration. Narrative-driven lyrical 
music,  such  as  songs  by  Bob  Dylan,  offers  teens  a  means  of  perspective-
taking,  emotional  identification,  and  self-expression.  The  often  disjointed 
or  emotionally-opaque  lyrics  of  much  modern  music  can  limit  this  depth  of 
engagement.  At  its  best,  music  should  function  as  a  mirror  of  human  feeling, 
enabling listeners to process emotions rather than suppress them. In this sense, 
listening to somber music during periods of sadness can actually be emotionally 
validating, facilitating release rather than repression. 

The  psychological  power  of  music  is  supported  by  robust  empirical  evidence. 
Music therapy has emerged as an evidence-based clinical intervention. During 
periods  of  distress,  the  autonomic  nervous  system  becomes  dysregulated. 
Musical  rhythm,  however,  can  entrain  physiological  systems,  gently 
restoring equilibrium and coherence. 

Researchers  have  found  that  the  vagus  nerve,  often  described  as  the  primary 
communication  pathway  between  brain  and  body,  is  directly  influenced  by 
music.  Classical  music  in  particular  facilitates  entry  into  the  ventral  vagal 
state,  a  condition  in  which  the  nervous  system  shifts  away  from  fight-
or-flight  and  toward  safety  and  calm.  This  physiological  recalibration  is 
foundational for healing chronic stress and emotional dysregulation. 

Perhaps  the  most  remarkable  historical  example  of  music’s  power  to  sustain 
humanity  comes  from  Brian  Moynahan’s  Leningrad:  Siege  and  Symphony. 
Moynahan recounts the devastating Siege of Leningrad during World War 
II  and  the  extraordinary  role  of  Dmitri  Shostakovich’s  Seventh  Symphony 
in  embodying  the  city’s  will  to  survive.  Following  the  German  invasion  of 
the Soviet Union in 1941, Leningrad was encircled for years under brutal winter 
conditions.  Its  people  faced  starvation,  freezing  temperatures,  and  relentless 
deprivation. 

Yet,  in  the  summer  of  1942,  as  Nazi  forces  stood  only  miles  away,  a 
makeshift  orchestra  of  starving  musicians  performed  Shostakovich’s 
symphony.  The  symphony  is  very  physically  and  technically  demanding, 
requiring  eighty  musicians.  Moreover,  the  musicians  were  not  only  starved, 
but also exhausted by the concentration the piece requires. Yet, they sacrificed

What  the  research  shows  is  that  the  biology  of  kindness  is  chemical.  Giving 
activates  reward  pathways  in  the  brain  linked  to  pleasure,  motivation  and

Oxytocin in particular helps regulate stress, which in turn helps lower blood pressure, 
reduce stress responses, and build feelings of social connection. Turning  on  these 
pathways repeatedly, over time, is the key to why generosity is linked to better 
health, especially in conditions influenced by chronic stress, such as depression 
and heart disease. 

Giving is also a powerful way to ameliorate loneliness and even deaths of 
despair, two crises of epidemic proportions, especially in the U.S. (See articles). 
As  an  example,  one  survey  by  researchers  at  the  University  of  Michigan  found 
that,  for  men,  ten  years  of  volunteering  slashes  death  rates  significantly.

gaps  in  factors  like  cardiovascular  issues,  deaths  of  despair  and  suicide,  “the 
medicine of giving” holds so much potential for improving health outcomes 
worldwide. 

Consider more of the new and compelling findings: 

Two decades ago, a landmark study led by the psychologist Robert A. Emmons 
sought  to  understand  how  people  benefit  psychologically  from  gratitude,

physical benefits that come with “counting one’s blessings.” 

In  a  recent  study  on  organ  donations,  Dr.  Emmons  found  that  the  more 
gratitude a recipient of an organ feels, the faster the person’s recovery. 

Another study found that just five minutes of gratitude can shift the nervous

HeartMath in Boulder Creek, CA, found that states of appreciation help activate 
our parasympathetic nervous systems, inducing “rest and digest” as opposed to 
“fight or flight.”

No More Pretending: from Rule of Law to 
Rule of Power.
Since  the  Second  World  War,  the  United  States  has  publicly  insisted  that  its 
foreign  policy  was  in  accord  with  international  law  and  norms,  driven  by  core 
moral  and  ethical  principles  such  as  respect  for  human  rights,  relatively  free 
markets,  and  democracy.  The  reality  has  always  been  somewhat  mixed,  but 
these  agreements  imposed  real  constraints  on  American  policy.  After  the  end 
of  the  Cold  War,  with  no  comparable  power  or  clearly-opposing  ideology  to 
challenge it, American foreign policy became increasingly transactional. Where 
previous  post-Cold War  administrations  at  least  paid  lip  service  to  the  Rule  of 
Law,  in  its  second  term  the Trump  Administration  has  abandoned  any  pretext 
that  its  foreign  policy  is  driven  by  anything  other  than  self  interest.  The 
Venezuela operation leaves no doubt we have entered a new 
era of Rule of Power over Rule of Law in international affairs. 

While  it  goes  through  cycles  of  relevancy,  a  system  of  international  law  has 
existed  throughout  recorded  history.  The  earliest  treaties  between  nation 
states  were  written  in  cuneiform  on  clay  tablets.  Although  underwritten  by 
raw  power,  most  nations-states  and  even  empires  recognized  that  there 
must be some agreements on conduct and further that those agreements 
must  be  kept  (pacta  sunt  servanda).  The  concepts  of  diplomatic  immunity 
and  protections  afforded  to  heads  of  state  are  of  equally-ancient  origins.  The 
modern era of diplomacy is generally marked by the Congress of Vienna in 1814-
1815. The rules set forth in it and later agreements such as the Hague (1899 and 
1907)  and  Geneva  Conventions  (1864,  with  numerous  additions  and  revisions) 
culminated  in  the  founding  of  the  United  Nations.  This  system  governed 
relations  during  the  Cold  War,  and  was  expanded  with  bi-  and  multi-lateral 
treaties on key issues such as trade and arms control. Since then, that framework 
has come under increasing stress.

outside the jurisdiction of the United States. Although in 1909, Supreme Court 
Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes could state that there is a “presumption against 
extraterritoriality,"  the  U.S.  now  exercises  broad  claims  of  extraterritorial 
jurisdiction  –  in  other  words,  the  right  to  impose  U.S.  Law  on 
the  citizens  and  even  leaders  of  other  countries,  even  if 
otherwise prohibited by international law and treaties. 

The  key  difference  between  the  Trump  administration’s  policies  and  prior 
administrations is the lack of effort to otherwise justify them under international 
law.  In  contrast,  the  Trump  administration  is  actively  promoting  the  fact 
many  of  its  activities  are  contrary  to  the  UN  charter  and  other  treaties, 
claiming it proves its “America First” agenda. The rationale for the U.S. seizing 
Maduro  and  even  running  the  country  are  essentially  practical  and  based  on 
control of the Western Hemisphere. Oil and other natural resources top Trump’s 
comments in this respect. In addition, keeping other nations out of the Western 
Hemisphere, specifically China, Russia, and Iran, are also given high priority. 

The Secretary of State, Marco Rubio, said in response to a journalist questioning 
the administration’s narratives that “I don't care what the UN says.” The President 
has  suggested  that  unilateral  actions  will  be  taken  in  other  areas  such  as 
elsewhere in Central America as well as potentially Greenland (see Section 11). 
And  the  U.S.  ambassador  to  the  UN,  Mike  Waltz,  said  on  Monday “You  cannot 
continue  to  have  the  largest  energy  reserves  in  the  world  under  the  control  of 
adversaries of the United States.”

From  military  actions  to  third-party  tariffs,  sanctions,  and  extraterritoriality,  it 
is  evident  that  the  U.S.  is  increasingly  acting  on  its  own  outside  the  system  of 
international  law.  The  consequences  of  the  destruction  of  this  system  are 
likely  to  be  felt  across  the  scope  of  international  relations,  especially  the 
world economy. International trade is highly dependent on stable, predictable 
relations between governments. Trade is utterly dependent on property rights,

The seizure of the Russian-flagged tanker “Marinera” Wednesday morning 
is another dramatic step in the replacement of the “Rule of Law” with “Rule 
of Power.” This seizure is directly aimed at Iran, Russia, and indirectly at China, 
which  depends  on  oil  transported  by  ships  that  operate  outside  the  Western

future attempts at this type of interdiction will likely see the potential for a direct 
military confrontation as both Russia and China, with its rapidly growing Navy, will 
likely begin close escorts of their vital shipping. 

Another  major  consequence  of  the  dismissal  of  international  law  and 
treaties by the U.S. and other major powers is likely to be felt in the area of 
nuclear proliferation. Many point to the example of North Korea, and the lack 
of action or decreased rhetoric towards Kim Jong Un, as well as India, Pakistan, 
and Israel. Other nations such as Iran or Ukraine have to consider how different 
their situation would be if they had nuclear weapons. Even nations technically 
still under the U.S. nuclear umbrella, such as Poland and Japan, are considering 
acquiring these ultimate weapons as insurance, as they no longer feel they can 
trust the US. A new European defense system in place of NATO is being actively 
sought despite the severe challenges (WILTW April 17, 2025). 

What happens next is a critical problem to be faced by world governments. 
The  other  major  powers  such  as  Russia  and  China  have  been  working  on 
alternative  systems  of  agreements  such  as  SCO  and  BRICS+.  While  these  are 
mostly  centered  on  economics,  they  have  the  potential  to  be  expanded  into 
other realms. The enormous military power of the U.S. means it will be a factor 
for  the  foreseeable  future.  While  a  direct  confrontation  is  increasingly  likely, 
these nations are attempting to avoid it for now. Instead, they are attempting to 
enact the gradual marginalization and isolation of the U.S. and any nations that 
choose to remain within its sphere of influence to the extent possible.

In  conclusion,  raw  power  has  always  been  a  major  factor  in  international

where  the  only  rules  are  the  self  interests  of  nations  with 
the  military  power  to  enforce  them,  the  relative  stability 
and  prosperity  of  the  old  global  system  are  fading.  Many  of 
the  themes  we  have  been  exploring  ranging  from  abstract  geopolitical
