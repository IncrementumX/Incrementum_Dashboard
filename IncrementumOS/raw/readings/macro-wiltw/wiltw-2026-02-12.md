It is worth stating up-front that global oil markets are awash in excess supplies. 
The Paris-based IEA is forecasting a global oil stock-build approximating 3.5 to 4 
million bpd this year (see left-hand chart below), while the U.S. EIA is forecasting 
a  smaller  3  million  bpd  stock-build  for  all  of  2026.  Despite  the  persistent

has traded in a $55-$65 band over the last six months.

is unprecedented. We suspect that oil has lagged because of the OPEC supply 
increases and Washington’s desire for lower gasoline prices.

Surviving the age of post-literacy: how to 
raise children who love to read.
We  held  our  annual  Brain  Trust  over  the  weekend,  during  which  we  shared  a 
presentation  on  why  reading,  writing,  and  an  education  in  the  humanities  are 
more  important  than  ever  in  the  age  of  AI. That’s  all  very  well,  one  client  told 
us, but as the father of young children, he wanted more. In a time of ubiquitous 
screens  and  increasingly  addictive  algorithms,  how  do  you  actually  get  your 
children  to  choose  a  book  over  a  screen?  How  do  you  not  make  the  process 
anything other than a fight, one that risks turning books into a chore? How do 
you raise your kids to love reading, the solitary act of losing themselves in 
a story? How do you raise lifelong readers?

In a time when teen reading is in free-fall (see WILTW October 16, 2025), these 
are million dollar questions for so many parents, including ourselves. As parents 
of young children facing similar challenges and concerns, our client’s question 
resonated deeply with us. The benefits of reading are obviously huge, not just 
for future success, but also for longevity, brain health, and mental health. 

A study by Mindlab International found that in just six minutes, reading can 
reduce stress by as much as 68%, lowering cortisol levels and heart rates while 
easing  muscle  tension.  By  this  metric,  reading  is  a  more  effective  way  to  reduce 
anxiety than taking a walk, or having a cup of tea. 

And so, we dove into the research, some of the best of which comes from other 
parents who have made raising readers their mission. There are so many layers 
and  steps  to  this  process—becoming  a  reader  is  truly  a  lifelong  journey—
that  it’s  something  we  will  explore  over  the  course  of  a  series  of  articles,  from 
home life to the power of phonics to higher education. 

Here,  we  begin  with  the  foundations:  how  to  create  a  literary-rich 
environment at home:

spoken  through  a  screen,  but  through  parents,  caretakers,  friends,  and

Interestingly,  reading  ability  is  more  closely  linked  to  a  child’s  range 
of  vocabulary  than  their  age,  and  their  verbal  language  skills  are  a  high 
predictor of later literacy skills.

As  parents  and  children  spend  increasing  amounts  of  time  on  devices, 
their verbal interactions decrease. A 2025 Swedish study reports a negative 
association  between  screen  time  and  both  grammar  and  vocabulary  skills 
at  age  5,  suggesting  that  increased  screen-media  exposure  during  early 
childhood impedes language development. 

Another,  first-of-its-kind  study  published  by  JAMA  Pediatrics  tracked  over 
220  Australian  families  over  two  years  and  found  that  screens  interfered 
significantly  with  children’s  language  opportunities.  The  researchers  found 
that the average three-year-old “was exposed to two hours and 52 minutes 
of screen time a day” resulting in “children being exposed to 1139 fewer 
adult words, 843 fewer child words, and 194 fewer conversations,” each 
day. 

  Read  every  day,  read  multiple  times,  whenever  you  can.  Read  to  your 
children.  Read  in  public  instead  of  offering  screens.  Read  as  a  family.  Read 
on your own, and let your children see you doing it—as the old saying goes, 
monkey-see, monkey-do. If you read on a device, make sure the activity is 
transparent: let your children know what you’re reading, whether it’s a 
novel, the news, or baseball statistics, and invite them into the experience 
at  every  opportunity  (“listen  to  this!”).  Reading  at  home  is  the  foundation 
from  which  everything  else  follows.  Without  this  foundation,  your  child’s 
learning future is on much shakier ground. (See WILTW July 18, 2024).

schooling and those that don’t.

The New Holznot: America’s critical 
minerals crunch is redefining global trade, 
investment, and power dynamics. What are 
the implications? How to invest. 
The  mid-century  pre-industrial  Central  European  term  Holznot  (meaning 
“wood shortage”) encapsulates the concept of a temporary and severe lack 
of essential fuel. In the 16th century, wood was the primary fuel and material for 
construction. Increased demand for housing construction, maritime trade, and the 
mountain-mining  industry  squeezed  supply.  Early  forestry  policies  were  formed 
to regulate production. Holznot peaked in the mid-18th century as the industrial 
revolution  drove  substantial  increases  in  iron  and  coal  production.  Timber 
scarcity had finally passed. 

For millennia, civilizations have regulated themselves to mitigate scarcity, 
real  or  perceived.  Throughout  the  key  crisis  points  during  the  Holznot,  state 
intervention prioritized timber reserves for shipbuilding, restricted logging, and 
increased  imports  from  neighbouring  regions  of  Scandinavia,  the  Baltic,  and 
Russia to meet soaring demand. 

The  West  is  facing  its  very  own  critical  minerals  Holznot.  As  we  wrote  in 
WILTW  January  29,  2026,  several  structural  supply-side  deficits  are  emerging 
across a wide range of metals. Prices will know no ceiling as demand for defense, 
robotics, electrification, AI-data centers, and renewable technologies skyrockets. 
Government interventions worldwide are ramping up.  

The  Trump  administration  is  no  exception.  Last  week,  it  announced  the 
creation  of  a  national  commercial  critical-minerals  stockpile  (“Project  Vault”). 
Additionally, following the inaugural Critical Minerals Ministerial attended by 55

As A.I. continues to grow as a more powerful 
force in everyday life, more Americans are 
intentionally developing screen-free habits 
to overcome the emotional and psychological 
strain of a hyper-digital culture. 
From a pointed uptick in crafting to record sales in “dumb phones,” this definitive 
shift  from  digital  minimalism  as  a  niche  wellness  trend  to  a  more  mainstream 
correction  has  been  so  significant  that  the  current  year  has  been 
hailed as “Analog 2026.”

The  link  between  an  excessive  online  life  and  the  deepening  mental  health 
crisis afflicting the United States and other nations across Europe and Asia has 
been documented. In recent years we’ve also covered the topic in various forms 
(see WILTW December 16, 2023; September 12, 2024; June 19, 2025). The issues 
are  especially  acute  for  kids  and  teens,  whose  soaring  rates  of  depression  and 
anxiety have coincided with the growing presence of social media in their lives. 

But the toll of digital domination in all its forms cuts across generations. A 
December survey of 2,000 Americans by Talker Research painted a clear picture 
of  the  effects.  Numerous  respondents  reported  they  experienced  heightened 
levels  of  anxiety,  irritability  and  a  general  sense  of  feeling  overwhelmed 
when  they  spent  “too  much  time  on  screens.”  Maybe  most  telling,  survey 
participants told researchers that 70% of the time, online life 
increased levels of disconnection and loneliness.

With the advent of more powerful A.I. this digital fallout is sure to only deepen. 
And  that  is  why  there  has  been  a  growing  hunger  to  find  and  live  a  life 
beyond  the  reaches  of  the  online  experience.  One  that  people  can  actually 
feel and touch.

According  to  Google  Trends,  the  phrase  “going  analog”  reached  an  all-
time  high  in  December  and  in  the Talker  survey,  nearly  half  the  respondents 
reported they had deliberately established screen-free time into their daily life. 
As a result, the group reported feeling more productive and present.

Underestimating  the  scope  of  climate  risk  is  distorting  investment  and 
policy  decisions,  increasing  the  threat  to  the  global  economic  system, 
warns the expert analysis from the University of Exeter and Carbon Tracker. 

Current economic models underestimate climate damages because they largely 
assume the coming decades will be much like the past. They fail to encompass 
the  systemic  risks  from  the  ongoing,  non-linear  transformation  of  the 
climate and biosphere that underpin the global economy. 

For example, models assume climate damages are marginal when the impacts 
can  become  structural  and  compound  with  global  warming  of  2  degrees  C  or 
higher. 

We quote:

Rather  than  simply  reducing  output,  climate  change  is  likely  to  reshape 
economic  structures  themselves  –  altering  where  people  live,  what  can 
be  produced,  how  infrastructure  functions,  and  which  regions  remain 
economically viable.

For example, with 2 degrees C of warming, more than a quarter of the world’s 
population  would  experience  an  additional  month  of  severe  heat  stress  each 
year,  while  high  temperatures  and  drought  could  combine  dangerously  in 
places like the Amazon and the Western U.S.

Even  if  an  economic  model  assumes  a  significant  climate  impact  on  the 
economy, such as a 20% reduction in GDP relative to today’s levels annually by 
2050,  it  also  assumes  steady  GDP  growth  will  outpace  those  losses.  They  are 
unable to fully account for any systemic disruption. 

These  models  also  base  climate  impacts  on  global  mean  temperatures  rather 
than on extreme weather such as heatwaves, floods, droughts, or cyclones. In 
reality,  most  climate-related  damage  is  driven  by  extremes, 
as we have warned since 2002. (See reports.)

Models  also  fail  to  incorporate  the  impacts  of  climate-tipping  points  that 
could trigger a global financial crash. 

The  risks  of  crossing  tipping  points  rise  in  lockstep  with  rising  temperatures. 
Above  1.5  degrees  C,  multiple  climate-tipping  points  could  be  triggered, 
including  the  collapse  of  ice  sheets  in  Greenland  and  Antarctica,  permafrost 
melt, Amazon dieback, and changes in ocean circulation, as we have previously 
warned. (See reports.) 

Warm-water coral reefs are the first to have crossed a tipping point and are 
now in permanent decline. (See WILTW October 16, 2025.)

GDP  as  a  metric  also  masks  the  true  impacts  of  damage,  as  it  often  increases 
GDP while welfare, health, ecosystems, and social stability deteriorate. 

The legacy of unrealistic economic modelling has led to complacency and 
delays  in  policy  change,  according  to  a  new  report  by  the  UK  Institute  and 
Faculty  of  Actuaries  (IFoA)  and  climate  experts  at  the  University  of  Exeter. The 
IFoA is the professional body for UK actuaries.

If  climate  change  remains  unchecked,  mass  mortality,  involuntary  mass 
migration,  and  severe  GDP  contraction  are  likely,  writes  Sir  David  King, 
former Chief Scientific Adviser to the UK government, in the report. 

There  are  clear  parallels  with  the  Global  Financial  Crisis,  since  risk  models  at 
the time could not foresee the accumulation of systemic risks. That led to risk 
underestimation,  misplaced  confidence,  and  near  systemic  collapse  in 
2008.
