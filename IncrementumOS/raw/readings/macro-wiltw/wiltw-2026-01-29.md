What is the Empathy Advantage and how will 
it de$ne the success of future generations?
Child  psychologist  and  parenting  expert  Dr.  Michele  Borba  introduced  the 
concept in her 2016 book$UnSelfie: Why Empathetic Kids Succeed in Our All-About-
Me World—a work that was under appreciated at the time, but whose insights 
are  now  more  important  than  ever.  Borba  argues  that  empathy  is  no  longer  a 
“soft skill,” writing:$“In today’s world, empathy equals success, and it’s what I 
call the Empathy Advantage that will give our children the edge they need 
to  live  meaningful,  productive,  and  happy  lives  and  thrive  in  a  complex 
world.”  What  is  the  Empathy  Advantage  and  how  can  parents  intentionally 
cultivate it in an age of distraction, digital immersion, and rising youth mental-
health challenges?$

Dr.  Borba  has  spent  decades  researching  parenting,  child  development,  and 
moral formation across cultures worldwide. While parenting norms vary across 
societies,  she  identi"es  a  striking  commonality:  parents  in  California,  Taipei, 
and  Paris  alike  tend  to  view  academic  achievement  as  the  clearest  metric  of  a 
child’s  success. Yet Borba  argues  this  approach  is  deeply  !awed. 
Academic  performance  alone  reveals  little  about  a  child’s 
inner psychological world including their resilience, sense of 
purpose,  or  capacity  for  healthy  relationships.  Her  central  claim 
is that empathy, rather than achievement alone, is the strongest predictor 
not  only  of  academic  success,  but  of  long-term  professional,  social,  and 
personal !ourishing. 

Empathy  provides  the  foundation  for  perspective-taking,  which  is  itself 
the  cornerstone  of  moral  development  and  kindness.  It  strengthens  family 
and  peer  relationships,  enables  children  to  "nd  meaning  beyond  themselves, 
and  fosters  a  stable  sense  of  self-worth.  Empathy  is  also  one  of  the  most 
powerful protective factors against the teen mental-health crisis, but only if it is 
intentionally cultivated by parents rather than assumed to develop on its own. 

A  key  component  of  this  cultivation  is  what  Borba  describes  as  “self-
esteem-focused  parenting.”  This  approach  emphasizes  building  a  child’s 
inner  con"dence  and  resilience  through  unconditional  love,  praising  effort 
and process rather than outcomes alone,  and encouraging healthy  risk-taking.

New empirical research suggests that the use of large language models (LLMs)

Concern  over  memory  is  not  new.  From  the  earliest  days  of  philosophical 
re!ection, thinkers have worried about the cognitive costs of externalizing 
thought. In Plato’s Phaedrus, Socrates famously warns that writing will weaken 
memory, encouraging forgetfulness by allowing individuals to rely on external 
marks  rather  than  internal  recollection.  Socrates  himself  never  wrote 
anything down, fearing that written records would substitute 
for genuine understanding. This anxiety arose in a sociocultural context 
in  which  individuals  routinely  composed,  retained,  and  recited  epic  poems 
hundreds of pages long in an intellectual world radically different from our own. 

Just  as  writing  once  represented  a  disruptive  technology  that  provoked 
skepticism about its effects on memory, AI occupies a similar role today. Yet 
AI  complicates  the  issue  further.  Memory  is  implicated  not  only  in  how  these 
tools affect human cognition, but also in how AI systems themselves function. 
LLMs  rely  on  vast  stores  of  training  data—often  described  as  “memory”—
and  debates  surrounding  AI  ethics  frequently  hinge  on  what  such  systems 
remember,  forget,  or  retain  across  interactions.  For  example,  what  happens 
to  one’s  sense  of  worth  or  companionship  when  their  AI  “girlfriend”  remembers 
nothing once its memory is cleared? Or when an AI psychiatrist cannot construct a 
coherent narrative across sessions because it lacks persistent memory?

At  a  technical  level,  LLMs  operate  through  next-token  prediction,  generating 
probabilistic  continuations  based  on  patterns  learned  from  massive  datasets. 
While this enables impressive generalization, it is fundamentally different from 
human  memory.  Human  cognition  does  not  merely  predict  sequential 
symbols;  it  integrates  experiences  across  time  into  durable,  structured 
representations.  AI  may  increasingly  replicate,  or  even  outperform,  many 
discrete  human  tasks.  But  this  very  fact  makes  it  all  the  more  important  to 
preserve  what  distinguishes  human  intelligence:  a  memory  grounded  in  lived 
experience, identity, and meaning. 

Scholars estimate that approximately one-quarter of human 
general intelligence is linked to memory. Without memory, identity 
itself begins to erode. Learning, reasoning, and problem-solving all depend 
on  the  brain’s  ability  to  store,  retrieve,  and  manipulate  information. 
Memory allows us to avoid repeating past mistakes, to recognize patterns in the 
world,  and  to  build  associative  knowledge.  Without  the  ability  to  retain  prior 
computations,  cognition  would  become  energetically  ine%cient,  requiring 
constant recomputation from scratch. 

For  this  reason,  many  researchers  describe  memory  as  the  “cornerstone  of 
intelligence.”  Neuroimaging  studies  further  suggest  that  highly-intelligent 
individuals  exhibit  greater  global  e%ciency  and  connectivity  when  engaging 
working memory, activating neural “superhighways” that allow information 
to !ow rapidly across the brain. High-IQ individuals tend to demonstrate more 
e%cient working memory, characterized by faster retrieval and more structured, 
map-like organization of information. 

Working memory in particular plays a central role. It refers to  the  ability  to 
hold  information  temporarily  in  mind  for  the  purpose  of  completing  a  task, 
such  as  retaining  a  phone  number  long  enough  to  dial  it. This  is  distinct  from

Memory  is  not  merely  instrumental.  It  de$nes  who  we  are.  It  anchors

Tolstoy’s  masterpieces  draw  deeply  on  his  personal  and  historical  memories,

depend on memory: the $rst time one meets the love of one’s life often feels

Yet  according  to  Kosmyna’s  recent  study,  AI  use  is  eroding  this  capacity  over 
time.  This  is  intuitively  plausible  as  most  of  us  can  recall  moments  of 
overreliance  on  tools  like  ChatGPT  or  Google’s  AI  Overview.  For  instance, 
spelling, once internalized through repetition, is now automatically corrected in 
an instant. Recall is increasingly outsourced. 

Kosmyna’s  team  asked  participants  to  write  an  essay  under  three  conditions: 
with  the  assistance  of  an  LLM,  with  a  search  engine,  or  with  no  external  tools. 
The  brain-only  group  demonstrated  the  strongest  memory  recall  and  the 
most widespread and robust neural activation. Participants who used 
LLMs  performed  signi$cantly  worse,  often  unable  to  quote 
from their own essays. 

More  strikingly,  research  suggests  that  individuals  frequently  misattribute 
AI-generated  knowledge  to  themselves.  When  asked  to  identify  the  source 
of  information,  such  as  why  they  know  that  the  capital  of  Saint  Barthélemy 
is  Gustavia,  participants  often  credit  their  own  memory,  even  when  the 
information  was  supplied  by  an  AI  system.  The  brain,  it  seems,  readily 
assimilates AI outputs as internally generated knowledge. 

The  boundary  between  convenience  and  cognitive  erosion  is  rapidly  blurring. 
As  AI  increasingly  simulates  human  intimacy  through  virtual  companions, 
therapists,  or  friends,  the  question  becomes  more  pressing: 
if  memory 
deteriorates,  does  our  grip  on  reality  weaken  as  well?  Memory  grounds  us  in 
a  shared  world,  distinguishing  the  real  from  the  simulated,  the  lived  from 
the  generated.  If  we  lose  it,  we  risk  losing  more  than  recall. We  risk  losing 
ourselves.

The loneliness crisis plaguing the developed 
world—the U.S. and across parts of 
Asia in particular—has alarmed medical 
professionals and garnered signi$cant

Our  own  coverage  of  the  topic  has  been  substantial  (see  WILTW  November 
22,  2018;  January  11,  2024;  and  June  13,  2024)  in  no  small  part  because  the 
consequences  of  this  health  crisis  run  wide  and  deep.  Among  doctors  and 
policymakers there is a growing consensus that loneliness—not 
heart  disease  or  obesity—is  the  leading  health  crisis  in  the 
developed world. 

In its landmark 2023 report, “Our Epidemic of Loneliness and Isolation,” the U.S. 
Department  of  Health  and  Human  Services  laid  out  how  this  growing  lack  of 
connection  wasn’t  just  a  mental  health  emergency;  for  millions  of  Americans 
loneliness  poses  a  signi"cant  risk  to  their  physical  well-being.  The  effects 
included  a  29%  increased  risk  of  heart  disease;  a  32%  increased  risk  of 
stroke; and a 50% increased risk of developing dementia for older adults.

"In  the  last  few  decades,  we’ve  just  lived  through  a  dramatic  pace  of  change,” 
explained then Surgeon General Dr. Vivek Murthy following the report’s release. 
“We move more, we change jobs more often, we are living with technology that 
has  profoundly  changed  how  we  interact  with  each  other  and  how  we  talk  to 
each other."

In  its  report,  the  federal  agency  outlined  several  remedies  to  combat  this 
epidemic,  including  reforming  digital  environments,  building  out  more  social 
infrastructure,  such  as  parks  and  libraries,  and  strengthening  government 
policies around programs like paid family leave.

Absent, however, was a basic but surprising antidote: talking to strangers. 
There is no single solution for this crisis, of course, but a growing body of credible 
research has revealed the bene"ts humans experience when they engage with 
people  they  don’t  know.  It  boosts  self-optimism,  increases  empathy,  helps 
heal divisions, and strengthens a sense of community.

“Talking  to  strangers—under  the  right  conditions  is  good  for  us,  good  for  our

Even the smallest of interactions can yield positive effects. One large-scale study 
involving  60,000  participants  in  the  UK  and  Turkey  demonstrated  that “low 
cost”  interactions  such  as  saying “thank  you”  to  a  bus  driver  resulted  in 
higher life satisfaction.

In  2013,  psychologists  Gillian  Sandstrom,  at  the  University  of  Sussex  in  the  UK 
and  Elizabeth  Dunn  at  Canada’s  University  of  British  Columbia,  published  the 
results of an experiment that worked with 60 adult customers at a coffee shop 
in Toronto: half the group was assigned to warmly engage with their barista, the 
rest were instructed to act business-like in their transaction. Overwhelmingly, 
those  who  conversed  with  their  barista  reported  a  “more  positive  effect 
than  people  who  were  as  e%cient  as  possible.”  Additionally,  researchers 
“found  initial  evidence  that  these  effects  were  mediated  by  feelings  of 
belonging.”

Extensive  research  on  this  topic  has  also  been  done  by  Nicholas  Epley  of  the 
University  of  Chicago’s  Booth  School  of  Business  and  Julianna  Schroeder  of 
the  University  of  California,  Berkeley’s  Haas  School  of  Business,  who  recruited 
commuters  on  trains  and  buses  in  Chicago  and  London  and  assigned  them

solitude;  or  strike  up  a  conversation  with  a  stranger.  Overwhelmingly,  those 
who  were  assigned  the  latter  reported  they  found  their  conversations—and 
their commute—to be more enjoyable than they had predicted. The study has 
since been replicated in other countries and produced similar results, with 
respondents  reporting  that  they  come  away  from  the  interactions  feeling 
happier, more empathetic, and less lonely. 

“Humans may be social animals,” the researchers write, “but may not always be
