Preface: Inside Geoeconomic War

Global markets have given way to geostrategic reaction functions. 
Policy, power, and capital are now fused at the national level.

Over the past eighteen months, my thinking has been shaped by a growing conviction 
that the operating logic of the global system has changed. What I have described in 
recent essays as geoeconomic war is a recognition that economic policy, technology, 
national security, energy, and defence have become inseparable as strategic 
competition between the United States and China has intensified—most visibly around 
artificial intelligence and its myriad effects on national security and industrial capability.

As the events of the past year unfolded, this thesis has become well understood and the 
consensus narrative is beginning to understand the phase shift that is occurring in the 
world. Our team has spent a great deal of time refining our perspective on how to think 
about this shift. The body of work we chose to share was not produced as commentary 
for its own sake but has formed pillars of a framework for understanding how power was 
being exercised, and how that exercise of power would begin to express itself through 
markets in this world order.

I have found this framework particularly useful in interpreting the past year not as a 
sequence of unrelated shocks, but as the expected outcomes of a new operating logic 
among the great powers. Developments that can appear disconnected—a sudden 
escalation in Venezuela, renewed focus on global chokepoints such as rare earths or  
the Arctic, sharp moves in gold and commodities, rising emphasis on energy and power, 
flaring tensions between regional powers, or the rapid appreciation of space and  
biotech equities in both China and the United States—cease to look random. They 
become modal outcomes of a world reorganising around security, sovereignty, and 
strategic competition.

The repricing of defence, energy, and critical-technology assets is therefore not 
coincidental. Nor is the renewed bid for hard assets, or the growing sensitivity of 
currencies to resource endowment and geopolitical alignment. These are not isolated 
narratives. They are connected expressions of a system moving away from equilibrium 
and toward strategy. Markets are not mispriced but being repriced by governments.

Underlying this shift is a deeper structural change I have written about before: we no 
longer live in a world of independent policy levers. Monetary policy does not operate 
separately from fiscal constraints; industrial strategy cannot be disentangled from 
national security; energy and technology policy is intimately related to trade policy.  
What has emerged instead is a world governed by national reaction functions—
integrated frameworks which fuse these various levers of policy into one grand national 
reaction function. Markets are increasingly shaped by the visible hand of governments, 
rather than by decentralised optimisation alone.

There is a further irony in how this moment has unfolded. President Xi and the Chinese 
state have operated within a geoeconomic framework for decades. In around 2016 Xi 
emphatically—judging by Xi’s own speeches— forced Beijing to make a deliberate  
pivot away from credit-fuelled stimulus and monetary and financial abstraction toward 
the physical realm, prioritising hard technology, industrial capacity, and strategic control 
over production.

10 years later the AI race and the revealed vulnerabilities in the pandemic have forced 
the United States to compete on Xi’s chosen battleground. AI has pulled capital and 
policymaking back toward the physical foundations of power—hardware rather than 
software, resources rather than leverage, production rather than financial engineering. 
The parallel move toward tighter coordination between the state and the private sector 
reflects a logic China has been applying for years.

In that sense, we are witnessing a rare and revealing moment in which competing 
systems are being reshaped by the same underlying constraints—an unusually clear 
period for observing how governments adapt under sustained strategic pressure.

This piece seeks to discuss how the world is adapting to this shift.

My assertion is that this process is not merely competitive, but constructive. 
Geoeconomic war is no longer just a contest of pressure and response; it is actively 
shaping a new global structure. The United States and China are, in effect, drawing the 
contours of a G2 world—defining domains, spheres, and priorities—while every other 
country, large or small, is being forced to react and respond. This is not a recalibration of 
the old order. It is the early construction of a new world order.

In this regime, policy, power, and capital are fused at the national level. Monetary, 
fiscal, energy, defence, and industrial policy no longer operate independently; they are

They are governed by strategy.

The essence of this shift is asymmetric but coherent. The United States is responding 
through prioritisation—fusing financial power, institutional flexibility, and selective 
expansionism to defend key domains and hemispheres. China is responding through 
scale and saturation—converting surplus into industrial capacity, exports, and hard 
technology in order to embed itself globally. These are not tactical choices; they are 
structural reactions to constraint.

Crucially, this process is no longer merely competitive. It is constructive. The United 
States and China are actively shaping a G2 world, drawing the contours of spheres, 
domains, and influence. Every other country—large or small—is being forced to adjust.

Markets are already repricing this reality. Japan is undergoing a strategic reawakening 
as policy levers come alive under security pressure. Europe is being squeezed 
simultaneously—pushed toward autonomy by the United States, threatened by Russia  
on security, and pressured by China through deflationary competition. Latin America is   
re-emerging as a strategic beneficiary in a resource-driven, hemispheric world. 
Commodities and currencies are being repriced around power, resources, and resilience 
rather than growth alone.

This essay sets out how this new world order is being built in real time—and how markets 
are already trading it.

The United States: National Reaction Functions Under Strain

I.A. The Costs of Geoeconomic War

As the geoeconomic contest between the United States and China has intensified, my 
focus has increasingly shifted to the costs of sustaining such a strategy. Competing at 
the frontier of technology, energy, and national security is not only a strategic challenge; 
it carries material economic, social, and political consequences.

These costs are now visible across household balance sheets, labour markets, and 
political behaviour, and I expect them to play a growing role in shaping both policy 
responses and market outcomes.

In my September update, I noted the structural tension between democratic systems 
and prolonged periods of national security stress. Historically, episodes of heightened 
hard-power stress—whether war, pandemics, or national security threats—have required 
greater centralisation of authority, often narrowing the scope of normal democratic 
processes. This is not an ideological observation, but a recurring institutional pattern.

That tension is now becoming evident again. The degree of coordination and 
centralisation required to compete in a geoeconomic war is rising at the same time as 
domestic tolerance for higher costs is being tested. The interaction between strategic 
necessity and social legitimacy is one of the most important dynamics when analysing 
the US situation in my view.

This friction—between the requirements of geoeconomic competition and the 
constraints of democratic consent—now sits at the centre of the US national reaction 
function and I expect the Trump administration to take specific measures ahead of the 
midterms to handle these costs.

The election of Zohran Mamdani in New York has been a catalyst for this awakening 
within the Trump administration. 

Affordability and Social Stress

The most immediate cost of geoeconomic war is affordability.

Over the past three years, US households have faced a convergence of pressures: 
elevated interest rates as we move to a world where fiscal policy has lifted neutral rates 
away from the zero lower bound, persistently high shelter inflation, and the indirect price 
effects of supply-chain fragmentation and strategic trade policy.

Power as Constraint: The Energy Chokepoint

The second clearest pressure point is power, in the most literal sense.

We are all now aware of the extent to which the US electricity grid is operating  
under conditions of structural constraint. Data-centre demand—driven by AI training  
and inference—has emerged as a new, non-cyclical load. Industry estimates  
suggest that US data-centre electricity consumption could double or even triple by 
2030, adding demand equivalent to multiple large metropolitan areas. At the same  
time, grid investment, permitting timelines, and generation buildout lag materially  
behind requirements.

This creates a paradox. The AI race is a central front in the geoeconomic contest,  
yet it is bottlenecked by an energy system that was not designed for this scale or 
speed. Power prices are rising not just for hyperscalers, but for households and small 
businesses—feeding directly into the affordability pressures described above.

This constraint explains much of the apparent urgency in US energy policy. The push 
to lower oil and gas prices, the renewed focus on domestic production, the willingness 
to revisit previously closed energy relationships, and the tolerance for off-grid or 
unconventional power solutions are all rational responses to the same problem: time-to-
power has become a strategic variable.

This is why we have significant exposure in the portfolio in ‘time to power’ off-grid 
solutions that solve this dynamic and are applicable immediately, unlike nuclear which is  
a future solution and not an immediate one.

China understood this over a decade ago. As discussed in our last annual essay, its solar 
and grid buildout—now measured in hundreds of gigawatts annually—has given it  
a decisive advantage in the energy intensity of industrial and technological expansion. 
The United States is now forced to respond under time pressure.

A Treasury-Fed Wartime Fusion

Monetary Power in an Age of Strategy

One of the most consequential developments of the past year has been the  
re-centring of the US Treasury as the fulcrum of American economic statecraft.  
As the world has moved deeper into geoeconomic war, the balance of gravity within US 
policymaking has shifted. Monetary policy still matters, but it no longer sits alone at the 
apex of the system. The Treasury Secretary has become, once again, a central strategic 
actor. This was the central observation of my 2024 essay, The Visible Hand – that we now 
live in a world where fiscal policy and arms of government expenditure have a dominant 
role in markets as opposed to monetary policy, as was the case in the zero lower bound 
era in the years after 2008. 

This should not be surprising. In periods of sustained geopolitical competition or wartime 
contests, the instruments of policy that matter most are those that are versatile and 
direct: fiscal capacity, spending plans, centralised coordination with private sector 
national champions, and the ability to direct capital across sectors and time. Central 
banks are powerful technocratic institutions, but they are narrow by design. Treasury 
departments are not. They sit at the intersection of finance, politics, and strategy.

In a geoeconomic war, that distinction becomes decisive and has allowed Scott Bessent 
to emerge as the most consequential player in the Trump Administration and in the  
global reordering.

Re-Thinking “Independence”

Much of the contemporary debate around the Federal Reserve remains framed in terms 
of central bank independence, as though this were a binary condition that could be 
preserved or violated. I find this framing increasingly unhelpful. It reflects a peacetime 
mindset applied to a wartime environment and the sooner we shed this framing, the better 
equipped we will be to monetize the effect of the change occurring at the Federal Reserve.

Historically, periods of existential competition have always produced institutional fusion. 
During World War II, the original Treasury–Fed Accord explicitly subordinated monetary 
policy to the financing of national objectives. Yield curves were managed. Balance sheets 
were mobilised. Inflation was tolerated within a broader strategic calculus. Independence 
was not the organising principle; outcome was.

What we are witnessing today is not a breakdown of institutional norms, but a reversion to 
type. As geoeconomic war has intensified, the separation between monetary and fiscal 
policy has become increasingly artificial. The Federal Reserve has found itself carrying 
an enormous balance sheet, deeply embedded in financial market functioning, while 
the Treasury has been forced to think simultaneously about debt sustainability, market 
stability, growth, geopolitical influence and the distribution of gains within society.

I believe a renewed Treasury–Fed fusion should be understood as a wartime accord, 
even if it is not formally labelled as such. Its purpose is not to optimise inflation targeting 
in isolation, but to preserve strategic flexibility across multiple domains: housing, energy, 
defence, industrial capacity, and financial stability.

The Treasury-Fed Accord of 1951 is widely purported to be the start of the environment 
of Federal Reserve independence. I think the reality is the exact opposite: we went from 
a period in which the Treasury subverted and dominated the Federal Reserve prior to  
1951, to a period of time when there was fusion and coordination between the Federal 
Reserve and Treasury.

Kevin Warsh’s Reaction Function

Kevin Warsh’s policy framework is best understood through his conviction that the 
United States is approaching a material, AI-driven productivity acceleration, and that this 
shift fundamentally alters the inflation–growth trade-off. In his view, the dominant driver 
of inflation over the past decade has not been economic growth or wage pressure, but 
excessive government spending and balance-sheet expansion. Productivity-led growth, 
by contrast, is inherently disinflationary. In this sense, he is fundamentally a supply-sider. 

The last Federal Reserve Chair to operate with a genuinely supply-side instinct was Alan 
Greenspan. A Warsh-led Fed would represent a return to that tradition—updated for an 
AI-driven productivity cycle and a post-QE balance sheet world.

This distinction is central to how Warsh would likely approach monetary policy. He does 
not appear inclined toward pre-emptive easing or nominal GDP targeting. Rather, he 
would allow interest rates to follow inflation lower, conditional on realised disinflation 
rather than forecasts alone. Under this framework, a policy rate in the low-to-mid 2% 
range becomes plausible over time—not as stimulus, but as a reflection of falling inflation 
in a higher-productivity economy.

Equally important is Warsh’s emphasis on the Federal Reserve’s balance sheet as 
a primary macro lever. He argues that the Fed’s large balance sheet has played a 
significant role in tightening financial conditions while simultaneously inflating  
asset prices, exacerbating the divergence between Wall Street and Main Street.  
In this framework, meaningful balance-sheet reduction could substitute for a  
substantial portion of conventional rate cuts, allowing borrowing costs for households  
to fall while maintaining discipline in asset markets. The implication is a policy mix that 
eases social and housing stress without undermining inflation credibility. However, 
I expect this to be done very slowly and in coordination with the Treasury and any 
immediate hawkish reaction to his announcement would be a mistake by the market  
and a trading opportunity.

A Global Race to Accumulate

At the heart of this dynamic is a global race to accumulate resources.

In a world governed by national reaction functions, financial surpluses are increasingly 
converted into physical optionality. Resources cannot be sanctioned easily, cannot be 
printed, and cannot be substituted quickly. They underpin industrial capacity, military 
readiness, and strategic autonomy.

China has been operating under this logic for years. As discussed earlier, its expanding 
trade and current-account surplus is not an end in itself. A significant portion of that 
surplus is being recycled—directly and indirectly—into resource acquisition, stockpiling, 
long-term supply agreements, and control over processing capacity. This is not a 
speculative posture. It is a deliberate conversion of financial surplus into physical power.

The result is a persistent and structural bid across commodity markets. Gold and silver 
sit at the apex of this dynamic, but the logic extends far beyond precious metals. 
Industrial metals, energy inputs, and critical materials are all being repriced as strategic 
assets rather than marginal commodities.

This is not a late-cycle inflation trade. It is a strategic accumulation cycle.

Part IV

Global Policy Reawakening

IV.A. Opportunity in a World of Revived Reaction Functions

One of the most underappreciated consequences of the construction of a G2 world is 
that it has forced every other economy to re-engage its policy levers. This is not a story 
confined to the United States and China. As trade, capital, technology, resources, and 
security are reorganised around strategic competition, governments everywhere are 
being compelled to respond.

In the post–Cold War era, many states were able to outsource macro outcomes to 
globalisation. Policy receded into the background; markets cleared; capital flowed 
passively. That world no longer exists. In a system governed by national reaction 
functions, policy has returned as the dominant macro variable—and it is returning 
everywhere, simultaneously, but in highly differentiated ways.

and deeply fertile. Studying governments, institutions, and policy responses has  
once again become central to understanding market outcomes—not just in the core,  
but across the periphery.

Within that framework, there are several regions where we believe the interaction 
between policy revival and structural change is particularly compelling.

The Philosophical Shift

One of the most profound consequences of geoeconomic war is not simply how  
states compete externally, but how societies change internally as a result.  
Strategic rivalry does not remain confined to trade, technology, or defence. Over time,  
it reshapes values, institutions, and the implicit assumptions that govern social order.

In this sense, geoeconomic war is not only a contest between nations. It is also a 
civilisational test.

V.A. From Soft Power to Hard Power

For much of the post–Cold War period, power in advanced economies was exercised 
primarily through soft means. Markets, institutions, norms, and global integration 
substituted for overt competition. Economic efficiency, growth, and stability were 
assumed to reinforce political legitimacy.

Bloomberg: Denmark […] is tired of feeling

(Jan 2026) 

That framework has eroded.

In a world defined by strategic rivalry, power is increasingly assessed in hard terms: 
production capacity, energy security, technological control, industrial depth, and military 
readiness. Soft power still matters, but it no longer determines outcomes on its own. 
Influence now rests on what a society can produce, secure, and sustain under pressure.

Markets have adjusted to this reality more quickly than political narratives. Capital has 
gravitated toward assets aligned with hard power—defence, energy, infrastructure, 
materials, strategic technology—not as a thematic fashion, but as a reflection of how 
value is now being anchored.

The AI story is – unlike previous technology waves– forcing capital into the physical realm, 
while capital is being forced to coalesce around hard assets.

V.B. The War Within Societies

As external competition intensifies, internal tensions become more visible.

The gains and costs of geoeconomic competition are unevenly distributed. Capital, 
strategic sectors, and incumbent asset owners tend to benefit early. Labour, younger 
cohorts, and regions tied to the old economic order often bear the brunt of the 
adjustment. Productivity gains can coexist with job insecurity. Energy transitions raise 
costs before delivering benefits. Defence priorities compete with social spending.

2026 CIO ESSAYThese dynamics create internal conflict, not necessarily ideological, but generational 
and distributive. Questions of fairness, opportunity, and identity become intertwined 
with questions of security and power. Political volatility, cultural polarisation, and shifting 
value systems are not separate phenomena; they are manifestations of the same 
underlying stress.

This is why periods of strategic competition so often coincide with social upheaval.  
The external war creates an internal one—not always violent, but deeply consequential.

Different systems will respond differently. Some may adapt by reconfiguring their social 
contracts. Others may struggle with legitimacy, cohesion, or continuity. Generational 
divides may widen. New value systems may emerge. Old assumptions about work, 
wealth, and identity may fracture.

From a macro perspective, this is precisely what makes the current environment so 
compelling—and so challenging.

A true macro theme is not one that explains a single market or cycle. It is one that 
connects economics, politics, culture, and generational change into a single framework.
