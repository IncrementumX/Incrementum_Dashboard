If, by some miracle, you’ve remained blissfully unaware of HBM, DRAM,

NAND and the alphabet soup of memory/storage, here’s a refresher on what

you need to know:

As GPUs scale up, high bandwidth memory (HBM) content per accelerator

rises fast (NVIDIA’s Blackwell Ultra GB300 has $4k worth of HBM3E). At the

same time, AI labs are pushing toward long-context (even 100M+ tokens) and

more agentic systems. These require a lot more working memory during

inference, because the model has to keep more “history” around.

Nvidia GPUs keep the “hottest” data in HBM, while pushing the “colder” Key-

Value (KV) cache to regular dynamic random access memory (DRAM) and

even solid-state drives (SSDs), so that HBM doesn’t become the bottleneck.

This structurally lifts demand for memory and storage across the stack (HBM,

DRAM, SSDs), especially as video and “world model” workloads grow in

usage.

We’re getting a lot more interested in looking at the beneficiaries of making

more memory - both DRAM and HBM.

here, so we’re going to do a brief dive into the landscape to see what else

The supply chain for standard commodity memory testing (DDR4, DDR5,

LPDDR, and NAND Flash) is a mature, highly optimized ecosystem focused

on throughput and cost reduction. That means it’s a good way to understand

the process – which gets significantly more complex when you’re looking at

the HBM supply chain.

Every DRAM and NAND chip runs the same obstacle course. It starts at

wafer sort, where probers from Tokyo Electron (8035 JP) or Accretech

(Tokyo Seimitsu 7729 JP) align hundreds of dies against probe cards so an

Advantest (6857 JP) or Teradyne (TER US) tester can zap them with

patterns and map the failures.

Laser repair tools blow fuses to swap in redundant rows, saving dies that

would otherwise hit the scrap heap. Surviving chips get sawed apart,

packaged into Ball Grid Arrays (BGAs), and loaded onto burn-in boards for a

trial by fire (literally), a specialty of Aehr (AEHR US). The goal is to stress

weak cells into failing now rather than in the data center.

Final test is where chips earn their stripes. Handlers from Cohu (COHU US)

or Advantest (6857 JP) grab packaged parts, temperature-condition them,

and jam them into test sockets for speed grading. This socket market is a

And as we move deeper into a memory-driven AI cycle, test demand will keep

rising because the cost of failure is exploding. Unlike standard DRAM, where

testing happens mostly at the end, HBM requires rigorous testing at every

layer of assembly.

Advanced HBM

HBM is built by stacking many DRAM dies, so suppliers try hard to make sure

only good dies go into the stack in the first place. That means heavy wafer-

level screening, called “known good die” testing, before stacking. Then,

after the HBM stack is assembled and packaged, it gets tested again as a

finished HBM package.

Importantly, NVIDIA has reportedly tightened these standards even further by

requiring suppliers to fully test HBM packages before sending them to the

foundry for advanced packaging integration, which adds pressure for more

complete screening before HBM ever touches the GPU package flow.

Before the final HBM stack is mounted onto the 2.5D interposer (alongside

the GPU), the entire stack is probed.

This is the most challenging test in the entire industry. The probe card

must contact thousands of microbumps (40µm pitch) without damaging them.

This requires MEMS-based probe technology, almost exclusively supplied by

FormFactor (FORM US) and Micronics Japan (6871 JP), designed with just

enough force to touch but not break.

The Hybrid Bonding Transition

The industry is currently preparing for the transition to HBM4E (expected

2026/2027), which will introduce even more radical changes to the testing

supply chain.

Currently, HBM is stacked by pressing the dies together under heat and

force, often with tiny solder/copper bumps helping the connection. SK Hynix,

uses a Mass Reflow - Molded Underfill (MR-MUF) technology, while

Samsung and Micron use a technique known as Thermal Compression -

Non-Conductive Film (TC-NCF). However, the need for ever improving

HBM4E changes how the stack is stitched together, moving toward hybrid

bonding and tighter wiring/spacing. Hybrid bonding is a way to join dies

where the copper pads connect directly (metal-to-metal) and the surrounding

oxide surfaces bond too. This boosts bandwidth but makes probing and

“known good die” screening much harder.

Contrary to some views that hybrid bonding adoption by the Memory Big 3

has been delayed to HBM5, Samsung has effectively confirmed the

adoption of hybrid bonding in HBM4E.

Recent tests conducted by Samsung on HBM4 hybrid bonding reportedly

showed that yields are highly mature, with little meaningful difference

compared to TC-NCF. BESI (BESI NA), a name we discussed as a hybrid

bonding beneficiary in 26 Trades for 2026, is one of our favorites for this

transition.

Semi-Cap & Subsystems: More
Picks and Shovels

Memory bears have some salient points, the most convincing of which is

“this time isn’t different”.

As we laid out in Trade #2 of 26 Trades for 2026, the classic demand

response from companies like Samsung and SK Hynix has been to lean in,

increase capacity and inevitably glut the market.

In all honesty, they probably aren’t wrong. That’s likely going to happen at

some point – I’m not clairvoyant so I can’t exactly tell you when (if I had to

guess, sometime in 2027), but human behavior is predictable and, normally,

greed does win out in the end even when supply is controlled by a cartel-like

dynamic. What the bears aren’t thinking about, though, is how that new

capacity comes online.

New capacity requires new capex spending. And the fab’s capex spending

is another player’s revenue.

To frame the size of the semicap buildout: TSMC is expected to spend

around $190B-$200B in CAPEX in the next 3 years. Meanwhile, Intel could

ramp to 100k wafers per month of 18A-P + 14A capacity by 2029-30.

Even despite the market’s disappointment with Intel’s 4Q earnings, Intel’s

2026 capex forecast has held up better than many have assumed. Intel

Foundry (IFS) investments are set to be front-loaded in 1H, with a greater

allocation toward equipment purchases. This suggests Intel is becoming

more confident in its ability to secure foundry customers.

As the industry transitions from FinFET (Fin Field-Effect Transistor) to

GAAFET (Gate-All-Around Field-Effect Transistor), allowing for more gate

contact, better control and therefore less power leakage, the importance of

deposition grows exponentially. As devices move to GAAFET with more 3D

surfaces and tighter tolerances, film uniformity/conformality becomes more

critical, so deposition steps tend to increase in importance.

How do you deposit a thin, uniform film of material on every surface of a

nanometer thin sheet? You flow gases one atomic layer at a time - Atomic

Layer Deposition (ALD). ASM International has the highest exposure to this.

Subsystems

Sub-systems are the “picks and shovels for the picks and shovels”.

All of those tools that AMAT, KLAC, TEL, LRCX etc are shipping are

assembled from hundreds of subcomponents. Vacuums, fluid delivery

systems, wafer handling, gas boxes… These are semicap “subsystems” and

they’ve been somewhat ignored by the market thus far in favor of easier

plays.

We were slightly early here, specifically calling out ICHR and UCTT as

beneficiaries of the semi-capex cycle back in July:

“We see subsystem-level suppliers as having a potential revenue

Silicon Photonics & Legacy Fabs

Ironically, TSMC & Samsung are cutting their lagging edge production volume

just as the introduction of 800 high-voltage DC in Data Centers is boosting

demand for power semis.

AI clusters are bandwidth hungry. As East-West traffic (i.e. data between

GPUs and servers within the data center) explodes, data needs to be

transferred at a faster pace. Scaling copper beyond 224Gb/s per lane is

practically impossible as higher speeds shrink the electric reach and require

more retimers, which adds to power and cost.

Silicon Photonics helps address this issue. “Silicon Photonics” is just what it

sounds like – the integration of the optical components onto silicon wafers.

Rather than electrical signals, light pulses through tiny silicon pathways

(waveguides) on the chip. These Photonic Integrated Circuits (PICs) enable

much higher speeds, bandwidth and energy efficiency, with data transmission

rates exceeding 400 Gbps.

PICs today are primarily being utilized in pluggable transceivers and optical

engines that connect switches, servers and GPU clusters. Eventually, co-

packed optics (CPO) may bring these elements into the actual GPU/ASIC

architecture, but the challenge of thermal management, manufacturing and

reliability are still gating factors. For now, they are more practical in separate

pluggable elements.

It’s difficult to discuss this without descending into the alphabet soup - so the

simple upshot is this: copper cabling is hitting a physics wall as AI data

centers need exponentially more internal bandwidth.

Silicon photonics is the only viable path forward, and the market for these

components is likely to inflect within the next two years. When exactly that

happens is a matter of some debate in the industry, but the companies

positioned to manufacture these photonic chips at scale are not trading as

particularly critical bottlenecks. We believe they can see their specialty

semiconductor businesses re-rated once the market appreciates their

importance.

There are two beneficiaries we will focus on, which not only benefit from

photonics exposure but also from any upcycle in legacy semis and the

upcoming power semiconductor boom.

In November 2025, GFS acquired Advanced Micro Foundry which gives

with the acquisition, GFS plans to establish a silicon photonics R&D hub in

Singapore, which will help them push towards 400G (per lane).

Our Watchlist: Trades & Emerging
Trends

So much has happened during the beginning of 2026 that it’s almost been

hard to keep up. Here are four emerging trends, trades and setups we’re

watching that span both semis and the broader “AI Trade”.

1) Legacy Memory Squeeze

Even though the “memory squeeze” is being driven by massive AI demand at

the leading edge, the rush of the memory manufacturers to shift production

capabilities to these newer products is also cutting off supply on lagging edge

– products that still have strong demand across other industries.

Samsung, SK hynix, and Micron have initiated DDR4 end-of-life/phase-

out plans, though supply persists via limited production and long-term

contracts into late 2025–2026; CXMT, the preeminent Chinese memory

player, is also expected to phase out DDR4 as it pivots toward

DDR5/HBM.

As a result, DDR4 is facing severe global shortages, and at one point, it even

traded at a premium to DDR5. We also understand that memory buyers are

engaging in panic buying of DDR4 in 1Q. Therefore, we believe DDR4 pricing

could rise not only in 1Q but also in 2Q—and, under a more bullish scenario,

even into 3Q. We expect the magnitude of the 1Q price increase to be similar

to the prior quarter, with another double-digit increase potentially achievable

in 2Q as well.

Nanya Technology (2408 TT) and other Taiwan legacy-memory names such

as Winbond (2344 TT) are posting strong gains again in early 2026, even

after delivering very strong performance last year. However, we do not view

this as an unfounded rally. For example, we believe the supply tightness in
