Introduction 

Our Approach to the “Year Ahead Outlook”: 

It’s a bit of a ridiculous premise isn’t it, the “year ahead outlook”? 

Just because the number on the end of the calendar changes, you’re 
supposed to have a better idea of what happens over the next twelve 
months? I don’t know about you, but if I felt strongly that I knew what was 
going to happen over the next twelve months, I would not wait until December 
to share it. 

As speculators, we spend the majority of our time trying to be right about 
the future. That tends to be a good thing—efforts spent towards being right 
(or at least less wrong) tend to be rewarded. 

It doesn’t take a long or storied career as an investor to have numerous 
realizations that you’ve missed something very obvious in hindsight. Our 
vocational emphasis on accurate forecasting sometimes hurts us more than 
it helps. Becoming too in-the-weeds risks stiﬂing the creative process that is 
a prerequisite to developing differentiated views. 

Once a year, we drop all pretense. Just for this exercise, our primary focus 
isn’t being right at the right time. Instead, we look at what’s occurred in the 
passing year and what might occur in the year ahead. Without any of the 
pressure surrounding “this has to happen or else I’m wrong,” we ﬁnd that the 
ideas tend to more closely approximate the truly unpredictable nature of how 
things will unfold. 

Instead of an “outlook”, we create our Twenty-Something Trades. You can 
take a look at last year’s to get a better idea.  

The end result is a long list of ideas. Some we think could be real winners, 
some are preparation for a speciﬁc scenario or eventuality, some we simply 
want to keep on our screen. Our aim is to minimize the risk of intellectual 
blind spots. 

Are they all going to be good trades? Of course not, there’s 26 of them. Think 
of this as a thematic watchlist.  

The purpose of this article is to put as many relevant ideas in front of you as 
possible, in hopes that one of them resonates at some point during the next 
12 months. If you think one of these trade ideas is hilariously wrong, GOOD. 
Don’t keep it to yourself – we could end up ﬁnding the right trade from that 
discussion.  

It’s the end of the year, another absolutely crazy year for that matter. Now, we 
get to prepare to do it all again… but differently.

1. Bullshit Jobs 

In his book Bullshit Jobs, David Graeber says: 

If we think about the pyramid of white-collar labor in an average organization, 
the vast majority of earnings potential comes from the top tiers. Meanwhile, 
some degree of “negative expected value employees” will exist in any 
company at the bottom.  

Anyone who has used AI to a reasonable degree for knowledge work knows 
that eventually many of these “bullshit jobs” will be replaced by AI. But the 
adoption of technology is always slower than the pace of underlying 
advancements. We know these advancements are coming fast. We have 
seen some froth in the “AI Infrastructure” trade, so how could we position 
ourselves to express our continued belief that AI will keep getting better 
without taking “the infrastructure bubble pops” risk?

While the market has been primarily focused on what it takes to build and 
better this technology, it’s going to become increasingly important to think

It’s not controversial to say the larger and more paper-pushy the organization,

We’ve seen a lot of progress in AI, and the concerns that many have raised

-  Terriﬁed of hallucinations? RAG architectures that force citations from

-  Worried AI can’t digest your library of compliance ﬁles? Context 

windows have exploded to millions of tokens, enough for even IBM’s 
corporate handbook.  

Meanwhile, price wars and distilled models have driven down the cost of 
inference by over 90% since we wrote “25 Trades for 2025”.

over and over. The notion of high-cost employees being replaced by 
lower-cost resources (both through technology and outsourcing/offshoring)

However, “low decile net income per employee and cutting headcount” is a 
pretty casual and noisy screen. So how do we create a list of companies that 
might beneﬁt from the capabilities of LLMs? 

To ﬁnd large, expensive organizations in high-wage economies that generate 
less in net income per employee than their peers, we proxy “bureaucracy” 
using headcount per dollar of net income and overhead ratios (G&A/Revenue, 
SG&A/Revenue), both converted into sector-relative z-scores. This gives us a 
Bureaucracy Score that ﬂags ﬁrms with unusually heavy administrative and 
managerial layers versus peers. 

Then, we compute a Margin Optionality Score – a z-score ranking of lower 
margin than industry average – to distinguish which companies should truly 
be able to earn more if they ran leaner.  

Finally, we take a blend of the Bureaucracy Score and Margin Optionality 
Score to create an AI Bureaucracy Alpha ranking.  

We’ve applied these scores to more than 800 names across large- and 
mid-cap developed markets and taken two sets. First, the top 100 in terms of 
AI Bureaucracy Alpha ranking and, second, the top 100 scorers out of those 
who are already reducing headcount.

2. Post-Traumatic Supply Disorder 

Last year, we had success by asking ourselves a simple question: what did 
the most signiﬁcant and surprising outperforming stocks of the past year have 
in common? What thread connected 2024’s winners like APP, SE and CVNA – 
and could tugging on this thread help us ﬁnd similar winners?  

This culminated in one of our better performing themes. We proposed in 
“Crouching Tigers, Hidden Dragons” that the link was stimulus-era 
overbuilding, ﬁnanced at zero rates, and aggressive customer acquisition 
meant these former darlings had infrastructure, data and brand assets that 
would be prohibitively expensive for any new entrant to replicate in a higher 
cost of capital world.  

Meanwhile, they’d been left orphaned by their investor base who had been 
badly burned on them in 2022, but ready to leap as their models proved 
successful over a longer term.  

Our core crouching tigers performed well, and our broad basket also 
outperformed signiﬁcantly, led by Robinhood (HOOD US), Guardant Health 
(GH US), AppLovin (APP US), Lemonade (LMND US), Unity (U US) and SoFi 
(SOFI US).  

We return to this well of inspiration to ask the same question of 2025. Where 
can one draw a common thread between some of the most surprising 
outperformers of the year?  

Memory/storage names like Micron (MU US), SK Hynix (000660 KS) and 
Western Digital (WDC US) staged the seemingly-mythical back-half recovery 
and rallied between 170-260% year-to-date.

Gas turbine manufacturers Siemens Energy (ENR GR) and GE Vernova (GEV 
US) more than doubled in 2025.  

It might be easy to generalize here and shrug off these names as simply 
rising due to trends in AI, but plenty of industries have AI upside and yet don’t 
top the list of 2025 YTD returns. We think there’s something else that puts 
these names in the top percentile. 

They all have PTSD. 

That is… Post-Traumatic Supply Disorder. A cyclical corporate personality 
disorder of the capex-related-trauma variety.  

This PTSD arises in industries that have rapidly increased supply in response 
to a sudden boom in demand, only to see demand fade, the market drown in 
oversupply and share prices collapse. These cycles, while painful, have a 
natural tendency to consolidate industries among the strongest players while 
instilling a capex discipline (if not outright fear) when it comes to expanding 
capacity in the future, even when demand surges. When customers eventually 
show up again, these companies simply raise prices and allow their backlogs 
and ASPs to explode.  

Of course, we’re not applying a blanket “this time is different” to cyclical 
industries. And, naturally, any boom that goes on for long enough will 
convince even the most reserved teams to move to increase capacity – the 
cure for high prices, after all, is high prices. 

We’re on the lookout for the most remunerative varieties of PTSD across 
sectors. Natural gas turbine manufacturers were the perfect example this

year. Gas turbine and grid OEMs went through two long and painful 
overbuilds: a major cycle in the mid-2000s and a second coal-gas switching 
resurgence in the mid-2010s as the shale revolution unlocked cheap gas 
domestically – each fading into troughs of demand.  

Now, data center demands have led to record backlogs and stretched lead 
times, with only cautious capacity additions from a small oligopoly of GE 
Vernova, Siemens Energy and Mitsubishi (with Caterpillar picking up some 
spares in their Solar Turbines segment).  

GEV’s most recent investor day shows this dynamic quite well. Despite 
demand for electricity being projected to "double," GEV announced only a 
modest capacity expansion for heavy-duty gas turbines, increasing from ~20 
GW to 24 GW per year by 2028. Meanwhile, they raised their adjusted EBITDA 
margin target from 14% to 20%. In a normal "pre-trauma" cycle, a 
manufacturer seeing demand double would rush to build new factories, often 
sacriﬁcing near-term margins for market share. GEV is doing the opposite. 

The same is true for memory. The notoriously cyclical industry has been 
through three severe DRAM/NAND collapses in the past 8 years. Now, supply 
is not responding. In DRAM, Micron canceled/delayed multiple capacity 
expansions, SK Hynix slowed capacity conversion, and Samsung moderated 
DRAM capex for the ﬁrst time in over a decade. And even as the AI surge has 
pushed HBM demand to extremes, the players are extremely cautious about 
new capacity. 

So where do we see this pattern emerging in 2026?

Offshore Drilling 

Offshore oil remains a critical source of new oil supply, particularly as shale 
production slows (due to rapid declines, capital discipline, and inventory 
concerns). The reality is that deepwater wells produce some of the lowest 
breakevens of new exploratory plays, and remain economic even under ﬂat 
$60 per barrel long-term assumptions. As oil majors look towards the next 
decade of supply, they see stagnating shale, Russian declines and limited 
megaprojects in the Middle East. Offshore drilling – massive long-term, 
slow-declining projects capable of adding millions of barrels of incremental 
production – is needed to ﬁll the gap.  

Offshore is poised to take share structurally, not cyclically, and this remains 
true regardless of short-term oil prices. 

Meanwhile, the offshore drilling industry hasn’t even remotely recovered from 
the 2013-2014 oil price collapse and new supply is non-existent. The most 
recent new drillship was delivered in 2021 and there are zero new drillships in 
the pipeline. Even if an order was placed today, it would be unlikely to reach 
the market until at least 2030.  

With a truly ﬁxed supply, drillship day-rates have increased substantially over 
the past several years as the post-COVID recovery has restarted these 
long-lead projects. The contracting drilling companies that have survived (or 
were restructured) through the depression now hold the cards.

Copper 

The 2000s supercycle ended with a nasty capex hangover. A lot of large 
projects came on just as China slowed, and balance sheets and equity 
holders wore the pain. Since then, majors have been extremely reluctant to 
approve new greenﬁeld copper megaprojects. The talk is “value over volume,” 
brownﬁeld expansions, and M&A for optionality rather than a new 
Escondida-style build.  

Now, electriﬁcation and data center driven grid buildouts argue for 
structurally higher copper demand but corporate boards are still cautious on 
mega-projects, instead opting for marginal expansions, de-bottlenecking, and 
capital returns. Nobody in the industry is ready to model out the kind of 
capacity this will require. 

Unlike turbine manufacturers, copper miners are price-takers rather than 
price-makers. However, like lithium, copper production is much more 
concentrated among the large producers.

Trucking 

Less-than-truckload (LTL) freight is a service, not manufacturing, so the 
discipline dynamic is a bit different. However, we see some threads of 
similarity to those names in our PTSD sweet spot. Trucking was the 
poster-child COVID supply-shortage story that quickly ﬂipped oversupply as 
independent contractors ﬂooded the market, goods demand whipsawed and 
freight rates collapsed. The impact of the trade-war driven volume disruptions 
in 2025 only kicked the industry while it was down. The fallout has resulted in 
a wave of freight bankruptcies that is consolidating the industry.  

While few would call the current trucking market bullish, the reality is that a 
signiﬁcant amount of slack has already been removed from the system.

3. Inference on Device 

When people say "edge AI" or "inference on device," they're describing a 
simple idea with complicated implications: running AI models locally on your 
phone, laptop, or other device rather than sending your query to a datacenter 
somewhere and waiting for the answer to come back. 

Right now, when you ask ChatGPT or Claude a question, your words travel to 
a server farm, get processed by a model running on very expensive GPUs (or 
TPUs!), and the response travels back to you. The whole round trip takes a 
second or two, which feels pretty fast. It’s actually an eternity in computer 
time. Edge AI ﬂips this. The model lives on your device. Your query never 
leaves your phone. The response is generated locally. No internet required. 

This sounds obviously better, right? Faster, less expensive, works oﬄine…so, 
why isn't everything running on-device already? Because there's a catch. 
Actually, there are several catches (which we outline below).

4. Advanced Packaging  

The market continues to frame AI semiconductors as a zero-sum cage 
match: NVIDIA versus custom ASICs, TSMC versus Intel, Blackwell versus 
TPU. This framing fundamentally misunderstands where the bottleneck lives. 

Here’s the thing: the proliferation of custom AI silicon programs across every 
hyperscaler is structurally bullish for advanced packaging regardless of 
which designs succeed. 

Every Google TPU, every Trainium, Meta or OpenAI chip-in-waiting requires 
advanced packaging. Volume is additive rather than substitutive. Meanwhile, 
the packaging layer remains capacity-constrained through at least next year.  

The Architecture 

As usual, we will remind everyone that we are investors rather than chip 
designers. So here’s the basics of what’s going on: 

For most of the PC and early-smartphone era, performance came from 
shrinking transistors. Foundry leadership meant a smaller node, a denser die, 
a faster core. Packaging was largely a commodity service: put the ﬁnished 
die in a package, attach some balls, ship it. Cost and reliability mattered more 
than architecture.  

Now, as Moore’s Law slows, performance gains shift from transistor scaling 
to system-level integration: stacking memory, co-packaging accelerators, and 
mixing specialized chiplets.

For decades, a "chip" meant one piece of silicon with everything etched onto 
it. Your CPU, your memory controller, your graphics unit would be carved into 
the same slab. But AI accelerators are…enormous. Blackwell, TPUv7 – they’re 
much better thought of as a mosaic of components.  

However, you can only make a single piece of silicon so big before 
manufacturing yields collapse. And different parts of a chip actually want to 
be built differently: your high-performance compute cores beneﬁt from 
bleeding-edge 3nm manufacturing, but your Input/Output interfaces work 
perfectly ﬁne on cheaper solutions. Putting everything on one slab forces you 
to make expensive compromises. 

The solution seems simple in theory: stop making one big chip, make several 
smaller chips (chiplets) and connect (aka package…advanced-ly) them 
together.

Their limiting factor is now connecting all the pieces together. This is where 
“advanced packaging” comes in. It’s literally what it sounds like – how do we 
pack all these chiplets together in a way where they can talk to each other 
fast enough to not defeat the purpose? 

As science-ﬁctiony as all this gets, we still have to obey the laws of space. 
There’s only so many ways you can stack things - horizontally or vertically

hyperscaler's AI workloads grow independently. Designers are hitting the 
reticle-size limit. For many high-performance chips, you simply have to split 
the design between the dies and ‘stitch’ together using advanced packaging. 
Every new ASIC program consumes advanced packaging capacity regardless 
of commercial success.

10. Earned Wage Access 

If we weren’t in the “K-Shaped Economy”, we wouldn’t feel so strongly about 
the impact of tax refunds as a short-term trade in 2026. However, the 
K-shaped dynamic shows no signs of easing up. In the long term, outside of

Most Americans don’t need more credit. They need liquidity.  

A huge percentage of American workers report living paycheck to paycheck, 
and that number is consistently growing. 

It should be a surprise to no one that payday lending now exists in the digital 
realm. What’s more, unlike the lenders of yore, the new payday lenders are 
taking even less credit risk.  

This new kind of payday loan is referred to as “Earned Wage Access”, and it’s 
essentially turning the liquidity timing gap into a very high-return, very 
short-duration, very low-risk product. By integrating into your bank and 
debiting your direct deposit, the only risk that’s really being taken is that you 
access your paycheck early and then lose your job very quickly.  

And rather than BNPL, which is unsecured short-term consumer lending, EWA 
is effectively secured short-term lending via its direct deposit integration.  

The CFPB reported that in 2022, about 10 million workers used EWA products 
to access over $31.9 billion in advances – considering the trend that’s taken 
place over the past three years (the progression of the “K-Shaped Economy”), 
we’d expect that to have grown signiﬁcantly since even if there still isn’t any 
newer oﬃcial source for overall transaction volumes.

12. Insurance Marketing 

The auto insurance industry is sitting on record proﬁts it cannot keep. 
Regulatory frameworks, competitive dynamics, and calendar-year 
compensation structures all but guarantee that excess capital gets deployed 
into two channels: lower premiums and marketing spend. This is simply the 
nature of the cycle – Progressive’s (PGR US) expense ratio is currently more 
than 2 standard deviations over its 10-year average – it has not been higher 
at any point since COVID.  

Both are rocket fuel for the digital lead-generation platforms that connect 
insurers with customers.  

A Four-Year Cycle Nobody's Watching 

To understand where we are, you need to understand how we got here. In 
2020, COVID arrived and everyone stopped driving – naturally, accident 
frequency collapsed and auto insurers posted record proﬁt margins. Flush 
with proﬁts, insurers cut premiums to grab market share in 2021-2022. This 
was concurrent with a 50% spike in used car prices, which drove up claim 
severity due to much higher replacement costs. The industry swung from 
record proﬁtability to record losses in 18 months. By mid-2023, insurers were 
desperate but were bailed out by falling car prices and economic strength 
leading to aggressive rate increases.  

November 2025 industry pricing came in at 0-1% year-over-year. That means 
2026 pricing goes negative. The hard market is over and insurers know it.

18b. Oncology’s Copy-Paste Era 

For most of the last two decades, cancer drug development looked like 
exploration: scientists searched for entirely new ideas, accepted high failure 
rates, and celebrated rare breakthroughs that changed care in a single 
disease. The most dangerous part of any new class of drugs, especially in 
oncology, is proving the target is real, the format can be tolerated and there 
is a clinically meaningful response.  

What is changing now for cancer treatment is that many of those ideas have 
survived the hardest test. We know they can work, and we know they can be 
given to patients safely. That means there’s a lot in cancer that’s moving from 
discovery to execution. Instead of asking “can this drug work at all,” the 
industry is increasingly asking “where else can this work, and how eﬃciently 
can we scale it.” 

Essentially, oncology is entering its copy-paste era. New drug platforms like 
antibody drug conjugates, engineered antibodies, cell therapies, and 
precision small molecules are no longer science experiments.  

Biology repeats across most cancers more often than you’d intuitively expect. 
Once a company proves a mechanism in one cancer, they can often reuse the 
same approach across many others with only incremental changes. The 
gains in any single trial may look modest to a casual observer, but those 
gains compound. A modest beneﬁt can turn into something much larger 
when drugs move into earlier treatment, combine with existing therapies, and 
expand across tumor types in trials that are easier to enroll and select 
patients better, turning the economics into something like a manufacturing 
process for approvals and revenue.

proven, industrialized oncology platforms (things that already work), 
platforms that are currently shifting to scale and earlier-stage mechanisms

were (and still are) the ﬁrst round of bottlenecks: GPUs, facilities, cooling, 
turbines, and memory. 2025 saw the narrative shifting to the operational 
costs, speciﬁcally power. It’s why Stargate is in Abilene, Texas for access to 
cheap natural gas. It’s why Microsoft is restarting the reactor on Three Mile 
Island. But hyperscalers are going to have a diﬃcult time controlling their 
input costs here - the last thing they have control over is the eﬃciency of their 
digital reﬁneries. 

In March 2025, Nvidia announced Spectrum-X, a network switch that has 
Co-Packaged Optics. This technology reduces heat and energy losses, which 
has a cascading effect on cooling demand and overall power needs. These 
cost savings scale as data centers utilize tens of thousands of switches, 
which places an incredible ﬂoor demand on more eﬃcient technologies. The 
Spectrum-X contains lasers made of Indium Phosphide (InP), manufactured 
by Lumentum (LITE US).

The Devil You Know 

The energy constraints of data centers forced them to co-locate near cheap, 
reliable energy sources. You can’t just pack all the servers into Northern 
Virginia. You can’t even place them in the same neighborhood.  

And that creates the next constraint, as we showcased in our “Can You Hear 
Me Now” piece. 

If you want to tie multiple data centers together for training, then you’re 
limited by distance due to light slowing down in ﬁber optic glass. By creating 
a “hollow core” in the ﬁber, you can transmit light in a near vacuum which 
allows for improved latency. It’s why this past September, Microsoft partnered 
with Corning and German ﬁrm Heraeus to manufacture hollow core ﬁber to 
connect their Azure data centers in Northern Virginia. 

The next order of scale for AI will require longer distances and higher 
bandwidth to connect the clusters. The signal and power demands will need 
ﬁber optics to be manufactured in orbit to eliminate gravity-based 
imperfections. 

Flawless Photonics is a company that has used the ISS to create ZBLAN, a 
kind of ﬂuoride glass that is well suited for long distance communications. 
Another pioneering company was Made In Space, who proved that ZBLAN

Redwire in 2020.

The Year of “Maybe” 

As we hit a fever pitch of AI supercharging voices on the internet, I see a 
worrying trend forming in markets. Not one that has to do with valuations 
(they could be high, they could also be low) or volatility, rather one that has to 
do with belief.  

More and more, in response to whatever headlines hit that day, I see people 
feel an immediate need to take a side. Google trains a capable model on 
TPUs and everyone assumes that being pro-Google means being anti-Nvidia. 
Trump pulls out a posterboard and everyone is immediately either a 
protectionist or a globalist. OpenAI’s CFO says something misguided and 
you’re either pro-AI bailouts or on a soapbox preaching about the pitfalls of 
moral hazard.  

Maybe this has been going on for a while? To me, it seems like this has 
become the de facto starting point for market participants, however. I’ve 
fallen victim to it as well. A perpetual optimist might say that AI will 
encourage people on both sides to become more informed, a realist probably 
recognizes that’s not the case.  

Less and less, I see people open to “maybe”. But maybe is the most powerful 
word any of us have. It keeps us open to the possibilities while ensuring we 
don’t fall into a trap of zealotry. It ensures you’re not the grumpiest person in 
the world every day that Palantir adds another $5 billion to its market cap, 
and will ensure you’re not Hiro Onoda after the top – still ﬁghting for the bulls 
when they’ve already lost the war.  

The US is going to invade Venezuela? Maybe we are. We’re going to build data 
centers in space? Maybe that’s true. The S&P 500 is going to melt up to 
10,000? Maybe it is. Midterm-years are historically bad for the stock market? 
Maybe that has predictive value. 

Edgar Allan Poe once said “Believe none of what you hear and half of what 
you see.” I wonder what he would have said if he saw an AI generated video 
of himself recounting the news of the day.  

It’s undeniable that AI is a transformative technology. How much of a junior 
banker’s time is spent aligning logos on a slide deck? Now, there’s a model for 
that (it’s Gemini by the way). It was equally undeniable that railroads, steam 
engines, radio, the automobile, mainframe computers and the internet were 
all transformative as well. That didn’t preclude markets from taking things too 
far, and the advancements in technology did not preclude them from 
dropping precipitously (while the technology got better). In 2026, I think the 
best thing we can do as investors, traders and/or speculators is to be more 
open to “maybe”.  

Maybe is not a dismissal. If anything, it’s the opposite. While there’s a laundry 
list of things I wish I had done differently since the market bottomed in 
October 2022, I know that the only reason I’ve more than tripled my money

more compute to do it. Now, if I was smart enough, I’d have seen ChatGPT

CLS in July 2023. And perhaps if I’d been more open to saying maybe, I would 
still have 15% of my portfolio in CLS today like I did back then.  

This isn’t some sage wisdom, I simply think it’s a good time to give a reminder 
that you can make plenty of money in markets by combining an open mind 
and curiosity.  

That cuts both ways, it’s not just about being open to new technologies, new 
businesses or positive sentiment about the future. Is inﬂation going to 
reaccelerate? Maybe. Is the US going to lose central bank independence? 
Maybe. Are stocks broadly overvalued? Maybe.  

Most of the trades we’ve explored for next year are on the long side. The 
market is trending and, after a year where our performance would have been 
~8% higher had we not bothered to hedge, it’s easy to become complacent. 
It’s also easy to get absorbed into the wall of worry and end up sidelined and 
scrambling to catch up. Vague warnings about the market going down are 
relatively useless – we all know the market is capable of going down (or, at 
least, most of us know). Instead, we should all have a contingency plan - if x 
happens, y is likely to happen, so I’ll plan on doing z. 

For any reasonable investor, the more unabashedly bullish things seem, the 
more compelling narratives about how it could all end in a ﬁery blaze of glory 
become. Instead of laying out all the ways things could go wrong, let’s say 
“maybe” and remember there’s always a few angles to every new 
development.
