Gold Market Primer

Executive Summary 

n

At the one-year anniversary of our ﬁrst Precious Analyst, we take stock of what 

we’ve learned. This primer provides an overview of the global gold market and 

the core mechanisms that drive gold prices.  

n

Gold is not consumed – it’s accumulated. Nearly every ounce ever mined still 
exists. New annual production adds just over 1% to the existing stock and is 

stable and price inelastic. Unlike oil or natural gas – where price balances 

production and consumption – gold is not consumed. It is stored. That makes 

these traditional supply-demand models inadequate for gold; its market clears 

through changes in ownership, not production-versus-use balances.  

n

You can’t pump gold – but you can bid it out of someone’s hands. Gold 
doesn’t get used – it changes hands and gets repriced. The gold price reﬂects 

who is more willing to hold it and who’s willing to let go. There are two buyer 

groups: conviction buyers – ETFs, central banks, and speculators – who buy 

irrespective of price, and opportunistic buyers – households in emerging markets 

– who step in when the price is right. Conviction buyers allocate to gold on a 

macro- or risk-hedging view, and their thesis-driven ﬂows set price direction. 

Opportunistic buyers provide a ﬂoor under prices on the way down and 

resistance on the way up.  

n

Our simple model reduces the complexity of the gold market to its key drivers. 

Conviction ﬂows – the directional force – explain 70% of monthly gold price 
moves. As a rule of thumb, 100 tonnes of net purchases by conviction 
holders – central banks, speculators, and ETFs – corresponds to a 1.7% rise 
in the gold price. Opportunistic demand plays a secondary role, helping shape 
amplitude, not trend.  

n

In forecasting gold prices, we take a view on ETF ﬂows from the US policy rate 

and on central bank purchases from ﬁnancial and geopolitical factors. By 

contrast, we treat speculative positioning as noise – a source of volatility around 

the fair value anchored by slower-moving ETFs and central banks – and assume it 

to mean-revert, consistent with the data.

I. Global Market Structure: Where Gold Trades 
Gold trades globally, but two markets dominate price formation: London and New York 

(Exhibit 1). 

London is the core of the physical market. As an over-the-counter (OTC) market, it deals 
in large 400 oz bars (~12.5 kg) – about the size of a building brick – stored in secure 

vaults across the city and typically bought by large institutions such as central banks, 

sovereign wealth funds, and bullion banks. Nearly all the gold backing 
physically-backed-ETFs worldwide is stored in these London vaults. This is where the 

institutional gold market clears.  

New York, by contrast, runs the speculative paper market. COMEX futures trade in 100 
oz units (~3 kg) – about the size of a smartphone – but are ﬁnancially settled and rarely 
see delivery. Despite their differences, the New York and London markets are tightly 
linked by the Exchange-for-Physical (EFP) (New York-London price spread). In short: buy 
spot gold in London, sell futures in New York, or vice versa. If prices diverge, this 

arbitrage trade brings them back in line. 

In normal times, the link works seamlessly. If someone does request delivery on a 

COMEX contract, you ﬂy the 400 oz bar from London to Switzerland –  the global 

reﬁning hub – remelt it into 100 oz form to meet the COMEX contract speciﬁcations, 

then send it on a commercial ﬂight to the US. The link rarely fails, but it has twice in 

recent years. 

n

In March 2020, the COVID pandemic temporarily shut Swiss reﬁneries and 

grounded most commercial air trafﬁc. With no capacity to recast bars and no 
commercial ﬂights to ship them, the price spread between New York and London 

widened sharply.  

n

A second disruption occurred in late 2024 after President Trump’s re-election. 

Markets feared the introduction of a universal tariff on all US imports – including 

gold. This created delivery risk: traders had sold COMEX futures backed by gold in 

London, but if physical delivery was requested, they would have to import bullion 

into the US and potentially pay a tariff. The risk premium pushed New York prices 

above London, widening the EFP. To avoid potential tariffs, traders pre-positioned 

gold in New York. Some even bypassed Switzerland, ﬂying the 400 oz bars directly 

from the UK to the US, with plans to reﬁne them inside the US tariff walls if needed. 

As this shows, Switzerland, as the global reﬁning hub, sits at the crossroads. When 
ETFs or central banks are buying, Swiss reﬁners melt small bars into 400 oz bars and 

ship them to London. When that ETF/central bank demand fades, bars ﬂow the other 
way: from London to Switzerland, either for private vault storage or to be recast into 

smaller units for export to New York and to retail markets in Emerging Markets such as 

India and China.

III. You Can’t Pump Gold – But You Can Bid It Out Of Someone’s Hands 
In most commodities, prices help balance production and consumption, which is why 

supply-demand models are effective tools for forecasting markets like oil. Gold is 

different. It is not consumed – it is stored. This fundamental difference makes traditional 

supply-demand models structurally inadequate for gold. Instead, the gold price reﬂects 

the struggle between those who want to hold gold and those willing to part with it – 

who can force the bar from another’s hand, and what it takes for them to let go.  

A useful analogy is the Manhattan real estate market. The total number of apartments is 

largely ﬁxed, and the small amount of new construction each year is not what drives 

prices. What matters is the identity of the marginal buyer.  

In Manhattan, there are two buyer groups: conviction buyers – those with deep pockets 

who will live there at any cost – and opportunistic buyers – those who will buy only at 

the right price and are content to live in Jersey or Brooklyn otherwise. Assuming no 

construction/demolitions, when 100 apartments are bought, 100 have also been sold; 

the question is, who took the keys? Conviction buyers set price direction: when they 

buy, prices rise and opportunists step back; when conviction buyers leave, opportunists 

step in. In other words, opportunists provide a ﬂoor under prices on the way down and 

resistance on the way up.  

Gold behaves the same way. Conviction buyers are central banks, ETFs, and speculators 
– participants who buy on a macro-or risk-hedging conviction, and at any cost. They set 

the direction of price. Opportunistic buyers are emerging markets households – 

price-sensitive participants who step in at attractive levels (but rarely sell). They help 

shape the amplitude of price moves, not the trend.

IV. Our 3 Conviction Bucket Model: Cutting Through Complexity 
Our simple model strips the gold market down to its key drivers. Conviction ﬂows – the 
directional force – explain 70% of monthly gold price moves. As a rule of thumb, 100 
tonnes of net purchases by conviction holders – central banks, speculators, and 

ETFs – corresponds to a 1.7% rise in the gold price.

Left panel: Our model uses an OLS regression of monthly gold price percentage changes on central bank, gold ETF, and speculative (net long managed money on COMEX) ﬂows. The sample covers 
January 2014 to August 2024. We estimate that 100 tonnes of gold lifts gold prices by 1.7% with a 95% conﬁdence interval of 1.5% - 2%. Right panel: The sample covers Jan 2007 to May 2025.

This follows directly from the logic in section III. The market clears when: 

conviction purchases + opportunistic purchases = sales of existing stock + mining 
supply  

In our framework, opportunistic buyers – mostly EM households – do not set price 

direction. They buy on dips and step back in rallies, cushioning moves but not driving 

them. Instead, price direction is set by conviction buyers, and the key is to measure 

their demand against the gold actually available to buy. That supply comes from two 

sources: sales of existing stock and new mine output. 

Most existing-stock sales come from the conviction crowd itself – ETFs, central banks, 

and speculators selling to other conviction buyers. Emerging-market households rarely 

sell on net, and export restrictions in EM countries keep that gold from reaching 

international markets. That means price moves are driven by one ratio:  

net conviction purchases/mining supply.

V. Why High Prices Do Not Cure High Prices 
In commodities, the saying goes “high prices cure high prices”, meaning that higher 

prices reduce demand and bring more supply, which bring prices down. Gold breaks this 

rule. Its available supply – sales of existing stock plus new mine output – is barely 

affected by price. Mining output is limited, stable, and price inelastic. Most 

existing-stock sales come from other conviction holders, not from EM households, who 

rarely sell and whose gold is often kept off international markets by export restrictions.  

When conviction buyers are net buyers, prices can run far. The 1970s show how far. In 

1971, President Nixon ended Dollar convertibility to gold – then $42/toz – shifting the 

Dollar system from collateral-based to credibility-based, dependent on the Fed’s ability 

to preserve purchasing power and remain independent of political pressures. Over the 
decade, inﬂation surged, real rates fell in deeply negative territory, and gold prices rose 
ﬁvefold as the market sought a store of value it could trust.  

In 1979, a second shock hit. The Iranian Revolution and the US freeze of Iran’s central 

bank reserves raised doubts about the neutrality of Dollar-based reserves. That erosion 
of both monetary and geopolitical neutrality drove gold to $850/toz in January 1980 – 

twenty times its price less than a decade earlier.  

Only when Volcker took over at the Fed, raised interest rates sharply, and reasserted 

price stability did gold prices correct by more than 20%.  

In gold, high prices end only when conviction breaks. That raises the question: what 

drives our three conviction holders – and how can we forecast their net purchases?  

VI. Forecasting Gold Prices = Forecasting Conviction Holdings 
Our conviction holders are three: ETFs, central banks, and speculators. They allocate to 

gold on a macro or risk-hedging view, with the balance sheet to act decisively. Their 

ﬂows are thesis-driven rather than price-driven.  

While they share similar estimated price coefﬁcients in our empirical model – allowing 

us to treat them as one group – their motives differ, so ﬂows can diverge across groups. 

After 2022, for example, central banks saw record inﬂows even as ETFs recorded 
outﬂows.1

In these periods of low conﬁdence in the reserve system, gold serves as a neutral 

collateral that does not rely on the issuer’s credibility. And when a country wants to 

establish itself as the new dominant currency – whether it ultimately succeeds or not – 

gold accumulation is typically part of the strategy. Holding gold signals that the currency

Speculators  
Speculative positioning in gold is “fast money” – it builds quickly and unwinds just as 

fast. We see it as noise around the more fundamental price anchors set by 

slower-moving ETF and central bank ﬂows.  

Statistically, speculative positioning (net managed money on Comex; Bloomberg: 

CFCDUMMN) oscillates around its long-run average and tends to mean-revert after 

surges or drawdowns (Exhibit 15). This is why we treat extremes in positioning as 

short-term volatility risk rather than a lasting shift in fair value.  

Three patterns stand out:  

n

Event run-ups: Positioning often rises ahead of major events with wide-outcome 
uncertainty – a “waiting room” allocation to gold until clarity emerges. This was 

evident before the 2016 Brexit referendum, or the 2024 US election. Once 

outcomes are known, positioning often falls and gold can sell off as capital is 

redeployed.  

n

n

Headline sensitivity: Because it is fast money, positioning reacts quickly to news 
that shifts perceived uncertainty.  

Margin call-driven liquidation: In sharp equity drawdowns, gold’s liquidity makes it 
a source of funds for margin calls. This can cause an initial drop in price and 
positioning before rebounding if uncertainty persists – as in the GFC (2008), March 
2020 (Covid), and April 2025 (Liberation Day).

Other assets tend to hedge ‘normal’ inﬂation more effectively, but cycle dynamics

VIII. Local Market Dynamics 
China and India dominate EM retail gold demand. In our framework, EM buyers are 
“opportunistic” – they provide a ﬂoor when conviction buyers step back but do not set 

the global price direction. Imports are typically regulated to limit gold’s drag on the 

current account and, by extension, pressure on the currency, while various export 

restrictions keep most bullion in the domestic market. These controls, combined with 

local supply-demand dynamics, create price disconnects from the London benchmark, 

with premiums or discounts emerging in each market.  

The Centralized Chinese Retail Market  
China’s demand is dominated by physical gold ownership, rooted in strong tradition and 

supported by easy retail access. Private-sector transactions are routed almost entirely 
through the Shanghai Gold Exchange (SGE) – a closed and centralized market – due 
to high liquidity, strict regulation, and tax incentives. Withdrawals from the SGE are the 

best proxy for private physical demand (Haver Analytics ticker: sgauwvp@cmdty). The 

ofﬁcial sector is the exception, likely sourcing directly from international markets for 

liquidity and foreign-currency settlement – unlike the SGE’s Renminbi-only contracts. 

Gold traded on the SGE is sourced from (1) non-monetary imports (in ordinary trade) – 
the largest source4 ; (2) domestic mine production – ~ 400 tonnes annually, the largest 
in the world; and (3) scrap – variable.  

All gold for the private sector must meet SGE quality standards and be processed 

through approved reﬁneries. Once inside the domestic market, gold cannot be exported.

Shanghai-London Premium/Discount  

Because the Chinese gold market is closed – imports and exports are restricted – 

domestic imbalances show up as a Shanghai-London premium or discount (Bloomberg 

ticker: .CHGOLD G). A premium occurs when domestic demand runs ahead of import 

quotas. The local price rises until demand slows or domestic scrap supply increases. 

With a strict export ban, the reverse imbalance – excess supply – creates a discount 

until it is absorbed by local buying.  

Importantly, a Shanghai-London premium does not translate into sustained moves in the 
global price per se because:  

1.

2.

EM buyers do not set global price direction.  

A premium simply means domestic demand has hit the import quota and, by 

deﬁnition, is trapped in the local market. That containment is exactly why the 

premium exists in the ﬁrst place.  

Import quotas are reviewed quarterly and remain sensitive to broader macro 

considerations, including CNY depreciation risk as gold imports put additional pressure 

on the current account. In H2 2023, tighter import quotas pushed the Shanghai 

premium above $120/toz on 14 September.

rural incomes rise and households often convert cash into gold.  

Indian demand is typically low from May through August, after the Akshaya Tritiya peak 

and during the monsoon months when weddings pause and rural incomes are tied up in 

planting, as well as December-January between the two wedding seasons.  

India’s Sovereign Gold Bonds Programme 

India’s Sovereign Gold Bond (SGB) programme was launched in 2015 to tackle a persistent macro 

vulnerability: high physical gold imports widening the current account deﬁcit and weakening the rupee. 

Gold is India’s second-largest import after crude oil, but unlike oil, it is non-productive – once purchased, it 

sits idle in household vaults, often for decades. The SGB was designed as a ﬁnancial substitute for physical 
gold: bonds denominated in grams, issued by the RBI, and have an eight-year maturity with early 

redemption allowed after ﬁve years. Holders earn a 2.75% (later reduced to 2.5%) annual interest and 

receive the prevailing Indian gold price, paid in rupees, at maturity or redemption. Capital gains at maturity 

are exempt from tax, and there is no Goods and Services Tax (GST) or making charges. By capturing 
household gold demand in a paper instrument, the scheme aimed to lower dollar outﬂows, stabilize the 

currency, and mobilize savings into government funding.  

In theory, this offered investors a cheaper, more efﬁcient gold exposure than buying coins or jewelry. In 

practice, uptake proved limited outside of urban investors. Rural households – who hold the bulk of India’s 

gold – continued to buy jewelry for weddings, festivals, and as a visible store of wealth, not for interest 

income. Meanwhile, the SGB created a different kind of liability. Gold prices have more than doubled since 

many early tranches were issued, meaning the government must redeem these bonds at much higher 

prices while having paid annual interest throughout. New issuance was discontinued after February 2024, 

but tranche redemptions will continue until 2032. Outstanding SGBs can be tracked on the RBI website 

here.
